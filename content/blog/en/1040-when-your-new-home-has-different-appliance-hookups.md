# When Your New Home Has Different Appliance Hookups

You signed the lease or closed on the house. You measured the kitchen and laundry room. Everything fits. Then you show up on moving day and discover that your gas range has nowhere to connect because the new kitchen is all-electric. Or your dryer needs a 4-prong outlet but the house has a 3-prong. Or the washer hookups are on the wrong wall. Hookup mismatches are one of the most common surprises in Miami moves, especially when people are switching between older homes and newer condos or moving between different types of buildings.

## Common Hookup Mismatches in Miami

### Gas vs. Electric Range

![A Cozy Laundry Closet With A Dryer And V 2](/images/blog/2028/04/when-your-new-home-has-different-appliance-hookups/a-cozy-laundry-closet-with-a-dryer-and-v-2.webp)


Older Miami homes in neighborhoods like Coral Gables, South Miami, and parts of Kendall often have natural gas lines running to the kitchen. Newer condos and apartments, especially high-rises in Brickell, Downtown, and Edgewater, are typically all-electric. If your gas range won't work at the new place, your options are:

- **Buy an electric range.** This is usually the simplest and most cost-effective solution. Mid-range electric ranges start around $500.
- **Convert your gas range.** Some gas ranges can be converted to electric by swapping the power source and installing electric heating elements, but this only works on dual-fuel models. Most standard gas ranges can't be converted.
- **Install a gas line.** In a single-family home, you can have a gas line run to the kitchen. This costs $500-$2,000 depending on the distance from the existing gas main. In a condo, this is almost never an option since buildings don't allow individual gas line installations.

### Dryer Outlet Type

Electric dryers use 240-volt outlets, but there are two types:
- **3-prong (NEMA 10-30)**: Common in homes built before 1996
- **4-prong (NEMA 14-30)**: Required by code in homes built after 1996

If your dryer has a 4-prong plug and the new home has a 3-prong outlet (or vice versa), you can either change the dryer cord to match the outlet or have an electrician update the outlet. Changing the dryer cord costs about $20 for the cord and takes 15 minutes. Changing the outlet costs $100-$200 for an electrician.

### Gas vs. Electric Dryer

Similar to the range situation, your gas dryer won't work if the new place only has an electric hookup, and vice versa. Dryer conversion kits don't exist the way they do for some ranges. If the hookup type doesn't match, you'll need a new dryer. Used dryers in good condition sell for $200-$400 on Facebook Marketplace in Miami, so the switch doesn't have to break the bank.

### Washer Water Supply Location

In some Miami homes, the washer hookups are in the garage. In others, they're in a second-floor laundry room. In condos, they're typically in a closet. If the hookup location is different from what you expected, you may need longer hoses or a different washer configuration. Standard washer hoses are 4 feet long. If the valves are farther away, you can buy 6-foot or 8-foot hoses.

### Dryer Vent Location

This catches people off guard. Your old home might have had a wall vent behind the dryer. The new place might vent through the floor or ceiling, or it might not have an external vent at all (common in interior laundry closets in Miami high-rises). If there's no external vent, you'll need a ventless dryer or a condensation dryer. These units don't vent hot air outside; instead, they condense the moisture internally and drain it. They're standard in most Miami condo buildings built in the last 20 years.

### 120V vs. 240V Oven Outlets

Most American ovens and ranges use 240V. But some apartment kitchens in older Miami buildings were wired for European-style 120V compact ovens. If the outlet doesn't match your range, an electrician can run a dedicated 240V circuit, though this can cost $200-$500 depending on the distance from the electrical panel.

## How to Avoid Hookup Surprises

### Check Before You Sign

![Outdoor View Of Two Stacked Laundry Mach 1](/images/blog/2028/04/when-your-new-home-has-different-appliance-hookups/outdoor-view-of-two-stacked-laundry-mach-1.webp)


During your walkthrough of the new home, take photos of every appliance hookup: outlets, gas valves, water connections, dryer vents. Note the outlet types (count the prongs, check the voltage rating printed on the outlet cover). Look behind where the washer and dryer will go for the water valves and vent port.

### Ask the Landlord or Seller

If you're renting, ask the property manager exactly what appliance connections are available. If you're buying, your home inspector should note all utility connections, but verify the details yourself.

### Bring a Checklist

For each appliance you're moving, note what it needs:
- **Range**: Gas line or 240V electric outlet (3-prong or 4-prong?)
- **Dryer**: Gas line or 240V electric outlet (3-prong or 4-prong?), vent port or ventless setup
- **Washer**: Hot and cold water valves, drain standpipe, 120V outlet
- **Dishwasher**: Hot water supply under sink, drain connection, 120V outlet
- **Refrigerator**: 120V outlet, water supply for ice maker (optional)
