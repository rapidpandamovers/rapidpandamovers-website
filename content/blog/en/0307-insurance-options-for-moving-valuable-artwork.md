# Insurance Options for Moving Valuable Artwork

That painting hanging above your mantel in Coral Gables might be worth more than your car. If it gets damaged during a move, standard moving company liability won't come close to covering the loss. Understanding your insurance options before moving day protects your collection and your wallet.

## What Standard Moving Coverage Actually Covers

Federal law requires interstate movers to offer two levels of liability coverage. The basic option, released value protection, covers your belongings at just 60 cents per pound. A 10-pound framed painting worth $15,000 would be covered for exactly six dollars. That's not a typo.

![Art Gallery With Classical Statues And P 3](/images/blog/2025/08/insurance-options-for-moving-valuable-artwork/art-gallery-with-classical-statues-and-p-3.webp)


The second option, full value protection, means the mover must repair, replace, or pay the current market value for damaged items. This sounds better, but there's a catch. Most policies cap coverage at a declared value, and high-value items like artwork often need separate declarations with higher premiums. Always read the fine print.

## Third-Party Transit Insurance

For valuable collections, third-party transit insurance from a specialty provider offers the most thorough coverage. Companies that specialize in fine art insurance understand the market and offer policies tailored to artwork in transit. These policies typically cover:

- Damage from impact, dropping, or mishandling
- Water damage (a real concern during Miami's rainy season)
- Fire and smoke damage
- Theft during transport or storage
- Damage from temperature and humidity fluctuations

Premiums are usually calculated as a percentage of the declared value, typically between 1% and 3% for domestic moves. A collection valued at $100,000 might cost $1,000 to $3,000 to insure during transit. Compared to the cost of restoration or loss, that's a reasonable investment.

## Homeowner's Insurance and Art Riders

Check your homeowner's policy before the move. Some policies cover personal property during a move, but most have limits on individual items and exclusions for fine art. If you have a fine art rider or floater on your homeowner's policy, verify whether it extends to transit coverage or only covers items while they're in your home.

Many Miami collectors with waterfront properties already carry art riders because of hurricane risk. If that's you, call your agent and ask specifically about coverage during a physical relocation.

## Getting a Pre-Move Appraisal

Insurance claims require documentation of value. If your artwork hasn't been appraised recently, get an updated appraisal before the move. Art values change over time, and a five-year-old appraisal may not reflect current market prices. Miami has several certified art appraisers who work with collectors regularly.

![Woman Stands In A Modern London Art Gall 1](/images/blog/2025/08/insurance-options-for-moving-valuable-artwork/woman-stands-in-a-modern-london-art-gall-1.webp)


Keep the appraisal documents, purchase receipts, and provenance records in a safe place separate from the artwork itself. Digital copies stored in the cloud work well as backup.

## Documenting Condition Before the Move

Photos are your strongest evidence if something goes wrong. Photograph each piece from the front, back, and all edges. Capture close-ups of the surface, frame, and any pre-existing condition issues. Take these photos in good lighting with a high-resolution camera or phone. Include a ruler or reference object for scale.

Record the date of the photos and store them alongside your appraisals and inventory list.
