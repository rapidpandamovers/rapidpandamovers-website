Moving crews see the same packing mistakes on nearly every job. Some cause minor inconvenience. Others result in shattered glassware, scratched furniture, and damaged electronics that cost hundreds to replace.

Here are 8 packing fails that cause the most damage and how to avoid each one.

## 1. Stacking Plates Flat Instead of on Edge

Plates stacked horizontally crack under their own weight. The plate at the bottom bears the full load, and any bump during transport concentrates force on a single point.

![A Man Organizing Items Into Cardboard Bo 4](/images/blog/2029/11/8-packing-fails-that-damage-your-belongings/a-man-organizing-items-into-cardboard-bo-4.webp)


**The fix:** Pack plates vertically, like records in a crate. Wrap each plate individually in packing paper, then stand them on edge in a small or medium box with crumpled paper filling every gap. A dish cell box (with cardboard dividers) works even better.

## 2. Skipping Labels on Boxes

Unlabeled boxes cause two types of damage. First, movers cannot identify fragile contents, so they stack and handle unlabeled boxes the same way they handle a box of towels. Second, homeowners dig through boxes looking for specific items, tossing things aside and breaking contents in the process.

**The fix:** Write the destination room and a brief contents description on at least two sides of every box. Mark fragile boxes with bright tape or a large "FRAGILE" label. It takes 10 seconds per box and prevents the most avoidable damage.

## 3. Wrapping Items with Newspaper Ink-Side In

Newspaper works as padding, but the ink transfers to dishes, fabric, and porous surfaces. White porcelain, crystal, and light-colored linens are especially vulnerable. The ink stains can be permanent on unglazed ceramics.

**The fix:** Use clean packing paper (available in bulk at any moving supply store) instead of newspaper. If you use newspaper for cushioning between items, wrap the item in clean paper first so ink never contacts the surface.

## 4. Leaving Empty Space Inside Boxes

A half-filled box lets items shift during transport. When the truck brakes, accelerates, or turns, unsecured items collide with each other and the box walls. This is how drinking glasses chip, picture frames crack, and small appliance housings break.

**The fix:** Fill every void with crumpled packing paper, bubble wrap, towels, or clothing. The test is simple: close the box and give it a gentle shake. If anything moves, add more fill. The box should feel firm and solid with no rattling.

## 5. Forgetting to Secure Liquid Containers

Shampoo, cooking oil, cleaning products, and paint all leak during moves. In South Florida, heat buildup inside a moving truck can increase pressure inside sealed bottles, forcing lids open. A single leaking bottle can ruin an entire box of clothing or linens.

**The fix:** Remove caps and cover the openings with plastic wrap, then replace the caps over the wrap. Place each container in a sealed ziplock bag. Pack all liquids upright in a single, clearly labeled box lined with a garbage bag as a secondary containment layer.

## 6. Packing Electronics Without Removing Batteries

Batteries left inside devices during a move can leak acid if they overheat in the truck. Leaked battery acid corrodes internal components and often destroys the device entirely. Remote controls, wireless keyboards, flashlights, and children's toys are the most common culprits.

**The fix:** Remove batteries from every device before packing. Place the batteries in a separate labeled bag and tape it to the outside of the electronics box. For rechargeable devices, power them down completely (not just sleep mode) before packing.

## 7. Using Garbage Bags for Clothing

Garbage bags seem convenient for soft items, but they create problems. Bags do not protect clothing from crushing, they trap moisture (which causes mildew in Florida's humidity), and they offer no structure. A bag of clothes at the bottom of a stack gets compressed, wrinkling everything inside and potentially popping seams on structured garments.

![Person Packing Glassware Into Boxes With 5](/images/blog/2029/11/8-packing-fails-that-damage-your-belongings/person-packing-glassware-into-boxes-with-5.webp)


**The fix:** Use wardrobe boxes for hanging clothes. They include a metal bar at the top so clothes hang exactly as they do in your closet. For folded items, use medium-sized cardboard boxes. If you must use bags, choose clear bags so contents are visible and never stack anything on top of them.

## 8. Taping Only the Center Seam of Box Bottoms

A single strip of tape down the center seam holds for lightweight items. But boxes with books, kitchen equipment, or tools will split open when lifted from below. The tape fails at the edges where stress concentrates, and the contents drop straight through the bottom.

**The fix:** Apply the H-taping method: one strip down the center seam, then one strip across each end perpendicular to the seam. For heavy boxes, add two diagonal strips across the bottom as well. Use quality packing tape, not masking tape or duct tape, both of which lose adhesion over time and in heat.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What packing materials do I actually need?**
At minimum: sturdy cardboard boxes in multiple sizes, packing paper, bubble wrap, packing tape (not masking or duct tape), and a marker for labeling. Wardrobe boxes and dish cell boxes are worth the investment for clothing and kitchen items.

**Should I keep the original boxes for electronics?**
Yes. Original packaging was engineered for the exact dimensions of the device, including custom foam inserts. If you no longer have the original box, wrap screens with cardboard and use bubble wrap generously.

**How do I know if a box is too heavy?**
If you cannot comfortably lift it to waist height and carry it 20 feet, it is too heavy. Redistribute the contents into smaller boxes. As a guideline, keep boxes under 50 pounds.
