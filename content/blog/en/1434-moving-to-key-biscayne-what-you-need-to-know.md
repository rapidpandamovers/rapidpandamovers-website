Key Biscayne is a barrier island community of about 14,000 residents connected to mainland Miami by the Rickenbacker Causeway. It sits between Biscayne Bay and the Atlantic Ocean, bookended by two state parks, and offers a small-town pace of life that feels far removed from the mainland despite being just 15 minutes from Brickell and Downtown Miami.

If you are considering a move here, this guide covers the layout, logistics, and practical details you should know before crossing the causeway.

## What Makes Key Biscayne Different

Key Biscayne is one of the few places in Miami-Dade County where people get around by golf cart, bicycle, and on foot. Crandon Boulevard runs the length of the island and hosts most of the commercial activity: grocery stores, boutiques, restaurants, and services. The island has a walkable village center, which is rare in a county built around car culture.

![Vertical View Of Cape Florida Lighthouse 3](/images/blog/2029/10/moving-to-key-biscayne-what-you-need-to-know/vertical-view-of-cape-florida-lighthouse-3.webp)


The community is bordered by Crandon Park to the north and Bill Baggs Cape Florida State Park to the south. Together, these two parks cover more acreage than the residential area itself, giving residents access to beaches, nature trails, a championship golf course, a tennis center, and the historic Cape Florida Lighthouse.

The tradeoff is access. Everything on Key Biscayne depends on the Rickenbacker Causeway, a single bridge connecting the island to the mainland via Virginia Key. Rush hour traffic on the causeway is a daily reality, and any road incident can turn a 15-minute commute into an hour.

## Neighborhoods and Housing

### North End: Condos and Resort Living

The northern end of Key Biscayne features high-rise and mid-rise condo buildings with resort-style amenities: pools, fitness centers, concierge services, and ocean or bay views. Buildings like the Ocean Club, the Grand Bay, and Oceana offer luxury condo living. This area draws both full-time residents and seasonal owners.

### South End: Single-Family Homes

The southern section of the island is predominantly single-family homes on quiet, tree-lined streets. Properties range from modest ranch-style houses built in the 1960s and 1970s to newly constructed modern homes. Many families with children choose the south end for the neighborhood feel and proximity to local schools.

### Village Center

The commercial and civic heart of Key Biscayne sits along Crandon Boulevard between Harbor Drive and Fernwood Road. Village Hall, the community center, restaurants, banks, and shops are all within walking distance. Properties near the village center tend to be smaller homes and low-rise condos.

## Local Attractions and Parks

- **Crandon Park**: An 800-acre park on the north end with a two-mile beach consistently ranked among the best in the country, an 18-hole championship golf course, a tennis center that hosts professional tournaments, and a nature center
- **Bill Baggs Cape Florida State Park**: Covers the southern tip of the island with hiking trails, fishing piers, and the Cape Florida Lighthouse, originally built in 1825 and rebuilt in 1846. The lighthouse is the oldest standing structure in Miami-Dade County
- **Key Biscayne Community Center**: Hosts youth and adult sports leagues, fitness classes, and community events year-round
- **Crandon Boulevard shops and restaurants**: The island's main commercial strip with a Winn-Dixie grocery store, specialty shops, and a range of dining from casual to upscale

## Practical Tips for New Residents

### Getting Around

![Young Woman In Summer Wear Lounging By P 1](/images/blog/2029/10/moving-to-key-biscayne-what-you-need-to-know/young-woman-in-summer-wear-lounging-by-p-1.webp)


The Rickenbacker Causeway is your lifeline to the mainland. The toll (currently $1.75 for SunPass users) adds up for daily commuters. Many residents keep the causeway commute in mind when choosing work locations -- some specifically seek remote work or jobs in Brickell and Coral Gables to minimize drive time.

On the island itself, bicycles and golf carts are common for errands and school runs. The flat terrain and low speed limits make cycling practical year-round.

### Schools

Key Biscayne has its own elementary school (Key Biscayne K-8 Center) that serves students through eighth grade. For high school, students are typically zoned for Coral Gables Senior High on the mainland. Several private school options exist on the mainland in Coral Gables and Coconut Grove.

### Shopping and Services

The island has basic retail and grocery coverage along Crandon Boulevard, including a Winn-Dixie and CVS. For major shopping, residents head to Coral Gables, Brickell, or Dadeland on the mainland. Most Key Biscayne residents make one or two mainland runs per week for errands that the island doesn't cover.

### Hurricane Preparedness

Key Biscayne sits in a mandatory evacuation zone for any hurricane threat. The island evacuates early because the Rickenbacker Causeway closes when sustained winds reach tropical storm force. If you move to Key Biscayne, have an evacuation plan, know where you will go on the mainland, and be prepared to leave 24 to 48 hours before a storm's projected arrival. Flood insurance is required for most properties.

### Condo Move-In Requirements

Most condo buildings on Key Biscayne have strict move-in procedures: elevator reservations (often limited to specific days and hours), loading dock scheduling, security deposits, and insurance certificates from your moving company. Contact your building's management office at least two weeks before your move to get the requirements and reserve your time slot.

## FAQ

**How much does it cost to live on Key Biscayne?**
Key Biscayne is one of the most expensive communities in Miami-Dade County. Home prices and condo prices are well above the county average. Property taxes, HOA fees for condo owners, and the causeway toll add to the cost of living.

**How far is Key Biscayne from the airport?**
Miami International Airport is about 20 minutes from Key Biscayne via the Rickenbacker Causeway and I-95, depending on traffic.

**Is Key Biscayne safe?**
Key Biscayne consistently ranks among the safest communities in Miami-Dade County. The island's single access point and its own police department contribute to low crime rates.

**Can I live on Key Biscayne without a car?**
For daily island life, yes. Many residents handle errands, school, and recreation on foot or by bike. But you will need a car (or reliable rideshare) for anything on the mainland, and the causeway is not bike-friendly for regular commuting.
