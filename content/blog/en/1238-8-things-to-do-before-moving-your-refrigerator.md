# 8 Things to Do Before Moving Your Refrigerator

Your refrigerator is probably the most expensive single appliance in your home, and one of the heaviest. Moving it without proper preparation leads to compressor failure, water damage, broken door seals, and gouged floors. Before your next move across Miami, work through these eight steps to make sure your fridge arrives at the new place in working condition.

## 1. Use Up Your Food (1 Week Before)

Start eating down the contents of your fridge and freezer a full week before the move. Plan meals around what's already inside. Frozen meats, ice cream, and anything that needs constant refrigeration should be consumed or donated. On the day before the move, you should be down to condiments and a few basics. Toss anything expired. This is also a great time to donate unopened canned goods and dry goods to Feeding South Florida or a local food bank in Miami-Dade.

## 2. Unplug and Defrost (24-48 Hours Before)

Unplug the refrigerator and open both the fridge and freezer doors wide. Place old towels around the base and inside the bottom of the freezer compartment to absorb meltwater. In Miami's warm, humid climate, defrosting generates more water than you'd see in cooler, drier regions because ambient moisture condenses on the cold interior surfaces. Check the towels every few hours and swap them out when they're soaked. If there's heavy ice buildup in the freezer, place a pot of hot water inside and close the door for 20 minutes to speed up the process. Don't chip at the ice with a knife or screwdriver. You'll puncture the freezer lining or damage the evaporator coils.

## 3. Clean the Interior

Once fully defrosted, wipe down every surface inside the fridge and freezer with a solution of baking soda and warm water (two tablespoons per quart). This removes odors and residue. Pay special attention to the rubber door gaskets where mold likes to hide, especially in South Florida's humidity. Dry everything thoroughly with clean towels. A damp fridge sealed up during transport becomes a mold problem fast.

## 4. Remove and Pack All Loose Parts

Take out shelves, drawers, bins, crisper covers, and any removable dividers. Wrap each piece individually in packing paper or moving blankets. Glass shelves need extra padding because they crack easily during transport if they're vibrating against each other. Pack them in a box with "FRAGILE" labeling. If you leave these parts inside the fridge during the move, they slide around, crash into each other, and break.

## 5. Disconnect the Water Line

If your refrigerator has an ice maker or water dispenser, there's a water supply line running to it. Find the shutoff valve, which is usually on the wall behind the fridge or under the kitchen sink. Turn it clockwise to close it. Disconnect the water line from the back of the fridge. Have a bucket and towels ready because water will drain from the line. Coil the disconnected water line and tape it to the back of the fridge so it doesn't dangle.

## 6. Secure the Doors and Power Cord

Close the fridge and freezer doors and secure them with ratchet straps wrapped around the body of the fridge, or with appliance-grade tape. Don't use duct tape on stainless steel or painted surfaces because the adhesive leaves marks, especially in the heat of a Miami summer when it gets soft and gooey. Coil the power cord and tape it to the back of the unit so it doesn't catch on doorways or get rolled over by the dolly.

## 7. Measure Your Path

Grab a tape measure and check the width of every doorway, hallway, and passage between the fridge and the front door. Then do the same at the new home. Standard Miami refrigerators are 30-36 inches wide. A French-door model with handles can be 38-40 inches. Interior doorways in older Miami homes, particularly in areas like Little Havana, Coconut Grove, and Shenandoah, can be as narrow as 28-30 inches. If the fridge won't fit, you'll need to remove the fridge doors. Most refrigerator doors come off by popping the hinge pins at the top, gaining you 2-4 inches of clearance.

## 8. Plan for the Wait Time After Moving

At the new home, your fridge needs to sit upright and unplugged for a minimum of 2 hours before you turn it on. If the fridge was tilted more than 45 degrees at any point during the move (getting it onto the dolly, through a doorway, into the truck), extend that wait to 4-6 hours. This settling time lets the compressor oil, which may have shifted during transport, flow back to where it belongs. Plugging in too early forces the compressor to run without proper lubrication. Once plugged in, the fridge needs another 24 hours to reach full operating temperature before you load it with food.
