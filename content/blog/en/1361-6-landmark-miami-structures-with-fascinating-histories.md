Miami's buildings have seen a lot. Hurricanes, housing booms, refugee crises, and more than a few reinventions. Whether you have lived here for decades or just moved to South Florida, these six landmark structures are worth knowing about. Each one tells a story about how Miami became the city it is today.

## 1. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens

Industrialist James Deering spent a fortune building this Italian Renaissance-style estate on the Coconut Grove waterfront, and by December 1916, he was hosting his first Christmas party in it. The villa has 34 decorated rooms filled with European antiques and art, surrounded by 10 acres of formal gardens designed to look like they belong on the Italian coast.

![Beautiful Facade Of A Historic Mansion W 3](/images/blog/2029/06/6-landmark-miami-structures-with-fascinating-histories/beautiful-facade-of-a-historic-mansion-w-3.webp)


Deering employed roughly 1,000 workers during construction, which was about 10% of Miami's population at the time. Today Vizcaya is a National Historic Landmark and one of Miami-Dade County's most visited cultural sites. It sits at 3251 South Miami Avenue, just south of the Rickenbacker Causeway.

## 2. The Freedom Tower

Originally built in 1925 as the headquarters of The Miami News, this 17-story Mediterranean Revival tower was modeled after the Giralda in Seville, Spain. For a time, it was the tallest building in the South, and ships used it to find their way into port.

The building earned its current name in 1962 when it became the Cuban Refugee Assistance Center. From 1962 to 1974, hundreds of thousands of Cuban refugees were processed here, receiving medical exams, paperwork, and assistance starting new lives. Locals called it "El Refugio." Today the Freedom Tower houses Miami Dade College's Museum of Art and Design, and it remains one of Miami's most recognizable landmarks at 600 Biscayne Boulevard.

## 3. The Biltmore Hotel

When the Biltmore opened in Coral Gables in January 1926, guests arrived on special trains from the north. The 315-foot tower, also inspired by the Giralda, made it the tallest building in Florida at the time. The guest list read like a who's who of early 20th-century America: the Duke and Duchess of Windsor, Ginger Rogers, Bing Crosby, and Al Capone all passed through.

The Biltmore survived the 1926 Miami Hurricane just months after opening, sheltering 2,000 people left homeless by the storm. During World War II, the War Department converted it into a military hospital. It served as a VA hospital until 1968, then sat empty for nearly two decades before a $55 million restoration returned it to its original grandeur. The pool, once the largest in the world, still draws visitors today.

## 4. Art Deco Historic District

South Beach's Art Deco Historic District is the largest collection of Art Deco architecture in the world, with more than 800 buildings dating from the 1920s through the 1940s. The pastel-painted facades, porthole windows, curved corners, and neon signs are now iconic, but they nearly disappeared.

![Explore The Neoclassical Colonnades Of V 1](/images/blog/2029/06/6-landmark-miami-structures-with-fascinating-histories/explore-the-neoclassical-colonnades-of-v-1.webp)


By the 1970s, many of these buildings were slated for demolition to make way for high-rises. Activist Barbara Baer Capitman led the campaign that secured the district's listing on the National Register of Historic Places in 1979, saving the buildings that now define Miami Beach's identity. A walk along Ocean Drive or Collins Avenue is a lesson in what happens when a community fights to preserve its history.

## 5. Coral Gables City Hall

Built in 1928, this Mediterranean Revival building was designed by architects Phineas Paist and Harold Steward with artistic direction from Denman Fink. Its walls are constructed from Florida keystone, an oolitic limestone embedded with fossilized coral, giving it a texture you will not find in any modern building.

The Corinthian colonnade ringing the council chamber reflects George Merrick's vision for Coral Gables as a planned Mediterranean-inspired city. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places since 1974, the building still serves as Coral Gables' city hall, making it one of the longest continuously operating government buildings in South Florida.

## 6. Miami-Dade County Courthouse

Completed in 1928, this 28-story, 361-foot neoclassical tower at 73 West Flagler Street was the tallest building in Florida and the tallest south of the Mason-Dixon line when it was built. Atlanta architect A. Ten Eyck Brown and Miami architect August Geiger designed it with Stone Mountain granite on the lower floors and terra-cotta panels glazed to resemble granite on the upper stories.

The stepped pyramid cap at the top is thought to reference the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The courthouse has served Miami-Dade County continuously since its dedication in September 1928 and is listed on the National Register of Historic Places.
