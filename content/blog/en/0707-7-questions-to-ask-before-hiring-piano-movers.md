# 7 Questions to Ask Before Hiring Piano Movers

Not every company that says they move pianos actually specializes in it. Some general movers add "piano moving" to their website to win more business, then send a crew that's never handled anything heavier than a sectional sofa. Asking the right questions before you book separates the professionals from the pretenders. Here are seven questions that reveal what you're really getting.

## 1. How Many Pianos Do You Move Per Month?

A company that moves two pianos a year is not a piano moving company. A crew that handles pianos weekly has the muscle memory, the communication patterns, and the problem-solving instincts that only come from repetition.

![A Close Up Of Grand Piano Keys Bathed In 2](/images/blog/2027/02/7-questions-to-ask-before-hiring-piano-movers/a-close-up-of-grand-piano-keys-bathed-in-2.webp)


Look for a team that moves at least 10 to 15 pianos per month. At that volume, the crew has encountered tight staircases, difficult building management, heavy rain on loading day, and every other curveball that Miami throws at piano movers. They know what to do when things don't go as planned.

## 2. What Equipment Do You Carry for Piano Moves?

This question exposes generalists quickly. A real [**Piano Moving**](/piano-moving) operation will mention piano boards, heavy-duty piano dollies, stair-climbing equipment, padded ratchet straps, quilted blankets, and floor protection. If the answer is "a dolly and some blankets," that's a furniture moving crew, not a piano moving crew.

For grand pianos specifically, ask about piano boards and leg removal tools. If they don't carry these, they can't safely move a grand piano. Full stop.

## 3. What Insurance Do You Carry?

Ask for specifics: cargo insurance limits, general liability limits, and workers' compensation coverage. Then ask to see proof. A reputable company will provide a certificate of insurance without hesitation.

The key number is the cargo insurance limit per item. If your piano is worth $15,000, you need a policy that covers at least that amount. Basic released value protection ($0.60 per pound) would only pay $360 for a damaged 600-pound baby grand. That's not protection; it's a technicality.

For Miami high-rise moves, ask if they can provide a COI listing your building as an additional insured. If they don't know what a COI is, keep looking.

## 4. Can You Handle Stairs and Tight Access?

Miami is full of access challenges: narrow Art Deco building staircases on South Beach, spiral staircases in Coral Gables homes, tight freight elevators in Brickell towers. The mover needs to assess these conditions before quoting the job.

Ask if they do pre-move site assessments, either in person or via photos and measurements. A company that quotes a price without asking about stairs, doorway widths, and building access is guessing, and guesses often turn into surprise charges on moving day.

## 5. How Do You Protect Against Miami's Heat and Humidity?

Any piano mover working in South Florida should have a clear answer to this. You're looking for specifics: early morning scheduling, minimal outdoor exposure, moisture barrier wrapping for high-value instruments, shaded truck positioning, and direct transit with no stops.

![Close Up View Of A Black Grand Piano In  1](/images/blog/2027/02/7-questions-to-ask-before-hiring-piano-movers/close-up-view-of-a-black-grand-piano-in--1.webp)


If the answer is "we wrap it in blankets and put it on the truck," they haven't thought about the climate challenges that are unique to this area. A piano sitting in a hot truck in July needs more protection than blankets alone.

## 6. Who Will Actually Be Moving My Piano?

Some companies subcontract their piano moves to third-party crews. You might book with Company A, but Company B's team shows up on moving day. This creates accountability gaps. If something goes wrong, who's responsible?

Ask whether the crew is employed by the company or subcontracted. Ask if the same crew handles both the pickup and delivery (especially for long-distance moves). Knowing who will be in your home with your instrument matters.

## 7. What Happens If Something Goes Wrong?

No one likes to think about damage, but knowing the claims process before it's needed is smart planning. Ask:

- How do I file a damage claim?
- What's the timeline for claim resolution?
- Do you repair, replace, or reimburse?
- What documentation do I need to provide?

A company with a clear, straightforward claims process has dealt with issues before and handled them professionally. A company that gets uncomfortable with this question may not have a real process in place.

## Bonus: Check Reviews for Piano-Specific Feedback

Online reviews that specifically mention piano moves carry more weight than general moving reviews. Look for comments about careful handling, proper equipment, and successful outcomes with grand pianos, uprights, and difficult access situations.
