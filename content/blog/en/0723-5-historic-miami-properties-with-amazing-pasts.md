Miami's architecture tells stories that span a century of booms, busts, and reinventions. From Art Deco gems on South Beach to Mediterranean Revival mansions in Coral Gables, these five properties have witnessed the city transform from a frontier outpost to an international destination. Each building has its own moving story, with furniture, artifacts, and entire collections relocated as these spaces changed hands and purposes over the decades.

## 1. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens

This 1916 Italian Renaissance-style estate sits on 50 acres along Biscayne Bay in Coconut Grove. Industrialist James Deering built it as his winter residence, importing European antiques, Renaissance paintings, and decorative arts from around the world. The logistics of moving these treasures to Miami in the early 20th century required coordinating shipments from Spain, Italy, and France through the Port of Miami.

![American Flag Waving Against An Art Deco 1](/images/blog/2027/02/5-historic-miami-properties-with-amazing-pasts/american-flag-waving-against-an-art-deco-1.webp)


Today, the museum maintains over 2,500 objects in its permanent collection. Curators regularly rotate pieces between storage and display, requiring careful handling of fragile antiques that have survived over a century. The estate has hosted presidential visits, international summits, and countless weddings in its formal gardens overlooking the bay.

## 2. The Freedom Tower

Known as "Miami's Ellis Island," this 1925 Mediterranean Revival tower at 600 Biscayne Boulevard originally housed the Miami News. The building's second life began in 1962 when the federal government used it to process Cuban refugees fleeing Castro's revolution. Over 650,000 Cubans passed through its doors, receiving medical exams, immigration paperwork, and their first steps toward new American lives.

The tower sat empty and decaying for years before developer Jorge Perez purchased and restored it. The renovation required moving tons of debris out and bringing restoration materials in through downtown Miami's congested streets. Today it houses the Museum of Art and Design and stands as a National Historic Landmark honoring Miami's Cuban heritage.

## 3. The Biltmore Hotel

This 1926 Coral Gables landmark at 1200 Anastasia Avenue has lived more lives than most buildings twice its age. Developer George Merrick built it as the crown jewel of his planned City Beautiful community. The 315-foot tower, modeled after the Giralda in Seville, dominated the South Florida skyline for decades.

![Classic Vintage Car Parked Outside Park  4](/images/blog/2027/02/5-historic-miami-properties-with-amazing-pasts/classic-vintage-car-parked-outside-park--4.webp)


During World War II, the Army converted the hotel into a hospital, with beds filling the grand ballrooms and the famous pool serving as a therapy facility. The transition required moving out luxury furnishings and moving in medical equipment. After the war, the building served as a Veterans Administration hospital and later a University of Miami medical campus before closing in 1968.

The city of Coral Gables acquired the deteriorating property and oversaw a $55 million restoration in the 1980s. Contractors had to source period-appropriate fixtures and furnishings to return the hotel to its original grandeur. The Biltmore reopened in 1987 and now operates as a luxury resort, with its pool remaining the largest hotel pool in the continental United States.

## 4. Art Deco Historic District

South Beach's Art Deco Historic District contains the largest collection of Art Deco architecture in the world, with over 800 buildings constructed between 1923 and 1943. These pastel-colored hotels and apartment buildings along Ocean Drive, Collins Avenue, and Washington Avenue have housed generations of Miami Beach residents through the city's many transformations.

In the 1970s, the neighborhood had become a neglected retirement community. Preservationist Barbara Capitman led the fight to protect these buildings from demolition, resulting in the district's listing on the National Register of Historic Places in 1979. The designation sparked a revival that brought galleries, restaurants, and a new generation of residents.

Many of these buildings have been converted multiple times between hotels, apartments, and condos. Each conversion means moving entire floors of furniture, fixtures, and personal belongings. The narrow streets and lack of loading zones make moving in the Deco District particularly challenging, requiring early morning moves and creative logistics.

## 5. Coral Gables City Hall

Built in 1928, this Mediterranean Revival building at 405 Biltmore Way serves as the seat of government for one of Miami's most elegant communities. Architect Phineas Paist designed the structure with a bell tower, arched windows, and a central courtyard that reflects George Merrick's vision for Coral Gables as a "City Beautiful."

![Colorful Neon Lights And Lively Street S 3](/images/blog/2027/02/5-historic-miami-properties-with-amazing-pasts/colorful-neon-lights-and-lively-street-s-3.webp)


The building has witnessed the growth of Coral Gables from an ambitious real estate development into a thriving city of 50,000 residents. City offices have expanded and contracted within the same walls, with departments moving between floors and annexes as needs changed. The adjacent Coral Gables Museum, opened in 2011 in the former police and fire station, tells the story of Merrick's dream and its lasting impact on South Florida.
