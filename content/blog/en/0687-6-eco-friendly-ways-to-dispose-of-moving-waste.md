# 6 Eco-Friendly Ways to Dispose of Moving Waste

Moving generates a surprising amount of waste. Between packing materials, discarded items, and the general debris of transitioning between homes, the average residential move in Miami produces a significant amount of stuff headed for the trash. But most of it doesn't have to end up in a landfill. Here are six ways to handle moving waste responsibly.

## 1. Reuse and Pass Along Packing Materials

Cardboard boxes, bubble wrap, packing paper, and foam peanuts don't need to be single-use items. After you unpack at your new place, post your moving boxes on your neighborhood's Buy Nothing group on Facebook or on Craigslist under the free section. In Miami, someone is always moving, and free boxes disappear within hours of being posted.

![A Chaotic Pile Of Metal Scrap And Waste  4](/images/blog/2027/01/6-eco-friendly-ways-to-dispose-of-moving-waste/a-chaotic-pile-of-metal-scrap-and-waste--4.webp)


If you're getting supplies before your move, check those same groups before buying new. Many Miami residents give away boxes in bulk after their own moves. UHaul also has a box exchange program at some locations where people drop off used boxes for others.

## 2. Recycle Cardboard and Paper Properly

Miami-Dade's curbside recycling program accepts cardboard, paper, and paperboard. Flatten all boxes before putting them in your recycling bin to maximize space. Remove any tape, labels, and non-paper packing materials first.

If you have more boxes than your bin can handle (common after a move), break them down and stack them next to the bin. Most waste haulers in Miami-Dade will take overflow cardboard on recycling day. For truly large volumes, you can drop off directly at the county's recycling facilities.

## 3. Donate Unwanted Items Instead of Trashing Them

The decluttering that happens before and during a move is the perfect opportunity to donate. Clothing, furniture, kitchenware, books, toys, and working electronics all have homes waiting for them at Miami donation centers.

Goodwill, Salvation Army, and Habitat for Humanity ReStore all operate in Miami-Dade and accept a wide range of household items. For larger pieces, schedule a free pickup. For smaller loads, drop off during store hours. Each item donated is one less bag in the landfill and a potential tax deduction for you.

## 4. Handle Electronics Through E-Waste Programs

Old computers, phones, TVs, cables, and peripherals contain toxic materials that shouldn't go in regular trash. They also contain valuable metals that can be recovered through proper recycling.

![A Cluttered Street With Broken Bottles A 1](/images/blog/2027/01/6-eco-friendly-ways-to-dispose-of-moving-waste/a-cluttered-street-with-broken-bottles-a-1.webp)


Best Buy locations across Miami accept electronics for recycling at no charge. Miami-Dade County also hosts periodic e-waste collection events at parks and community centers throughout the year. Check the county's event calendar for upcoming dates near you.

## 5. Dispose of Chemicals and Hazardous Materials Safely

Moving often uncovers forgotten chemicals: old paint cans under the sink, pool chemicals in the garage, cleaning solvents, batteries, and expired medications. None of these belong in regular trash or recycling bins.

Miami-Dade's Home Chemical Collection Center at 8831 NW 58th Street accepts all of these materials from county residents for free. The center handles paint, pesticides, motor oil, batteries, propane tanks, fluorescent bulbs, and more. Plan a single trip before your move to drop off everything at once.

For medications, many CVS and Walgreens pharmacies in Miami-Dade participate in drug take-back programs that accept unused and expired prescriptions.

## Small Changes, Big Impact

No single action eliminates all moving waste, but combining these six approaches dramatically reduces what ends up in Miami-Dade's landfills. Your move becomes lighter on the environment and often lighter on your wallet too, since many of these options are free or save money compared to standard disposal.
