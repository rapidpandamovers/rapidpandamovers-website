Antique furniture does not respond well to the standard moving playbook. Old wood is more brittle, joints rely on hide glue instead of modern adhesives, veneers lift at the edges, and finishes can scratch from materials that are perfectly safe for newer furniture. If you are moving antiques in Miami this December, the good news is that winter conditions actually work in your favor. Here is what proper antique moving looks like.

## Why Antiques Need Special Handling

Modern furniture is built with engineered wood, metal fasteners, and synthetic finishes designed to withstand some rough handling. Antiques are a different story:

![A Couple Packing Labeled Boxes In A Mini 3](/images/blog/2029/12/december-moving-antique-moving-fundamentals/a-couple-packing-labeled-boxes-in-a-mini-3.webp)


- **Wood joints dry out over time.** Mortise-and-tenon joints in pieces from the 18th and 19th centuries may hold by friction alone if the original glue has degraded.
- **Veneers are thin and fragile.** A veneer edge that has lifted even slightly can catch on a blanket and peel away during a move.
- **Finishes vary widely.** Shellac, lacquer, oil, and wax all react differently to pressure, moisture, and temperature. Plastic wrap placed directly against a shellac finish can bond to the surface and pull it off when removed.
- **Hardware is often original.** Brass pulls, hinges, and escutcheons may be irreplaceable. They need to be removed, wrapped individually, and labeled.

## Before the Move: Documentation and Prep

### Get appraisals and photographs
Before anything gets wrapped, document every piece. Photograph each item from multiple angles, including close-ups of any existing damage, maker's marks, and hardware. If pieces have not been appraised recently, get updated appraisals for insurance purposes.

### Dust but do not polish
Wax or oil applied before a move creates a slippery surface that makes pieces harder to grip. Dust each piece gently and leave it at that.

### Remove detachable parts
Take off drawers, shelves, doors, mirrors, and hardware. Wrap and label each component separately. Drawers should be emptied completely. A drawer that slides open during transport can damage the piece and anything nearby.

### Check structural stability
Gently test each joint. If a chair leg wobbles or a table leaf is loose, our crew needs to know before they lift it. Weak joints get extra support during wrapping.

## The Wrapping Process

The layering order matters:

1. **Acid-free tissue or clean cotton sheets** go directly against the finish. Never use newspaper (the ink transfers) or plastic (it traps moisture).
2. **Moving blankets or furniture pads** provide the cushioning layer. Two to three layers work for most pieces.
3. **Stretch wrap goes over the blankets,** never directly on wood. The wrap holds the blankets in place without tape touching the finish.

For pieces with glass (china cabinets, curio cases), the glass gets taped with painter's tape in an X pattern, then padded separately. If the glass is antique or has beveled edges, custom cardboard shielding is safer than tape.

## Why December Works for Antique Moves

Miami's summer humidity regularly hits 85-90%, and antique wood absorbs and releases moisture with every shift. This expansion and contraction stress old joints and can cause cracking in dry-climate pieces that have been in Florida for a short time.

December humidity drops to the 60-70% range, and temperatures stay in the mid-70s. These conditions are easier on old wood:

- Less moisture absorption during loading, transport, and unloading
- No heat stress on finishes from a truck interior that can reach 130+ degrees in summer
- Reduced risk of condensation when moving between air-conditioned spaces and outdoors

If you have a choice of when to move antiques in South Florida, winter gives you the most stable conditions.

## Climate Control During Transport

Even in December, we take precautions:

![Close Up Of A Person Wrapping A Wooden C 2](/images/blog/2029/12/december-moving-antique-moving-fundamentals/close-up-of-a-person-wrapping-a-wooden-c-2.webp)


- **Climate-controlled trucks** maintain stable temperature and humidity throughout transport
- **No stacking heavy items on or near antiques.** Each piece gets its own padded space in the truck
- **Strapping without pressure.** We use ratchet straps on the pads, never directly against the piece, and tighten just enough to prevent shifting without compressing the wood

## FAQ

**Should I get insurance for antique moving?**
Yes. Standard moving coverage (released value protection) pays only $0.60 per pound per item. A 40-pound antique chair worth $5,000 would get $24 under that coverage. Full-value protection or a separate rider through your homeowner's insurance is the way to go.

**Can movers handle grandfather clocks?**
Yes, but they require special preparation. The pendulum, weights, and movement must be secured or removed before transport. The case must stay upright throughout the move.

**How do I find the value of my antiques?**
Contact a certified appraiser through the American Society of Appraisers or the Appraisers Association of America. Online estimates are unreliable for insurance purposes.

**Will moving damage antique furniture?**
Not when handled correctly. Professional movers trained in antique handling use proper materials, wrapping techniques, and transport methods that protect pieces. The risk comes from shortcuts: wrong materials, rushed wrapping, or stacking.
