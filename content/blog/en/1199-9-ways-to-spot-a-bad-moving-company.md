Moving scams cost Americans millions every year, and South Florida has more than its share of shady operators. Before you hand over your belongings and your money, know what to look for. Here are nine red flags that separate legitimate Miami movers from those who will leave you stranded or overcharged.

## 1. No Physical Address

Legitimate companies have real offices you can visit. In Miami, that might be a warehouse in Doral or an office near the airport. A P.O. box only, a residential address, or no verifiable location at all should send you running. Scammers use fake addresses so you have nowhere to go when things go wrong. Before booking, Google the address and check Street View. If it looks like a strip mall mailbox store, keep searching.

## 2. Unusually Low Estimates

When you get quotes from five Miami movers and one comes in 40% lower, that is not a deal. That is bait-and-switch territory. These companies lowball to win your business, then pile on fees for stairs, long carries, heavy items, packing materials, and anything else they can think of. Your $800 quote becomes a $2,400 bill on moving day when your furniture is already on the truck. Get at least three quotes and treat outliers with suspicion.

## 3. Large Cash Deposit Requests

Professional movers might ask for a small deposit to secure your date, typically 10-20% payable by card or check. If someone demands hundreds in cash upfront, or wants a large percentage before they have touched a single box, something is off. Cash leaves no paper trail. When the company disappears with your deposit, you have no recourse. Reputable Miami movers accept credit cards and provide receipts for everything.

## 4. No Written Estimate

Verbal quotes are worthless. Everything should be documented: the total estimate, hourly rates, what is included, insurance coverage, payment terms, and any potential additional fees. In Florida, legitimate movers provide written estimates as required by state law. If a company resists putting numbers on paper, they are planning to change those numbers later. Get binding or not-to-exceed estimates in writing, and read the fine print.

## 5. No Insurance Information Available

Licensed movers carry cargo insurance and can show proof immediately. In Florida, moving companies must carry minimum liability coverage. Ask for the certificate of insurance and verify it is current. If they claim to have insurance but cannot produce documentation, or if they become vague about coverage limits, that tells you everything. When your grandmother's antique dresser arrives in pieces, you want more than a shrug and a "sorry."

## 6. Unmarked Trucks

Established moving companies invest in their brand. Their trucks display the company name, logo, phone number, and DOT number. An unmarked rental truck or plain white van suggests fly-by-night operations. These companies rent trucks specifically to avoid identification. After they damage your items or inflate your bill, good luck tracking them down. In neighborhoods like Brickell and Coral Gables, building management often requires moving companies to display credentials. Ask why their trucks are blank.

## 7. Negative or No Reviews

Check Google, Yelp, BBB, and the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services database. A pattern of complaints about hostage situations (holding belongings until you pay inflated fees), damaged items, or no-shows tells you what to expect. Equally suspicious: a company with zero reviews or only glowing 5-star reviews from accounts with no other activity. Look for detailed reviews that mention specific crew members, addresses, and timelines. In Miami, legitimate movers build reputations over years across multiple platforms.

## 8. Pressure to Sign Immediately

"This price is only good today" or "I have another customer ready to book your date" are pressure tactics used by desperate or dishonest companies. Legitimate movers give you time to compare quotes, ask questions, and read contracts. They know their reputation speaks for itself. If you feel rushed or manipulated, trust your instincts and walk away. Good movers are busy because they are good, not because they pressure customers into snap decisions.

## 9. No DOT Number

For interstate moves, movers must be registered with the U.S. Department of Transportation and display a DOT number on their trucks and paperwork. You can verify this number at FMCSA.dot.gov. For Florida-only moves, check registration with the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services. No registration means they are operating illegally and have no accountability. If something goes wrong, you have no regulatory body to file a complaint with.

## How to Protect Yourself

Beyond watching for red flags, take proactive steps:

- **Get everything in writing** before moving day
- **Take photos** of valuable items before they are packed
- **Pay by credit card** for dispute protection
- **Keep inventory** of everything being moved
- **Verify licenses** with state and federal databases

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much should a legitimate Miami move cost?

Local moves in Miami-Dade typically run $100-150 per hour for a two-person crew with truck. A 2-bedroom apartment takes 4-6 hours on average. Quotes significantly below or above this range warrant extra scrutiny.

### What should I do if I suspect a moving scam?

Report it to the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services at 1-800-HELP-FLA, file a complaint with the BBB, and leave honest reviews to warn others.

### Can I negotiate with moving companies?

Yes, but focus on value rather than price alone. Ask about included services, insurance options, and flexibility on timing rather than pushing for the lowest number.
