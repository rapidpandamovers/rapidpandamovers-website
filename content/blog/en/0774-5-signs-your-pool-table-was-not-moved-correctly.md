# 5 Signs Your Pool Table Was Not Moved Correctly

You hired movers, the table is set up in your new place, and everything looks fine at first glance. But after a few games, something feels off. Balls aren't rolling where they should. Shots that used to drop are rattling out. The table just doesn't play the way it did before. These are signs that something went wrong during the move, and catching the problems early can save you from bigger repairs down the road.

## 1. Balls Drift to One Side

This is the most obvious sign of a leveling problem. Place a ball in the center of the table, give it a gentle roll, and watch its path. On a properly leveled table, the ball will slow down and stop in a straight line. If it curves or drifts toward one rail, the table is tilted.

![A Person Arranging Billiard Balls On A B 2](/images/blog/2027/05/5-signs-your-pool-table-was-not-moved-correctly/a-person-arranging-billiard-balls-on-a-b-2.webp)


Set a ball on the center spot and leave it completely still. If it starts rolling on its own, the tilt is severe enough that the table needs immediate attention. Even a slight drift means the [**Pool Table Moving**](/pool-table-moving) crew didn't finish the leveling process properly. This is the most common problem we fix when called in after another company's move.

Sometimes the issue isn't the overall table tilt but a single slate section that's sitting higher or lower than its neighbors. This creates localized dead spots where balls change speed or direction as they cross a seam.

## 2. Visible or Feelable Seam Lines

Run your fingertip slowly across the playing surface, perpendicular to each slate seam. On a properly assembled table, you shouldn't feel the transition between slate sections. The beeswax filling should create a perfectly smooth, undetectable joint.

If you can feel a ridge, bump, or valley at the seam, one of several things went wrong:

- The slate sections weren't aligned flush with each other
- The beeswax filling was skipped or poorly applied
- The beeswax wasn't scraped flush with the slate surface
- The slate shifted during felt installation

Seam problems affect ball roll every time a ball crosses the joint. On a table with seams running across the width, nearly every shot crosses at least one seam, so this isn't a minor issue.

## 3. Loose or Uneven Rails

Grab each rail section and try to wiggle it. There should be zero movement. A rail that shifts even slightly will cause inconsistent ball rebounds. Players who rely on bank shots and kick shots will notice this immediately.

![A Row Of Empty Billiard Tables In A Diml 1](/images/blog/2027/05/5-signs-your-pool-table-was-not-moved-correctly/a-row-of-empty-billiard-tables-in-a-diml-1.webp)


Common causes of loose rails after a move:

- **Under-torqued bolts**: The mover didn't tighten the rail bolts enough. This is easy to fix but should have been caught during setup.
- **Cross-threaded bolts**: Forcing a bolt at the wrong angle damages the threads in the slate insert. This requires professional repair.
- **Wrong bolt positions**: If the rails weren't labeled during disassembly, they might be in the wrong positions. Pool table rails are not interchangeable. Each one is fitted to its specific location.

Also check the cushion response by rolling a ball firmly into each rail. The rebound should feel consistent around the entire table. Dead spots (where the ball doesn't bounce back as strongly) can indicate a damaged cushion or a rail that was remounted with the wrong orientation.

## 4. Wrinkled, Bunched, or Sagging Felt

Look at the playing surface at eye level from each end of the table. The felt should be perfectly smooth and taut. Wrinkles, bunches, or sagging areas indicate improper installation:

- **Wrinkles near the pockets**: Usually caused by pulling the felt unevenly during stapling. The cloth needs to be stretched from the center outward with consistent tension.
- **Bunching along the rails**: This happens when the felt wasn't trimmed properly or wasn't pulled tight before the rails were bolted down.
- **Sagging in the center**: The felt has lost its tension, possibly because it was reused after being stretched during the move, or because it absorbed moisture during transport in Miami's humidity.

Felt issues affect gameplay immediately. Wrinkles deflect balls, bunches create dead spots, and sagging slows ball speed in affected areas.

## 5. The Table Wobbles or Rocks

Place your hands on opposite corners of the table and push down alternately. The table should feel solid and immovable. If it rocks, the legs aren't properly adjusted for the floor, or worse, a leg joint was damaged during the move.

![Vibrant Billiard Balls Neatly Arranged O 4](/images/blog/2027/05/5-signs-your-pool-table-was-not-moved-correctly/vibrant-billiard-balls-neatly-arranged-o-4.webp)


Wobbling causes two problems: it throws off your level, and the constant micro-movement during play gradually loosens rail bolts and frame joints over time. What starts as a slight rock becomes a major structural issue after a few months of play.

In Miami homes with tile floors, a single leg sitting on a grout line instead of a flat tile surface can cause wobbling. This is easy to fix with a shim, but it should have been addressed during setup.
