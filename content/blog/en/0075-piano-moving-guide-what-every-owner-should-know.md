# Piano Moving Guide: What Every Owner Should Know

Your piano is probably one of the most valuable and sentimental items in your home. Whether it's a family heirloom that's been passed down through generations or a brand-new Steinway you saved years to buy, moving it safely takes real planning. Here in Miami, where humidity and heat add extra complications, getting this right matters even more.

## Why Piano Moving Is Different from Everything Else

A piano is not furniture. It's a precision instrument with thousands of moving parts, held in tension by strings exerting up to 20 tons of force on the frame. Drop a dresser, and you've got a dent. Drop a piano, and you could destroy an instrument worth tens of thousands of dollars.

![An Elderly Man Playing Piano In A Dark J 1](/images/blog/2024/10/piano-moving-guide-what-every-owner-should-know/an-elderly-man-playing-piano-in-a-dark-j-1.webp)


Weight is the obvious challenge. Upright pianos range from 300 to 800 pounds. A concert grand can top 1,200 pounds. But weight is only part of the story. The shape is awkward, the center of gravity shifts depending on the type, and the internal mechanisms are surprisingly fragile. One hard jolt can knock the action out of alignment, crack the soundboard, or snap strings.

In South Florida, you also have to think about temperature and moisture. Moving a piano from an air-conditioned Coral Gables living room into a hot, humid Miami afternoon, even briefly, puts stress on the wood and tuning pins.

## How to Plan Your Piano Move

### Measure Everything First

Before moving day, measure every doorway, hallway, and stairwell between the piano and the truck. Many Miami homes, especially older ones in neighborhoods like Coconut Grove and Little Havana, have narrow hallways and tight turns. If the piano won't fit through the front door, your movers need to know ahead of time so they can plan an alternate route or remove doors from hinges.

### Schedule Around the Weather

Miami's afternoon thunderstorms between June and October can roll in fast. We always recommend scheduling piano moves for early morning, when temperatures are lower and rain is less likely. Even a few minutes of exposure to rain can damage the finish and warp internal components.

### Lock the Keyboard Lid

Close and lock the keyboard lid before moving day. If it doesn't have a lock, use painter's tape to keep it shut. Open keys during transport can lead to broken keys and damaged hammers.

### Clear the Path

Remove rugs, wall decorations, and any obstacles between the piano's current location and the front door. Lay down floor protectors on hardwood and tile, which are common in Miami condos and can be scratched by piano wheels or dollies.

## After the Move: What to Expect

Plan to have your piano tuned about two to three weeks after the move. This gives the instrument time to acclimate to its new environment. In Miami, where indoor humidity levels can swing depending on your AC setup, this adjustment period is especially important.

Place the piano away from direct sunlight, exterior walls, and air conditioning vents. These all cause uneven temperature changes that affect tuning stability.
