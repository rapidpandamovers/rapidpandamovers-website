# 7 Ways to Tell If Your Pool Table Is Properly Leveled

After a move, a re-felt, or even just settling into a new home, your pool table's level can shift. Sometimes the change is obvious. Other times, you just feel like something is off without being able to pinpoint it. Here are seven tests you can run to determine whether your table is properly leveled, ranging from simple eyeball checks to precision measurements.

## 1. The Still Ball Test

Place a ball on the center spot and take your hands away completely. Watch it for 30 seconds. On a level table, the ball should stay exactly where you placed it. If it rolls in any direction, even slowly, the table is tilted. Try this at five points: center, and about 12 inches from each cushion along the center line in both directions. A ball that stays put at the center but drifts near the edges can indicate a localized slate problem rather than an overall tilt.

## 2. The Slow Roll Test

Place a ball against one cushion at the center of a long rail. Roll it gently toward the opposite cushion. The ball should travel in a straight line and arrive at a point directly across from where it started. Repeat from both short rails too. If the ball curves left or right during its roll, the table has a cross-tilt. This test is more sensitive than the still ball test because it reveals slight tilts that aren't steep enough to start a ball rolling from a standstill.

## 3. The Rail-to-Rail Bounce Test

Hit a ball firmly into a cushion at a 90-degree angle from the center of the table. It should bounce straight back to where it started. If it returns at an angle, the table may be tilted, or the rails may not be mounted evenly. Test all four rails from the center point. Uneven rail mounting is a separate issue from table level, but both problems show up with this test. ## 4. The Machinist's Level Test

This is the most accurate method. Place a 4-foot machinist's level on the playing surface (on top of the felt) and check it in multiple positions:

- Down the center lengthwise
- Down the center widthwise
- Along each rail edge (about 2 inches from the cushion)
- Diagonally from corner to corner, both directions

A properly leveled table will show the bubble centered in the vial at every position. If the bubble is off-center at some positions but centered at others, you likely have a slate alignment issue rather than an overall table tilt. Phone apps are not accurate enough for this test. Use a real machinist's level with a sensitivity of at least 0.02 inches per foot.

## 5. The Break Test

Rack a full set of balls and break them from the head end. Watch how the balls spread. On a level table, the balls should scatter roughly evenly across the playing surface. If most of the balls consistently end up on one side of the table after the break, gravity is pulling them that direction. This isn't a precise measurement, but experienced players can spot the trend after just two or three breaks.

## 6. The Slate Seam Check

Run your fingertip across each slate seam (where two slate sections meet). You should feel nothing. The transition should be perfectly smooth, with the beeswax filling creating an undetectable joint. If you feel a ridge, valley, or bump at the seam, one slate section is sitting higher or lower than its neighbor. This won't show up as an overall tilt on a level, but it creates a dead spot that deflects balls every time they cross the seam. Seam problems are one of the most common issues after a move that skipped proper beeswax filling.

## 7. The Lag Test

The lag is a standard billiards exercise where two players simultaneously shoot a ball from behind the head string toward the foot cushion and back. The ball that stops closest to the head cushion wins the lag. For a level test, lag a ball from each end of the table and see where it stops. On a level table, a ball lagged with the same speed from each end should travel approximately the same total distance. If the ball consistently rolls farther when shot from one end, gravity is helping it on the return trip from that direction.
