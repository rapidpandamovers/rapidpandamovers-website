Hiring the wrong moving company can turn a stressful day into a genuine disaster. Every year, the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (FMCSA) receives thousands of complaints about movers who damage belongings, inflate prices, or hold household goods hostage for extra payment.

The good news is that most bad movers show warning signs before moving day. Here are 9 red flags that mean you should reconsider your choice, and what to do if you spot them.

## 1. No Physical Address

A real moving company has a real office or warehouse. If the company only lists a P.O. box, or if you cannot verify their address through Google Maps or a drive-by visit, that is a problem.

![A Businessman Is Reviewing A Contract Wh 2](/images/blog/2029/08/9-signs-youve-hired-the-wrong-moving-company/a-businessman-is-reviewing-a-contract-wh-2.webp)


Fly-by-night operators deliberately avoid a permanent location so they are harder to track down after a bad move. Search the company name and address before booking, and confirm the location actually exists.

## 2. The Estimate Is Dramatically Lower Than Everyone Else

If one quote comes in 30 to 50 percent below the others, it is almost certainly a lowball tactic. The company gives you an unrealistically low estimate to win your business, then adds charges on moving day once your belongings are loaded on the truck.

This bait-and-switch is one of the most common moving scams in the industry. Get at least three estimates and treat outliers with skepticism.

## 3. They Want a Large Cash Deposit Upfront

Legitimate movers may ask for a small deposit or credit card hold, but demanding a large cash payment before the move is a major red flag. Once cash changes hands, you have little recourse if the company disappears or fails to show.

The FMCSA advises against paying large deposits, and many state consumer protection agencies flag this as a common scam indicator.

## 4. Nothing Is in Writing

A professional mover provides a written estimate that includes the scope of work, pricing structure, liability coverage, and any additional fees that might apply. If the company offers only verbal quotes or avoids putting terms in writing, you have no documentation to fall back on if disputes arise.

At minimum, you should receive either a binding estimate (a fixed price) or a non-binding estimate (an approximation with conditions). Both should be on paper or in a signed digital document.

## 5. They Cannot Show Insurance or Licensing

Every interstate mover must be registered with the U.S. Department of Transportation and carry a USDOT number. You can verify any company's registration, insurance status, and complaint history through the [FMCSA's SAFER system](https://safer.fmcsa.dot.gov/).

![Close Up Of A Hand Signing A Formal Docu 3](/images/blog/2029/08/9-signs-youve-hired-the-wrong-moving-company/close-up-of-a-hand-signing-a-formal-docu-3.webp)


For local moves within Florida, the company should hold a valid state registration and carry liability insurance. Ask for proof, and verify it independently. If they hesitate or refuse, walk away.

## 6. Their Trucks Are Unmarked

Professional moving companies use branded vehicles with the company name, USDOT number, and contact information clearly displayed. Unmarked rental trucks suggest the company may not own equipment, may not be properly licensed, or may be operating informally.

This does not mean every rented truck is a scam, but combined with other red flags on this list, unmarked vehicles should raise your concern.

## 7. Online Reviews Show a Pattern of Complaints

Check Google, Yelp, and the Better Business Bureau. No company has a perfect record, but watch for patterns:

- Multiple complaints about damaged items with no resolution
- Reports of price increases on moving day
- Accusations of held-hostage belongings (the company refuses to unload until you pay extra)
- A complete absence of reviews, which can indicate a brand-new operation created to avoid a bad reputation

Also check whether the company has responded to negative reviews. How a company handles complaints tells you a lot about how they will handle your move.

## 8. They Pressure You to Sign Immediately

High-pressure sales tactics, like telling you a price is only valid "today" or that spots are filling up "right now," are designed to prevent you from comparing options. A reputable company gives you time to review the estimate, ask questions, and make a decision.

If a salesperson pushes hard for an immediate commitment, they are counting on you not doing your homework. Take the time to compare.

## 9. No DOT Number for Interstate Moves

Any company performing moves across state lines must have a USDOT number issued by the FMCSA. This number should appear on the company's website, written estimates, and truck signage. If a company cannot provide a DOT number for an interstate move, they are operating illegally.

You can verify DOT numbers at [FMCSA's website](https://safer.fmcsa.dot.gov/). Enter the number and confirm the company's name, address, and operating authority match.

## What to Do If You Spot These Signs

If you notice these red flags before your move:

![Unrecognizable Worker Standing At Table  1](/images/blog/2029/08/9-signs-youve-hired-the-wrong-moving-company/unrecognizable-worker-standing-at-table--1.webp)


1. **Cancel and get a refund.** If you paid a deposit, dispute the charge with your credit card company if the mover refuses to return it.
2. **File a complaint.** Report the company to the [FMCSA](https://nccdb.fmcsa.dot.gov/nccdb/home.aspx), your state attorney general, and the Better Business Bureau.
3. **Find a replacement.** Ask friends for referrals, check Google reviews, and verify licensing before hiring a new company.

If you notice red flags on moving day:

1. **Document everything.** Take photos and video of the crew, truck, and condition of your belongings.
2. **Do not sign anything you disagree with.** You have the right to note discrepancies on the bill of lading.
3. **Call the police if your belongings are held hostage.** This is illegal, and law enforcement can intervene.

## How to Verify a Moving Company

Before booking with any mover, run through this checklist:

- Verify USDOT number (for interstate moves) at the FMCSA site
- Check Florida state registration for local moves
- Read Google, Yelp, and BBB reviews
- Confirm a physical address exists
- Get a written estimate with all fees itemized
- Ask for proof of insurance
- Confirm payment terms and methods accepted

## FAQ

**How do I check if a moving company is legitimate?**
Search their USDOT number on the FMCSA's SAFER website. For Florida local moves, check the company's state registration and reviews on Google, Yelp, and BBB.

**What should I do if a mover holds my belongings hostage?**
Call local law enforcement. Holding goods hostage for extra payment is illegal. File complaints with the FMCSA and your state attorney general's office.

**How many estimates should I get before hiring a mover?**
At least three. This gives you a baseline for pricing and helps you spot unusually low (bait-and-switch) or high estimates.

**Are online-only moving brokers safe?**
Some are legitimate, but brokers do not perform the move themselves. They sell your job to a carrier, which can lead to miscommunication about pricing and service. If possible, book directly with the company that will actually show up on moving day.
