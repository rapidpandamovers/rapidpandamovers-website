# How to Move a Marble Table Without Cracking It

Marble looks solid and indestructible sitting in your dining room. But the moment you try to move it, you discover the truth: marble is brittle, heavy, and shockingly easy to crack. A hairline fracture from a bump during transport can spread into a full break weeks later, and marble repair is expensive when it's even possible. In Miami luxury homes, marble tables are everywhere, and we move them without incident by following a specific process.

## Why Marble Cracks During Moves

Marble is a crystalline stone that fractures along natural weak points called veining. Those beautiful streaks running through your Calacatta tabletop are actually the spots most likely to break. Impact isn't the only threat. Vibration during truck transport can stress these veins over time, and uneven support underneath the slab creates pressure points that lead to snapping. Even temperature changes from a cool, air-conditioned home in Key Biscayne to a hot loading dock can cause thermal stress.

## Separating the Top from the Base

Almost every marble table should be disassembled for moving. The slab comes off the base and travels on its own. We check how the top attaches. Some are held by gravity alone, sitting on rubber pads. Others use adhesive, suction mounts, or mechanical clips. For adhesive mounts, we use a putty knife and dental floss technique to separate the slab without chipping the underside. The base gets wrapped and moved separately.

## Wrapping and Crating the Slab

A bare marble slab in a moving truck is a disaster waiting to happen. We start with a layer of furniture pads directly on the surface, followed by rigid foam board on each face, then corrugated cardboard, and finally moving blankets secured with stretch wrap. For high-value pieces, we build a custom wooden crate around the slab with internal bracing. The slab should travel on edge, like a picture frame, not flat. Standing it on edge distributes shock forces along the stone's strongest axis.

## Transport Position and Securing

In the truck, the wrapped marble slab stands upright between padded supports. We use A-frame dollies designed for stone and glass transport. Ratchet straps hold the slab to the A-frame at multiple points without putting pressure on the edges. Nothing gets stacked against the marble. We leave clearance on all sides so shifting cargo can't bump into it during the drive across town.

## Reassembly and Placement

At the destination, we set the base in position first and verify it's level. The marble slab goes back on with at least four people for anything over 100 pounds, which includes most dining table tops. We lift from underneath with suction cups and padded hands, never gripping edges. Once placed, we check that the slab sits evenly on the base with no wobble points that could create stress fractures over time.

## Dealing with Miami's Humidity

Marble is porous and absorbs moisture. In Florida's humidity, condensation can form on a cold marble surface brought into a warm environment, making it slippery and harder to grip. We let marble pieces acclimate to room temperature before final positioning and use suction-cup lifters rated for damp surfaces.
