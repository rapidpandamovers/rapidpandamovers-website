# 8 Tips From Museum Handlers for Your Home Move

Museum art handlers move priceless works across the globe without damage, and they follow strict protocols that any homeowner can adapt. Several members of our team trained in museum settings before joining us, and they brought those techniques to every residential move we do in Miami. Here are eight museum-grade practices you can apply to your own art collection.

## 1. Always Wear Clean Cotton Gloves

Museum handlers never touch artwork with bare hands. Skin oils contain acids that etch into metal, stain paper, and degrade varnish over time. Buy a pack of clean white cotton gloves from any art supply store and wear them whenever you handle framed art, sculptures, or unframed works. Replace them when they get dirty. A ten-pack costs under five dollars and protects surfaces that cost thousands.

## 2. Carry Paintings with Two Hands, One on Each Side

Never carry a painting by the top of the frame or the hanging wire. The wire can snap, and gripping the top puts all the weight on one joint. Museum handlers carry paintings vertically with one hand on each side, palms flat against the frame edges, thumbs over the top. This distributes the weight evenly and gives you full control. For large pieces, two people use this same technique, one on each end.

## 3. Clear the Path Before You Move Anything

Museum handlers walk the route before picking up a single piece. They check for obstacles, measure doorways, and identify any tight spots. Do the same in your home. Move furniture out of the path, secure loose rugs, prop open doors, and make sure your route from wall to truck is clear. In Miami high-rises, check elevator dimensions and reserve the freight elevator in advance.

## 4. Never Stack Paintings Face to Face Without Protection

When staging artwork for packing or loading, lean pieces face to face with a padded divider between them. Frame hardware, wire, and bracket edges on the back can scratch the front of an adjacent piece. Museum storage rooms use padded vertical racks with slots for each work. For a home move, leaning pieces against a padded wall with cardboard or foam between them achieves the same protection.

## 5. Control the Environment, Not Just the Packing

Museums maintain strict temperature and humidity ranges because environmental shifts cause more long-term damage than physical impact. You can't control the weather on moving day, but you can minimize exposure time. Pack artwork last, load it first, and get it into the climate-controlled truck as quickly as possible. At the destination, unload art first and bring it inside before handling furniture.

## 6. Use Condition Reports

Before any artwork changes location in a museum, a handler fills out a condition report documenting every scratch, crack, and blemish. Take photos of each piece from the front, back, and edges before your move. Note any existing damage in writing. This protects you for insurance purposes and helps your moving team know where to take extra care.

## 7. Let Artwork Acclimate Before Unwrapping

When a painting arrives at a new museum, it sits in its crate for 24 to 48 hours before anyone opens it. This gives the piece time to adjust to the new environment's temperature and humidity. Rapid changes cause condensation and dimensional shifts in wood and canvas. After your move, leave wrapped artwork in the new room for at least a day before unpacking, especially if the AC hasn't stabilized yet.

## 8. Store Art Upright, Never Flat

Flat storage puts weight on the canvas or paper surface and invites objects to be stacked on top. Museums store paintings vertically in padded racks. During your move, keep paintings upright in the truck, upright during staging, and upright in any temporary storage. The only time a painting should be horizontal is when it's being wrapped on a padded table.
