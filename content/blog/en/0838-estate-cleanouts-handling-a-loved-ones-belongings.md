# Estate Cleanouts: Handling a Loved One's Belongings

Losing a family member is hard enough without the added weight of clearing out their home. Whether it's a house in Little Havana that's been in the family for decades or a condo in Aventura, the task of sorting through a lifetime of belongings requires both physical effort and emotional energy. Knowing what to expect and having a plan makes the process more manageable.

## Give Yourself Time When Possible

If the property isn't under immediate pressure from a sale, lease termination, or estate timeline, take at least a few weeks before starting. Rushing into a cleanout while still processing grief leads to regretted decisions. Items get thrown away that held meaning, and family disagreements about who gets what can escalate when everyone is stressed.

When there is a deadline, such as a property closing or a condo association's vacancy requirements, having professional help becomes even more valuable. A crew can handle the physical labor while you focus on the personal decisions.

## Start With Valuables and Personal Items

Before any bulk removal happens, walk through the home and collect:

- **Financial documents:** Bank statements, insurance policies, property deeds, tax returns, and any safe deposit box keys. In Florida, these may be needed for probate.
- **Jewelry and heirlooms:** Check drawers, closets, shoe boxes, and between mattresses. Older generations often stored valuables in unexpected places.
- **Photos and personal letters:** These are irreplaceable. Gather them all in one place before any sorting begins.
- **Keys, passwords, and account information:** Check for lockboxes, home safes, and desk drawers.

## Divide Items Among Family Members

Once valuables are secured, invite family members to identify specific items they'd like to keep. Setting a fair process early prevents conflict. Some families do a round-robin selection, others assign categories by interest. The key is open communication before anything leaves the house.

For Miami families with relatives spread across the country or internationally, take photos of items and share them digitally so that out-of-town family members can participate in the selection.

## Donate What the Family Doesn't Want

Many items from an estate are perfectly usable but not something any family member needs. Furniture, kitchenware, clothing, books, and working appliances can go to Miami donation centers:

- **Goodwill** and **Salvation Army** accept a wide range of household items
- **Habitat for Humanity ReStore** takes furniture, appliances, and building materials
- **Vietnam Veterans of America** offers free pickup for clothing and household goods

Donation receipts can benefit the estate's tax situation, so save them for the executor.

## Florida-Specific Considerations

Florida probate law may affect the timeline for clearing a property. If the estate is going through probate, confirm with the executor or estate attorney before removing or disposing of any items. In some cases, an inventory of the home's contents may be required before anything is removed.
