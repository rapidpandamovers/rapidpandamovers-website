# Piano Storage Tips Between Moves

Sometimes the timing doesn't line up. You've sold your Miami home but the new place isn't ready yet. Or you're renovating and the piano needs to go somewhere safe for a few months. Storing a piano is very different from storing furniture, and doing it wrong can cause permanent damage. Here's what you need to know to keep your instrument safe during the in-between period.

## Why Pianos and Standard Storage Don't Mix

Walk into any self-storage facility in Miami and you'll find rows of metal units baking in the Florida sun. Interior temperatures in a non-climate-controlled unit can exceed 130 degrees in summer. Humidity inside those units can hit 90% or higher when the afternoon rains come.

![A Couple Relaxing With Candles Amidst Ca 2](/images/blog/2028/08/piano-storage-tips-between-moves/a-couple-relaxing-with-candles-amidst-ca-2.webp)


A piano stored in those conditions will suffer. The soundboard absorbs moisture and swells, pushing strings sharp and straining glue joints. Felt hammers and dampers soak up humidity and lose their shape. Tuning pins expand in the softened pin block and lose their grip. Mold can grow on felt, wood, and even inside the piano's cabinet.

After just a few weeks in a hot, humid storage unit, a piano can need thousands of dollars in repairs. After a few months, it might not be worth repairing at all.

## Climate-Controlled Storage Is Non-Negotiable

For any acoustic piano, climate-controlled storage is the only acceptable option. The storage space needs to maintain:

- **Temperature**: Between 65 and 75 degrees Fahrenheit, stable throughout the day
- **Humidity**: Between 40% and 50% relative humidity
- **Air circulation**: Stagnant air promotes mold growth even in temperature-controlled spaces

In Miami, climate-controlled storage facilities are common, but the quality varies. Ask about the HVAC system, whether the units are individually temperature controlled or just centrally cooled, and what happens during power outages. A facility with backup generators is ideal for long-term piano storage.

## Preparing the Piano for Storage

### Professional Cleaning

Have the piano cleaned before storage. Dust, fingerprints, and any residue on the surface can interact with the finish over time, especially in a closed environment. A piano technician can also clean the interior action and check for any existing pest issues.

### Wrapping and Covering

The piano should be loosely covered with a breathable cotton cover or light blankets. Do not wrap it tightly in plastic. Plastic traps moisture against the surface and creates a greenhouse effect inside the wrapping, which is exactly what you're trying to avoid in Miami's climate.

Leave the keyboard lid closed but not taped. Air needs to circulate around the instrument. If the storage unit has adequate climate control, a simple cover to keep dust off is sufficient.

### Moth and Pest Protection

Piano felt is made from wool, which moths love. Place cedar blocks or moth-repellent sachets near (not on) the piano. Check with your piano technician about whether to treat the hammers and dampers with a moth-proofing product before storage.

In Miami, pest concerns extend beyond moths. Cockroaches and other insects are attracted to the dark, enclosed spaces inside a piano cabinet. A clean piano in a well-sealed, climate-controlled unit minimizes this risk.

## Positioning in the Storage Unit

### Upright Pianos

Store an upright piano in its normal standing position. Push it against an interior wall, not an exterior wall where temperature fluctuations are greater. Leave a few inches of space between the piano's back and the wall for air circulation. Don't stack anything on top of the piano. The top panel isn't designed to bear weight and can warp.

### Grand Pianos

Grand pianos can be stored either assembled (if the space allows) or disassembled on a piano board. Assembled is preferable when the storage unit is large enough because it avoids the stress of repeated disassembly and reassembly. If space is limited, a professional [**Piano Moving**](/piano-moving) crew should disassemble it onto a board, just as they would for transport.

## How Long Can a Piano Stay in Storage?

With proper climate control and preparation, a piano can stay in storage for months or even years without serious problems. The key variables are the stability of the environment and how well the piano was prepared.

We recommend having a piano technician check the instrument every six months during long-term storage. They can catch early signs of humidity damage, pest issues, or mechanical problems before they become expensive.

## After Storage: Getting Your Piano Back

When it's time to move the piano out of storage and into your new home, plan for the same [**Piano Moving**](/piano-moving) protocols as any other move. The piano will need two to three weeks to acclimate to its new environment before tuning.

If the piano was in storage for more than six months, consider having a technician inspect it before the move. It's easier to address any issues while the piano is still accessible in the storage unit.
