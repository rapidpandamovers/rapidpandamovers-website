Moving scams cost Americans millions of dollars every year. In Miami-Dade County alone, the Better Business Bureau receives hundreds of complaints about fraudulent movers. Some families lose their belongings entirely when trucks never arrive at the destination. Others face surprise bills three times the original quote. Here are nine warning signs that help you spot a scam before handing over your possessions.

## 1. No Physical Address

Legitimate companies have real offices you can visit. A P.O. box only, a vague "Miami area" location, or no verifiable address is a major red flag. In South Florida, scam operators frequently list fake addresses in Doral or Hialeah industrial parks. Before hiring anyone, verify the address exists and matches their business registration with Florida's Division of Corporations.

## 2. Unusually Low Estimates

If one quote comes in at $800 when everyone else says $2,500, something is wrong. Bait-and-switch operators give unrealistic quotes to win your business. Once your belongings are on their truck, the price suddenly triples. Miami's competitive moving market has standard rates. A two-bedroom apartment local move typically runs $400-$800. Anything dramatically below market rate deserves scrutiny.

## 3. Large Cash Deposit Requests

Professional movers might request a small deposit to secure your date, typically 10-20% paid by credit card. A demand for large cash payments upfront, especially 50% or more, signals trouble. Scammers want cash because it cannot be traced or charged back. Reputable Miami movers like those registered with the Florida Movers and Warehousemen's Association accept credit cards and provide receipts.

## 4. No Written Estimate

Everything should be documented: the estimate, terms, insurance coverage, pickup and delivery windows, and all fees. Verbal agreements protect no one. Florida law requires movers to provide written estimates. If a company resists putting numbers on paper, they plan to change those numbers later. Get binding estimates that lock in your price regardless of actual weight.

## 5. No Insurance Information Available

Licensed movers carry cargo insurance and can prove it with a certificate of insurance (COI). Many Brickell and Coral Gables high-rises require a COI before allowing movers in the building. If a company cannot produce insurance documentation within 24 hours, they either lack coverage or are hiding something. Uninsured movers leave you with no recourse when items break or disappear.

## 6. Unmarked Trucks

Professional companies use marked, well-maintained vehicles displaying their company name, logo, USDOT number, and Florida registration. Unmarked rental trucks, especially Budget or U-Haul vehicles, suggest unregistered operations. Legitimate Miami movers invest in their fleet. An unmarked truck makes it nearly impossible to track down the company if they vanish with your belongings.

## 7. Negative or No Reviews

Check Google, Yelp, BBB, and the Florida Attorney General's consumer complaint database. A pattern of complaints about hostage situations (holding belongings until you pay inflated prices) or damaged items reveals the company's true nature. Equally suspicious: a company with zero online presence. Even newer operations should have some reviews. Ask for references from recent Miami-Dade moves and actually call them.

## 8. Pressure to Sign Immediately

"This price is only good today" or "We have another customer interested in your date" are pressure tactics. Legitimate companies understand you need time to compare quotes and verify credentials. High-pressure sales indicate desperation or dishonesty. Reputable Miami movers give you space to make informed decisions because they know their reputation speaks for itself.

## 9. No DOT Number

For interstate moves, movers must register with the Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration and display a USDOT number. You can verify this number at safer.fmcsa.dot.gov. For moves within Florida, the company should have a Florida Mover Registration Number from the Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services. No registration means they are operating illegally and have no accountability.

## How to Verify a Miami Moving Company

Before signing anything, take these steps:

- **Check Florida registration**: Search the Florida Department of Agriculture's Mover Registration database
- **Verify USDOT number**: Use the FMCSA's SAFER system for interstate movers
- **Read the BBB profile**: Look for complaint patterns, not just the rating
- **Request references**: Ask for contacts from recent moves in Kendall, Aventura, or wherever you live
- **Visit the office**: A legitimate company welcomes customers at their location
