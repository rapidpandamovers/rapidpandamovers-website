# How to Protect Pool Table Felt During a Move

Pool table felt is one of the most vulnerable parts of your table during a move. Whether you have tournament-grade Simonis cloth or a standard woolen blend, the felt can tear, stretch, or develop permanent creases if handled carelessly. In Miami's humid climate, felt also absorbs moisture quickly, which can cause it to sag or develop mildew if exposed during transit. Protecting it properly during your move can save you $300 to $800 in re-felting costs.

## Understanding Your Felt Type

Not all pool table cloth is the same, and each type has different vulnerabilities during a move:

![A Close Up Of A Game In Progress On A Bi 1](/images/blog/2024/12/how-to-protect-pool-table-felt-during-a-move/a-close-up-of-a-game-in-progress-on-a-bi-1.webp)


- **Woolen felt** is the most common type found in home game rooms. It's thicker and more forgiving, but it pills and stretches more easily. Woolen felt can often survive a move intact if handled carefully.
- **Worsted cloth** (like Simonis 860 or 760) is thinner, faster, and more expensive. It's also more delicate. Worsted cloth doesn't pill, but it tears more easily if snagged and shows creases more readily.

Knowing which type you have helps your movers decide whether to remove it or leave it attached during disassembly.

## When to Remove the Felt

Our [**Pool Table Moving**](/pool-table-moving) team recommends removing the felt in most cases, especially for:

- Long-distance moves where the slate will be in transit for hours
- Moves that require storage between pickup and delivery
- Tables with felt that is already showing wear or loose spots
- Any move during Miami's rainy season (June through November), when humidity peaks above 80%

If the felt is newer and in excellent condition, and the move is short and local, we can sometimes leave it attached to the slate and add extra protective layers. But removing it is almost always the safer choice.

## Proper Felt Removal Technique

Removing pool table felt without damaging it requires patience. The cloth is typically held in place by staples along the underside of the slate. Our team uses flat-head staple pullers rather than pliers, which can snag and tear the fabric. Each staple comes out individually with steady, even pressure.

![A Man Aligning Billiard Balls In A Rack  2](/images/blog/2024/12/how-to-protect-pool-table-felt-during-a-move/a-man-aligning-billiard-balls-in-a-rack--2.webp)


Once the staples are out, we roll the felt around a cardboard tube, never fold it. Folding creates sharp creases that affect ball roll and are nearly impossible to smooth out. The rolled felt goes into a breathable fabric bag or is wrapped in clean cotton sheeting. Plastic wrapping is a bad idea in Miami's heat because it traps moisture and can cause mold growth during even a short transit.

## Protecting Felt That Stays On

If we determine the felt can safely stay attached during a local move, we take these precautions:

- **Cover the surface** with a clean cotton sheet, then a layer of moving blankets secured with painter's tape (never duct tape, which leaves residue)
- **Wrap the entire slate** section in stretch wrap over the blankets to create a moisture barrier
- **Stand the slate upright** during transport so nothing presses against the playing surface
- **Keep climate control running** in the truck if available, or schedule the move during cooler morning hours

## After the Move: Inspecting Your Felt

Once the table is reassembled, check the felt carefully before your first game. Run your hand across the entire surface and feel for:

![Close Up Of Billiard Balls And Cue Stick 4](/images/blog/2024/12/how-to-protect-pool-table-felt-during-a-move/close-up-of-billiard-balls-and-cue-stick-4.webp)


- Lumps or ridges near the staple lines
- Loose spots where the cloth has stretched
- Damp patches that indicate moisture exposure
- Small tears or snags, especially near the pockets

Minor stretching sometimes tightens up after a day or two in an air-conditioned room. But if you notice tears, persistent wrinkles, or any mildew smell, re-felting is the right call.
