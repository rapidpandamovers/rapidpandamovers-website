Every culture has its own ideas about how to start life in a new home on the right foot. Some of these traditions go back centuries. Others are still practiced daily in communities across Miami-Dade County.

Here are six moving-day traditions from around the world that people follow for good luck, and the stories behind them.

## 1. Let the Cat Enter First (Russia)

In Russian tradition, a cat should be the first living creature to step inside a new home. The belief is that cats can sense negative energy and will absorb it, making the space safe for the family. Some families even borrow a neighbor's cat if they do not own one.

![A Family Unpacking Boxes In Their New Ap 4](/images/blog/2029/07/6-global-traditions-for-good-luck-when-moving/a-family-unpacking-boxes-in-their-new-ap-4.webp)


The tradition has roots in Slavic folklore, where cats were considered protectors of the home. In modern Russia, many families still follow this custom, even if they laugh about it afterward.

## 2. Scatter Coins Across the Floor (Philippines)

When Filipino families move into a new home, they toss coins across the living room floor on the first day. The coins represent prosperity and are meant to attract wealth to the household. Some families also place coins in the corners of each room before arranging furniture.

This tradition is often combined with moving in on a day when the moon is waxing (growing larger), which is believed to symbolize increasing fortune.

## 3. Enter with Bread and Honey (Turkey)

Turkish housewarming tradition calls for entering a new home carrying bread, salt, and honey. Bread ensures the family will never go hungry. Salt adds flavor to life and wards off bad energy. Honey brings sweetness and harmony to the household.

Neighbors often bring these items as housewarming gifts, which is why Turkish housewarming parties are rarely short on food.

## 4. Smudge with Sage or Palo Santo (Indigenous Americas)

Many Indigenous cultures in North and South America practice smudging, which involves burning dried sage, cedar, or palo santo and wafting the smoke through every room of a new home. The smoke is believed to clear out stagnant or negative energy left behind by previous occupants.

![A Joyful Couple Unpacks Boxes In Their N 1](/images/blog/2029/07/6-global-traditions-for-good-luck-when-moving/a-joyful-couple-unpacks-boxes-in-their-n-1.webp)


This practice has spread well beyond Indigenous communities. In Miami, it is common to see new residents smudging their homes in neighborhoods from Coconut Grove to Wynwood, regardless of cultural background.

## 5. Paint the Front Door Red (China)

In Chinese feng shui, a red front door invites good fortune and positive energy into the home. Red symbolizes luck, happiness, and prosperity. Many Chinese families will repaint the front door before moving a single piece of furniture inside.

The positioning of the door also matters in feng shui. A door facing south is considered the most auspicious, while the color red amplifies the good energy regardless of direction.

## 6. Break a Pomegranate on the Threshold (Greece)

Greek families celebrate moving into a new home by smashing a pomegranate on the front doorstep. The seeds represent fertility, abundance, and good fortune. The more seeds that scatter, the more luck the household will have.

This tradition is also common during Greek New Year celebrations, and many Greek-American families in South Florida keep the practice alive when moving into a new home.
