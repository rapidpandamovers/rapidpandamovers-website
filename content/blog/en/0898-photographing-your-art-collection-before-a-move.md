# Photographing Your Art Collection Before a Move

Imagine this: a painting arrives at your new home with a scratch across the lower left corner. Was it there before the move? Without photos, you have no way to prove it wasn't, and your insurance claim goes nowhere. Pre-move photography is one of the simplest, most overlooked steps in protecting an art collection during a relocation. Our team recommends it for every client, whether they're moving a handful of framed prints from a Brickell apartment or a full gallery collection from Coral Gables.

## Why Pre-Move Photos Matter

Condition documentation serves three purposes. It creates a record for insurance claims if damage occurs during transit. It provides a reference for proper reinstallation at the destination, showing exactly how each piece looked and hung. And it gives you and your moving team a shared understanding of existing condition issues so no one gets blamed for pre-existing wear.

![Art Gallery With Classical Statues And P 2](/images/blog/2027/10/photographing-your-art-collection-before-a-move/art-gallery-with-classical-statues-and-p-2.webp)


Professional art handlers call this a "condition report." Museums create them every time a work changes hands. You should, too.

## Equipment You Need

You don't need a professional camera. A modern smartphone with a decent camera works fine, as long as you follow a few rules. Make sure your lens is clean. Shoot in the highest resolution available. Turn off any beauty or HDR filters that might smooth out details you need to capture, like hairline cracks or texture variations.

You will need good lighting. Natural daylight from a window works best. Avoid shooting under mixed lighting (like a room with both overhead fluorescents and a table lamp), which creates uneven color. If you're photographing glossy or glass-covered pieces, angle yourself to avoid capturing your own reflection or a bright light glare.

A ruler, yardstick, or color reference card placed next to the piece in at least one photo gives scale and color accuracy for the record.

## What to Photograph

For each piece, capture the following:

![Grayscale Image Of A Classic Sculpture I 3](/images/blog/2027/10/photographing-your-art-collection-before-a-move/grayscale-image-of-a-classic-sculpture-i-3.webp)


**Full front view**: The complete face of the artwork, shot straight on, filling as much of the frame as possible. Avoid shooting at an angle.

**Full back view**: The reverse side showing the stretcher bars, backing, hanging hardware, labels, and any markings. This also documents the structural condition.

**All four edges**: Shoot the top, bottom, left, and right edges to capture frame condition, corner joints, and any chipping or wear.

**Close-ups of damage**: Zoom in on any scratches, dents, cracks, chips, discoloration, water stains, or repairs. Get close enough that the detail fills the photo.

**Signature and labels**: Artist signatures, gallery labels, framer stickers, and any provenance markings on the back should all be documented individually.

**Installation context**: A wide shot showing the piece hanging on the wall captures the surrounding environment and mounting method.

## Organizing Your Photos

Name each photo file or folder with the artist name, title, and a description of the view. Something like "Smith_Sunset_front.jpg" and "Smith_Sunset_back.jpg" keeps things clear when you're reviewing hundreds of images.

Create a master spreadsheet or document that pairs each piece with its photo set and notes on condition. Store everything in the cloud, Google Drive, Dropbox, or iCloud, so it's accessible from any device and safe from loss.

## Sharing With Your Moving Team

Send the photo inventory to your moving company before the move. This lets the crew know which pieces have pre-existing issues and where to take extra care. It also creates a shared reference point if questions arise at delivery.
