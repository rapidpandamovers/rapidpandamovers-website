# Reconnecting Your Hot Tub After a Move

The hot tub is on the pad at your new Miami home. The movers have left. Now comes the part that determines whether you're soaking by evening or troubleshooting for the next week: reconnection. Getting the electrical, plumbing, and water chemistry right after a move requires some patience and attention to detail.

## Step 1: Verify the Pad and Placement

Before reconnecting anything, confirm the tub is sitting level and properly positioned. Use a 4-foot level across the top rim in multiple directions. If the tub is off by more than a quarter inch, it needs to be shimmed or the pad needs adjustment. An unlevel tub puts stress on the shell, causes uneven water lines, and can make the cover fit poorly.

![Adult Man Enjoying Relaxation In A Woode 3](/images/blog/2027/01/reconnecting-your-hot-tub-after-a-move/adult-man-enjoying-relaxation-in-a-woode-3.webp)


Also check that the tub is positioned correctly relative to the electrical disconnect box. Per code in Miami-Dade County, the disconnect must be within line of sight of the tub and at least 5 feet from the water's edge. If the tub placement doesn't work with the electrical layout, now is the time to adjust, not after everything is connected.

## Step 2: Electrical Reconnection

This is a job for a licensed electrician, full stop. A 240V, 50-amp spa circuit carries enough power to be lethal if handled incorrectly. Your electrician will:

- Connect the wiring from the disconnect box to the tub's control panel
- Verify proper wire gauge and connections
- Test the GFCI protection (press the test button, confirm power trips, reset)
- Verify voltage at the tub's control board
- Turn on the breaker and confirm the tub powers up

If your new home already had a hot tub circuit from a previous owner, the electrician should still inspect it before connecting your tub. Old wiring, undersized circuits, and faulty GFCI breakers are common in older Miami homes.

## Step 3: Inspect for Damage

Before filling with water, do a thorough visual inspection:

- Check the acrylic shell for any new cracks, chips, or scratches from transport
- Inspect all plumbing connections inside the equipment compartment for loose fittings
- Look at the pump, heater, and control board for any visible damage or loose wires
- Check the drain valve to make sure it's closed and sealed

If you spot damage, document it with photos immediately. If the moving company is responsible, having documentation from the moment of discovery strengthens any claim.

## Step 4: Fill the Tub

Use a garden hose to fill the tub through the filter housing, not directly into the shell. Filling through the filter compartment forces water through the internal plumbing first, which helps push any remaining air out of the lines and reduces the risk of an airlock in the pump.

Fill to the recommended level marked on the tub's skimmer or indicated in your owner's manual. In most Miami homes, filling a 400-gallon tub takes about 2 hours with standard water pressure.

While the tub fills, watch for leaks. Check around the pump, heater, unions, and the drain valve. Small drips from plumbing connections are common after a move, as fittings can loosen during transport. Most can be tightened by hand or with a wrench.

## Step 5: Prime the Pump

After the tub is filled, the pump may have an air lock, which means air is trapped in the pump housing preventing it from circulating water. Signs of an airlock include the pump motor running but no water flowing through the jets.

![Shirtless Man Smiling In An Outdoor Hot  1](/images/blog/2027/01/reconnecting-your-hot-tub-after-a-move/shirtless-man-smiling-in-an-outdoor-hot--1.webp)


To fix it:

- Turn on the tub's main power
- Open all jet valves to the fully open position
- Loosen (don't remove) the pump union fitting slightly until you hear air hissing out and water starts seeping
- Once water flows steadily, retighten the fitting
- Run the jets and verify water is circulating

If the pump won't prime after several attempts, there may be a leak or a blocked line that needs professional attention.

## Step 6: Heat the Water

Turn the thermostat to your preferred temperature. In Miami, most people keep their tubs between 98 and 102 degrees. Heating from ambient tap water temperature (which runs 75-80 degrees in South Florida) takes 6 to 12 hours depending on the tub size and heater power.

Let the tub run through a full heating cycle before getting in. This gives the heater, pumps, and circulation system time to warm up and settle after the move.

## Step 7: Balance the Water Chemistry

Once the water is up to temperature, test and adjust the chemistry:

- **pH**: Target 7.2 to 7.8
- **Alkalinity**: Target 80 to 120 ppm
- **Sanitizer (chlorine or bromine)**: Follow your preferred system's guidelines
- **Calcium hardness**: Target 150 to 250 ppm (Miami's tap water tends to run on the harder side)

Run the jets for 15 minutes after adding chemicals to distribute them evenly.
