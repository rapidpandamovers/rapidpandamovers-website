# Moving Built-In Appliances: What You Need to Know

Built-in appliances are designed to look like part of the cabinetry. That's great for aesthetics, but it makes moving them a different job entirely compared to freestanding units. A built-in refrigerator is bolted to the wall, recessed into a custom cabinet opening, and connected to plumbing and electrical behind the panel. A wall oven sits inside a cabinet cutout with wiring running through the wall. Pulling these out without a plan damages the cabinetry, the appliance, and sometimes the wall itself. In Miami's high-end homes, from waterfront properties in Coconut Grove to renovated estates in Coral Gables, built-in appliances are the norm, and moving them requires specific knowledge.

## Why Built-Ins Are Harder to Move

Freestanding appliances sit on the floor, plug into an outlet, and roll out. Built-in appliances are physically attached to your kitchen. They're secured with mounting screws, brackets, and sometimes custom trim pieces that integrate them with surrounding cabinetry. The connections (water, gas, electrical) often run through the wall or floor rather than being accessible from behind the appliance.

Removing a built-in wrong means stripping screws from expensive wood cabinetry, cracking granite or quartz countertops above, or pulling electrical wiring that's not up to current code. It also means the cabinet opening is left exposed, which matters if you're selling the home.

## Types of Built-In Appliances and How They're Installed

### Built-In Refrigerators

![A Young Boy Focuses On Creative Work At  1](/images/blog/2028/08/moving-built-in-appliances-what-you-need-to-know/a-young-boy-focuses-on-creative-work-at--1.webp)


Brands like Sub-Zero, Thermador, and Miele make refrigerators designed to sit flush with 24-inch-deep cabinets. Custom panels match the surrounding cabinetry. These units are typically 84 inches tall and anchored to the wall or cabinet frame with brackets at the top. The water line for the ice maker runs through the floor or wall.

To remove one, you need to detach the custom panels (if you want to keep them), disconnect the water line at the shutoff valve, unplug the unit, remove the mounting brackets, and carefully slide it out of the cabinet opening. The unit alone weighs 400-600 pounds, so this isn't a two-person job without equipment.

### Wall Ovens

Single and double wall ovens are mounted inside a tall cabinet with screws through the side flanges. A 240-volt electrical connection runs through the wall behind the oven. Removing the oven means turning off the breaker, disconnecting the wiring, removing the mounting screws, and carefully sliding the oven out on its rails. Wall ovens weigh 100-200 pounds and the mounting position (usually at chest or eye height) makes lifting them awkward.

### Built-In Cooktops

Gas and electric cooktops drop into a countertop cutout. They're held in place by clips underneath the counter surface. A gas cooktop has a gas line running from below, and an electric cooktop has a dedicated circuit. Removing a cooktop means disconnecting the gas or electric from below, releasing the mounting clips, and lifting the unit straight up out of the counter. The risk here is damaging the countertop, especially if it's granite or marble.

### Built-In Microwaves and Coffee Makers

These smaller built-ins sit in purpose-cut cabinet openings with trim kits that hide the gaps. Removing them is straightforward: pull the trim kit, remove mounting screws, disconnect the power, and slide the unit out. The trim kits are model-specific and should be kept for reinstallation.

### Panel-Ready Dishwashers

These dishwashers have a custom cabinet panel on the front that matches your kitchen. They're screwed to the underside of the countertop and connected to water and drain lines under the sink. Removing one requires disconnecting the plumbing, removing the countertop screws, and sliding it out. Keep track of the mounting hardware since it varies by brand.

## Steps for a Successful Built-In Appliance Move

### Document Everything

![High Tech Washing Machines In A Los Ange 4](/images/blog/2028/08/moving-built-in-appliances-what-you-need-to-know/high-tech-washing-machines-in-a-los-ange-4.webp)


Photograph each appliance from multiple angles before touching anything. Capture the trim pieces, mounting brackets, panel attachments, and all visible connections. These photos are your reference for reinstallation at the new home.

### Gather the Right Tools

You'll need a socket set, screwdrivers (Phillips and flathead), an adjustable wrench, wire nuts, electrical tape, a voltage tester, and zip-lock bags labeled for each appliance's hardware.

### Turn Off Utilities

Kill the circuit breaker for each appliance. Turn off water supply valves. Shut off gas valves. Verify everything is actually off before you start disconnecting.

### Remove Trim and Panels

Carefully detach custom panels and trim kits. Label each piece with painter's tape noting which appliance and which side it came from. Wrap panels in moving blankets to protect the finish.

### Disconnect and Remove

With utilities off and trim removed, disconnect the appliance from its supply lines and mounting hardware. Slide the appliance out slowly, watching for snagged wires or hoses. Have help positioned to receive the weight.
