Miami's Health District, also called Civic Center, holds the second-largest concentration of medical and research facilities in the United States, behind only Houston's Texas Medical Center. If you work in healthcare, research, or public administration, this neighborhood puts you within walking distance of your workplace. Here is what you need to know before moving here.

## Why Choose the Health District?

The Health District is not your typical residential neighborhood. It is built around a cluster of major institutions:

![Emergency Entrance At A Hospital In Sahi 3](/images/blog/2029/07/relocating-to-health-district-in-miami-your-complete-guide/emergency-entrance-at-a-hospital-in-sahi-3.webp)


- **Jackson Memorial Hospital**: Miami's largest public hospital and one of the biggest in the country
- **University of Miami Miller School of Medicine**: A top-tier medical school and research center
- **Bascom Palmer Eye Institute**: Consistently ranked the number one eye hospital in the United States
- **Holtz Children's Hospital**: One of the largest pediatric hospitals in the Southeast
- **Miami VA Medical Center**: Serving veterans across South Florida
- **Miami-Dade County government offices**: The Stephen P. Clark Government Center is located here

For healthcare professionals, medical students, and researchers, living in the Health District means a short commute measured in minutes rather than miles.

### Location and Accessibility

The Health District sits roughly between NW 20th Street to the north and the Dolphin Expressway (SR 836) to the south, with NW 14th Avenue to the west and I-95/the Midtown Interchange to the east. It borders Overtown to the east and Allapattah to the north and west.

Transit access is a major advantage:

- **Civic Center Metrorail Station** connects you to downtown, Brickell, the airport, and points north and south
- **Multiple Metrobus routes** run through the area along NW 12th Avenue and NW 7th Street
- **I-95 and SR 836** provide direct highway access for drivers
- **Miami International Airport** is about 10 minutes west

### Who Lives Here

The neighborhood's population is relatively small at around 3,100 residents, giving it a quieter feel compared to nearby areas like Downtown or Midtown. Most residents are healthcare workers, medical students, government employees, and support staff who value the short commute. The area also attracts investors interested in the ongoing development around the medical campus.

## What Makes the Health District Different

Unlike neighborhoods defined by restaurants and nightlife, the Health District runs on institutional activity. The streets are busiest during shift changes and weekday business hours. That said, the area is changing. New mixed-use and residential developments are adding retail, dining, and housing options that did not exist a few years ago.

### Living Options

Housing in the Health District tends to be more affordable than nearby Downtown or Midtown:

- **New residential towers**: Several new developments are adding modern condo and apartment inventory, some within walking distance of the Civic Center Metrorail station
- **Older apartments and duplexes**: More affordable options along NW 12th and 14th Avenues
- **Nearby neighborhoods**: Many Health District workers live in adjacent Allapattah (increasingly popular with younger residents) or Overtown and commute on foot or by transit

### Cost of Living

The Health District generally offers lower rents and purchase prices per square foot than Downtown, Brickell, or Midtown. New construction is changing that in some pockets, but the neighborhood remains one of the more affordable options for living close to central Miami. Medical students and residents in particular benefit from the combination of affordability and proximity to the hospital campus.

## Moving to the Health District: What to Plan For

The Health District has some specific moving challenges worth knowing about:

![Rear View Of A Healthcare Professional I 1](/images/blog/2029/07/relocating-to-health-district-in-miami-your-complete-guide/rear-view-of-a-healthcare-professional-i-1.webp)


### Parking and Street Access

Street parking near the hospitals is heavily regulated with meters, time limits, and restricted zones. On moving day:

- Coordinate loading zones with your moving company in advance
- Avoid scheduling your move during weekday morning rush (7-9 AM) when hospital shift changes create heavy traffic
- NW 12th Avenue and NW 7th Street can get congested during peak hours

### Building Access

If you are moving into a newer condo or apartment building, expect standard requirements like freight elevator reservations and loading dock scheduling. For older buildings, access may be more straightforward but plan for tighter hallways and no elevator.

### Weather Considerations

Summer moves in South Florida mean daily afternoon thunderstorms. Schedule loading and unloading for early morning. Keep important documents and electronics in waterproof containers.

## Essential Local Resources

As a new Health District resident, here is what is nearby:

- **Groceries**: Sedano's and President Supermarket are the closest options, with a Publix in Midtown a short drive north
- **Dining**: Limited sit-down options within the district itself, but Allapattah and Midtown are minutes away with growing restaurant scenes
- **Healthcare**: You are surrounded by it. Jackson Memorial, UM Health, and Bascom Palmer are all within the neighborhood
- **Transit**: Civic Center Metrorail station plus multiple bus routes
- **Education**: Miami Dade College Medical Campus is in the district, and several public schools serve the area

## FAQ

**Is the Health District a good neighborhood to live in?**
It depends on your priorities. If you work at one of the hospitals or institutions in the area, the short commute and transit access are hard to beat. The neighborhood is still more institutional than residential, but new development is adding amenities and housing.

![The Prince Of Wales Hospital Entrance Wi 4](/images/blog/2029/07/relocating-to-health-district-in-miami-your-complete-guide/the-prince-of-wales-hospital-entrance-wi-4.webp)


**Is the Health District safe?**
The area around the hospital campus and Metrorail station sees steady foot traffic during the day. As with any urban neighborhood in Miami, stay aware of your surroundings, especially after dark. The ongoing development and increased residential population are improving conditions.

**What is the difference between Health District and Civic Center?**
They are the same neighborhood. "Civic Center" is the older name still used for the Metrorail station and some official maps. "Health District" reflects the area's medical campus identity and is commonly used in real estate and development.

**How much is rent in the Health District?**
Rents are generally lower than Downtown or Brickell. Expect roughly $1,500 to $2,500 per month for a one-bedroom in newer construction, with older apartments available for less.
