Miami's December architecture tour starts here. From Art Deco gems to Mediterranean Revival mansions, these are the most fascinating properties that have shaped our city through decades of transformation.

## 1. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens

This 1916 Italian Renaissance-style estate sits on the waterfront in Coconut Grove, just south of Brickell. Built by industrialist James Deering, the mansion features 34 decorated rooms filled with European antiques. The grounds have survived multiple hurricanes, including Andrew in 1992, and the estate has moved countless artifacts and artworks over its century of operation.

## 2. The Freedom Tower

Located in Downtown Miami at 600 Biscayne Boulevard, this 1925 Mediterranean Revival tower originally served as the headquarters for The Miami News. Known as "Miami's Ellis Island," the building processed over 650,000 Cuban refugees between 1962 and 1974. Today it stands as a National Historic Landmark and houses art exhibitions at Miami Dade College.

## 3. The Biltmore Hotel

Rising 315 feet above Coral Gables, this 1926 landmark on Anastasia Avenue has served as a military hospital during World War II, a VA hospital, and later a University of Miami campus building. Its 27-story tower, modeled after the Giralda in Seville, Spain, remains the tallest building in Coral Gables. Each transition through the decades required massive moves of furniture, equipment, and supplies.

## 4. Art Deco Historic District

Stretching along Ocean Drive, Collins Avenue, and Washington Avenue in South Beach, this district contains over 800 buildings from the 1930s and 40s. The pastel-colored hotels and apartments survived the threat of demolition in the 1970s thanks to preservation efforts led by Barbara Capitman. Today, these buildings house thousands of residents and visitors, with the humid salt air requiring constant maintenance and careful climate control during moves.

## 5. Coral Gables City Hall

Built in 1928 on Biltmore Way, this Mediterranean Revival building sits at the heart of George Merrick's planned community. The semi-circular colonnade and bell tower reflect the Spanish influence Merrick envisioned for "The City Beautiful." The surrounding Miracle Mile shopping district and tree-lined streets have witnessed generations of families moving into the area's historic homes.
