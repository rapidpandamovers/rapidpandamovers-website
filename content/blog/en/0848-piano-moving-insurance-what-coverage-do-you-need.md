# Piano Moving Insurance: What Coverage Do You Need

Your piano might be worth $5,000 or $50,000. Either way, a single accident during a move could destroy it. Insurance is the safety net that keeps a moving mishap from becoming a financial catastrophe. But not all moving insurance is the same, and the default coverage most movers offer won't come close to covering a piano's actual value. Here's how the different types work and what you should look for.

## The Two Types of Moving Coverage

Federal regulations require all licensed movers to offer two levels of liability coverage. Understanding the difference is where most piano owners get caught off guard.

![A Detailed Close Up Of Piano Keys With A 1](/images/blog/2027/11/piano-moving-insurance-what-coverage-do-you-need/a-detailed-close-up-of-piano-keys-with-a-1.webp)


### Released Value Protection (The Default)

This is the basic, no-cost coverage that comes standard with every move. It covers your belongings at $0.60 per pound. For a 600-pound baby grand piano worth $15,000, that means the mover is liable for exactly $360 if they destroy it.

Released value protection exists because regulations require movers to offer something. It is not real insurance. It's a liability limitation that heavily favors the moving company. If you sign off on released value protection for a piano move, you're accepting that risk.

### Full Value Protection

Full value protection means the mover is responsible for the current market value or repair cost of any item they damage. If your piano is worth $15,000 and it gets dropped, the mover either repairs it, replaces it with a comparable instrument, or pays you the appraised value.

This coverage comes with a deductible, usually between $250 and $500, and costs more than the default option. The price varies based on the declared value of the items being moved. For a piano-only move, you'll declare the piano's value, and the premium is calculated from there.

## What Professional Piano Movers Cover

Reputable [**Piano Moving**](/piano-moving) companies carry their own insurance policies beyond the minimum federal requirements. When evaluating a piano mover, ask about:

### Cargo Insurance

This covers the items being transported. A good piano mover carries cargo insurance with limits high enough to cover concert-quality instruments. Ask for the policy limit, not just confirmation that they have insurance.

### General Liability

This covers damage to property other than the piano. If the crew scratches your hardwood floor, dents your wall going through a doorway, or cracks a marble threshold in your Miami condo, general liability pays for the repair.

### Workers' Compensation

If a crew member gets injured moving your piano, workers' comp covers their medical costs. Without it, you could face liability claims from an injured worker on your property.

## What to Ask Your Piano Mover

Before booking, get answers to these questions:

- What is your cargo insurance limit per item?
- What is the deductible on full value protection?
- Can you provide a certificate of insurance before the move?
- Does your coverage include damage from climate exposure (heat, humidity, rain)?
- What is the claims process if damage occurs?

A legitimate mover will provide clear answers and documentation. If a company hesitates or gets vague about insurance, that's a red flag.

## Your Homeowner's Insurance

Your homeowner's or renter's insurance policy may also cover your piano during a move, but don't assume it does. Many policies exclude or limit coverage for items being transported by third parties. Some policies require a specific rider or endorsement for high-value items like pianos.

Call your insurance agent before the move and ask:

- Is my piano covered during transport?
- Is there a sub-limit for musical instruments?
- Do I need a scheduled personal property endorsement?
- Does coverage apply if a third-party mover is handling the transport?

## Getting an Appraisal

For any piano worth more than $5,000, get a written appraisal before the move. A piano technician or certified appraiser can provide a current market value assessment. This document becomes your proof of value if you need to file a claim. Without it, you're negotiating from a weak position.

Keep the appraisal, along with any receipts, photos of the piano's condition before the move, and the mover's insurance documentation, in a file you bring with you on moving day. Don't pack it in a box.

## Miami-Specific Insurance Considerations

High-rise buildings in Brickell, Downtown, and the Beaches typically require movers to provide a certificate of insurance listing the building as an additional insured. This is standard, and professional piano movers carry certificates ready to go. If your mover doesn't know what a COI is, find a different mover.
