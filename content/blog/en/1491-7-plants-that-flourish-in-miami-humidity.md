Miami's year-round humidity, which typically hovers between 60 and 80 percent, creates ideal growing conditions for tropical houseplants. If you just moved into a new home or apartment, adding a few plants is one of the fastest ways to make a space feel settled. These seven varieties do well indoors across South Florida without demanding a green thumb.

## 1. Pothos (Epipremnum aureum)

Pothos is the go-to starter plant for a reason. It tolerates low light, inconsistent watering, and air-conditioned rooms. In Miami's humidity, it grows quickly and produces long trailing vines that look great on shelves or hanging planters. The golden pothos variety has yellow-green variegation, while neon pothos offers bright chartreuse leaves. Water when the top inch of soil feels dry, roughly once a week in most Miami homes.

## 2. Monstera Deliciosa

The Swiss cheese plant is a South Florida favorite. Its large, fenestrated leaves develop more splits as the plant matures, and Miami's humidity helps those leaves grow bigger and healthier than in drier climates. Place it near a window with bright, indirect light. Water every one to two weeks and let the soil dry out slightly between waterings. A mature monstera can reach six to eight feet indoors, so give it room.

## 3. Bird of Paradise (Strelitzia reginae)

This plant brings a bold, architectural look to any room. In South Florida, bird of paradise grows outdoors year-round, but it also does well inside if placed near a sunny window. It prefers bright, direct or indirect light for at least six hours a day. Water when the top two inches of soil are dry. Indoors, it likely will not flower, but the tall, banana-like leaves make a strong visual statement on their own.

## 4. Bromeliad (Guzmania, Aechmea, and Neoregelia varieties)

Bromeliads are native to tropical America, and many popular varieties used in South Florida landscapes originally come from Brazil. They thrive in Miami's humidity and add color with their long-lasting flower spikes, which can persist for weeks to months. Bromeliads absorb water through their central cup and leaves rather than primarily through their roots, so keep the cup filled with fresh water and mist the leaves occasionally. They do well in bright, indirect light and are low-maintenance once established.

## 5. Snake Plant (Dracaena trifasciata)

Formerly classified as Sansevieria, the snake plant handles neglect better than almost any other houseplant. It tolerates low light, infrequent watering, and the temperature swings that come with opening and closing windows in Miami. Water every two to three weeks and make sure the pot has drainage -- overwatering is the main way people kill this plant. The upright, sword-shaped leaves work well in corners, entryways, and bedrooms.

## 6. Peace Lily (Spathiphyllum)

Peace lilies love humidity, which makes them a natural fit for South Florida bathrooms, kitchens, and living rooms. They produce white spathes (the flower-like structures) several times a year and are one of the few flowering houseplants that do well in low to medium light. The plant will droop slightly when it needs water, giving you a clear signal. Keep it away from direct sunlight, which can scorch the leaves.

## 7. Fiddle Leaf Fig (Ficus lyrata)

Fiddle leaf figs have a reputation for being finicky, but Miami's humidity solves the biggest challenge most owners face: dry air. These plants prefer humidity levels around 50 to 60 percent, which South Florida provides naturally. Place your fiddle leaf fig in a spot with bright, indirect light and keep it away from air conditioning vents, which dry out the leaves. Water when the top two inches of soil feel dry, typically every 7 to 10 days. The large, violin-shaped leaves make this one of the most visually striking indoor plants you can own.

## Where to Buy in Miami-Dade

Local nurseries carry specimens already acclimated to South Florida conditions, which gives them a better survival rate than plants shipped from out of state:

- **Fairchild Tropical Botanic Garden** -- Hosts plant sales throughout the year in Coral Gables
- **Costa Farms** -- A wholesale grower based in Homestead that supplies many local retailers
- **Pinecrest Gardens** -- Saturday morning plant market with local vendors
- **Living Color Garden Center** -- Located in Fort Lauderdale, worth the drive for their tropical selection

Big-box stores also stock most of these varieties, though the plants may not be locally grown.

## Moving with Plants

If you are relocating within Miami-Dade, most houseplants travel fine in a car with the air conditioning on. For professional moves, let your moving crew know about any large or heavy planters so they can plan accordingly. Keep plants out of the moving truck if possible, since temperatures inside a closed truck can spike quickly in Florida.

For longer moves, check state agricultural regulations. Florida has restrictions on certain plants entering and leaving the state.

## FAQ

**Can movers transport my plants?**
Most moving companies, including ours, will transport potted plants on local moves. For long-distance moves, plants are typically excluded from coverage, so it is best to transport them yourself in your car.

**Which of these plants is best for a dark apartment?**
Pothos and snake plants tolerate the lowest light levels. Peace lilies also do well in medium-low light. If your apartment gets very little natural light, start with one of those three.

**Do indoor plants attract bugs in Miami?**
Overwatered soil can attract fungus gnats, which are common in humid climates. Let the top layer of soil dry between waterings and avoid leaving standing water in saucers to prevent this.
