# How to Create an Inventory for Stored Items

Six months after putting thirty boxes into a storage unit, you need your winter jacket. Which box is it in? You don't remember, and neither does anyone else. Without an inventory, retrieving a single item means opening box after box in a warm storage unit until you find it. A simple tracking system prevents this and also gives you documentation for insurance purposes.

## Why Most People Skip Inventory (And Regret It)

On packing day, everyone is focused on getting items boxed up and loaded. Writing down the contents of each box feels like extra work you don't have time for. The problem shows up later:

![Interior View Of A Large Warehouse Aisle 2](/images/blog/2027/11/how-to-create-an-inventory-for-stored-items/interior-view-of-a-large-warehouse-aisle-2.webp)


- You can't find a specific item without opening multiple boxes.
- You forget what's in storage entirely, leading to duplicate purchases.
- If something is stolen or damaged, you can't prove what was there or its value.
- When you finally move out of storage, you waste time sorting through everything because nothing is labeled or tracked.

The 30 minutes it takes to create an inventory on packing day saves hours of frustration later.

## Choose Your Tracking Method

Pick one approach and stick with it:

**Spreadsheet (Google Sheets or Excel)**: Create columns for box number, room, contents, value, and location in the unit. This is the most searchable option since you can use Ctrl+F to find any item instantly.

**Notes app on your phone**: A numbered list with basic descriptions. Less organized than a spreadsheet but always accessible.

**Photo documentation**: Take a photo of the contents of each box before sealing it, then a photo of the sealed box with its number. Store photos in a dedicated album on your phone. This works well for visual items like decorations, artwork, and kitchenware where a description doesn't capture enough detail.

**Dedicated inventory apps**: Several free apps (Sortly, Memento, Home Inventory) let you catalog items with photos, categories, and estimated values. These work well if you want to track insurance values alongside locations.

## How to Number and Label Boxes

Use a consistent numbering system:

- **Sequential numbers** (1, 2, 3...) work for smaller storage units with fewer than 50 boxes.
- **Room-based codes** work better for larger inventories. K-1 through K-12 for kitchen boxes, BR-1 through BR-8 for bedroom, and so on.

Write the number on the top and at least two sides of every box using a thick marker. If you're using clear plastic bins, place a printed contents list inside the bin facing outward.

## What to Include in Each Entry

For each box or item, record:

- **Box/item number**: Matches the label on the physical box.
- **General category**: Kitchen, bedroom, office, seasonal, etc.
- **Contents summary**: Specific enough to be useful. "Dishes and glasses" is better than "kitchen stuff." "Blue Fiestaware dinner plates, wine glasses, coffee mugs" is even better.
- **Estimated value**: Helpful for insurance claims. You don't need exact figures; ballpark estimates work.
- **Condition notes**: "Mirror wrapped in bubble wrap" or "table legs removed, hardware in zip bag taped to underside."
- **Location in unit**: Front left, back right, top of stack. This makes targeted retrieval possible without moving everything.

## Map Your Unit Layout

Sketch a simple diagram of your storage unit showing where major items and box groups are positioned. Label zones: "Kitchen boxes - front right," "Bedroom furniture - back wall," "Holiday decorations - left side, middle." Keep this diagram with your inventory list.

![Interior View Of A Warehouse With Stacke 1](/images/blog/2027/11/how-to-create-an-inventory-for-stored-items/interior-view-of-a-warehouse-with-stacke-1.webp)


This becomes especially valuable in larger units (10x15 and above) where items in the back are out of sight from the door.

## Update as You Go

Every time you add or remove items from the unit, update your inventory. Cross off retrieved items. Add new ones with the next sequential number. This keeps the list accurate and prevents confusion when you can't remember if you already pulled something out.
