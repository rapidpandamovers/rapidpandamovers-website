Packing mistakes account for most moving damage claims. The good news? Every one of these errors is completely preventable. Whether you're preparing for a December move across Miami or relocating to a new state, avoiding these seven common packing errors will protect your belongings and save you money.

## 1. Overpacking Large Boxes

**The Mistake:** Filling a large box with books, tools, or heavy kitchen items until it weighs 80+ pounds.

![A Person Sealing A Cardboard Box With A  5](/images/blog/2027/11/7-common-packing-errors-and-how-to-avoid-them/a-person-sealing-a-cardboard-box-with-a--5.webp)


**Why It's Dangerous:**
- Boxes exceeding 50 pounds are nearly impossible to lift safely
- Heavy boxes cause back injuries for both homeowners and movers
- Overloaded boxes have bottoms that give out during transport
- Movers may refuse to handle dangerously heavy boxes

**The Fix:**
- Use small boxes (1.5 cubic feet) for heavy items like books, canned goods, and tools
- Reserve large boxes for lightweight items: pillows, linens, stuffed animals
- Target 30-40 pounds per box maximum
- Test lift every box before sealing

**Pro Tip:** If you struggle to lift a box comfortably at waist height, it's too heavy. Remove items until it's manageable.

## 2. Insufficient Cushioning

**The Mistake:** One layer of newspaper or a thin sheet of bubble wrap and calling it "protected."

**Why It Fails:**
- Moving trucks vibrate constantly on highways
- Even gentle braking causes items to shift
- Temperature changes in Florida can make materials more brittle
- A single bump can crack inadequately cushioned items

**The Professional Standard:**
- 3 inches of cushioning on the bottom of each box
- Individual wrapping for every fragile item
- Padding between all items (nothing touches anything else)
- Fill every gap with packing paper or foam peanuts
- 2-3 inches of cushioning on top before sealing

**The Test:** Gently shake the sealed box. If you hear movement or feel items shifting, open it and add more padding.

## 3. Leaving Hollow Items Empty

**The Mistake:** Packing vases, cups, bowls, and other hollow items without internal support.

**Why It Matters:**
- Hollow items are structurally weak at their thinnest points
- Impact force concentrates where there's no support
- Handles on mugs and vases are especially vulnerable
- Nested items can crack each other from inside pressure

**The Solution:**
- Fill every hollow item with crumpled packing paper
- Stuff the inside firmly but not so tight it creates outward pressure
- Wrap the exterior after filling the interior
- Use tissue paper for delicate painted or gilded items
- Pack handles facing inward, protected by extra padding

## 4. Using Old, Weak Boxes

**The Mistake:** Reusing boxes from years ago, or grabbing whatever free boxes you can find behind grocery stores.

**The Problems:**
- Cardboard loses 50% of its strength in high-humidity environments like Miami
- Old boxes may have weakened seams and compromised corners
- Previous contents (produce, liquids) may have damaged the integrity
- Mismatched boxes stack poorly and waste truck space

**The Smart Approach:**
- Purchase new boxes from a moving supply store or your moving company
- If reusing boxes, inspect every seam and corner for weakness
- Never use boxes that have been stored in garages or outdoor areas
- Reinforce box bottoms with extra tape in an H-pattern
- Avoid boxes that show water stains, soft spots, or mold

**December Consideration:** Holiday shopping creates a surplus of cardboard, but shipping boxes are designed for different purposes than moving boxes. Use purpose-built moving boxes for fragile items.

## 5. Improper Dish Packing

**The Mistake:** Stacking plates horizontally in a box, like they sit in your cabinet.

![Close Up Of Cardboard Boxes Stacked With 3](/images/blog/2027/11/7-common-packing-errors-and-how-to-avoid-them/close-up-of-cardboard-boxes-stacked-with-3.webp)


**Why Plates Break This Way:**
- Weight from upper plates concentrates on the bottom plate
- Horizontal stacking creates pressure points at the edges
- Even small chips spread into cracks under sustained weight
- The bottom plate in a stack is almost guaranteed to crack

**The Correct Method:**
- Pack plates vertically on their edges, like vinyl records
- Use cell dividers (dish packs) for maximum protection
- Wrap each plate individually in packing paper
- Place heavy plates at the bottom of the box, lighter ones on top
- Bowls go inside each other with paper between, then pack on edge
- Fill remaining space so nothing can shift

**The Investment:** Dish pack boxes with cell dividers cost more but reduce dish breakage by 90%+ compared to standard boxes.

## 6. Skipping the Label

**The Mistake:** Sealing boxes without marking contents, or writing vague labels like "Kitchen Stuff" or "Misc."

**The Consequences:**
- Movers can't identify fragile items for careful handling
- Boxes end up in wrong rooms, requiring extra lifting
- Unpacking takes three times longer as you search for essentials
- Important items stay buried in unmarked boxes for weeks
- Insurance claims become difficult without documented contents

**Effective Labeling:**
- Write on at least two sides of every box (so labels are visible however boxes are stacked)
- Include room destination: "KITCHEN" or "MASTER BEDROOM"
- Add brief contents: "Pots & Pans" or "Books - Fiction"
- Mark handling instructions: "FRAGILE" and "THIS SIDE UP"
- Number boxes and keep an inventory list for valuable items

## 7. Packing Too Early Without an Essentials Box

**The Mistake:** Getting so efficient that you pack everything, including items you'll need immediately after the move.

**What Gets Buried:**
- Phone chargers (first night frustration guaranteed)
- Toiletries and medications
- A change of clothes
- Toilet paper
- Basic cleaning supplies
- Important documents
- Tools to reassemble furniture
- Pet supplies

**The Solution:**
Pack a clearly labeled "OPEN FIRST" or "ESSENTIALS" box containing:
- Toilet paper and paper towels
- Basic toiletries (toothbrush, soap, shampoo)
- Medications (both daily and emergency)
- Phone and device chargers
- Change of clothes for each family member
- Snacks and bottled water
- Basic toolkit (screwdriver, pliers, utility knife)
- Flashlight
- Cleaning supplies (all-purpose cleaner, trash bags)
- Important documents (lease, IDs, moving paperwork)
- Pet food and supplies if applicable
- Bed linens (if arriving same day)

**Critical:** Load this box LAST so it comes off the truck FIRST.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many boxes will I need for my move?**
Plan for approximately 10 boxes per room for a standard home. Kitchens and garages typically need more. We can provide an accurate estimate during your free quote consultation.

**Should I pack liquids like cleaning supplies?**
Transport liquids in your personal vehicle when possible. If packing them, place bottles in sealed plastic bags, pack upright, and mark boxes clearly. Never pack flammable materials.

**How far in advance should I start packing?**
Begin 4-6 weeks before your move. Start with rarely-used items (storage areas, guest rooms, seasonal items) and work toward daily essentials in the final days.

**Can I leave clothes in dresser drawers?**
For local moves, light clothing can stay in drawers if the dresser is wrapped properly. For long-distance moves, remove all items to reduce weight and prevent shifting.

**What items shouldn't I pack myself?**
High-value art, antiques, wine collections, and irreplaceable items benefit from professional packing. We also recommend professional handling for large mirrors, chandeliers, and oversized electronics.
