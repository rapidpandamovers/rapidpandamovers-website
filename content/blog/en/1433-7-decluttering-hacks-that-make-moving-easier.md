A move is the one time you are forced to touch every item you own. That makes it the best opportunity to cut the dead weight, but most people run out of time and end up paying to haul things they should have left behind. These seven hacks target the areas where clutter hides deepest and moving costs pile up fastest.

## 1. The Trash Bag Sprint

Set a timer for 15 minutes, grab a large trash bag, and walk through your home collecting anything that is obviously garbage. Expired pantry items, old magazines, junk drawer contents, dried-out markers, mangled charging cables, and empty bottles. No decisions required -- if it belongs in a trash can, it goes in the bag.

![A Pile Of Empty Cardboard Boxes Arranged 4](/images/blog/2029/10/7-decluttering-hacks-that-make-moving-easier/a-pile-of-empty-cardboard-boxes-arranged-4.webp)


This sounds simple because it is. The point is momentum. Most people stall on decluttering because they start with hard decisions about sentimental items or furniture. The trash bag sprint builds early wins and clears visual clutter so you can see what actually needs sorting.

Do one sprint per day for the first week of your pre-move timeline and you will fill six or seven bags without a single difficult decision.

## 2. The Replacement Cost Test

For every item you are unsure about, look up what it would cost to replace. If the replacement cost is less than what it costs to move it, let it go. A bulky desk lamp from Target costs $25 new. Moving it takes box space, packing materials, and labor time that could easily exceed that number, especially on a long carry or a move with stairs.

This works particularly well for:
- Particle board furniture that does not survive disassembly well
- Small kitchen appliances you use once a year
- Outdated electronics collecting dust in a closet
- Books you have already read and will not reference again

The math gets even more decisive for moves within Miami-Dade where replacement stores are everywhere. Why pay to move a $15 ironing board across town when you can buy one at the new place?

## 3. The Reverse Packing Audit

Start packing your least-used rooms first, beginning six weeks before moving day. As you pack, you will naturally encounter items you forgot you owned. Anything that surprises you -- anything where your reaction is "oh, I still have this?" -- goes into a donate pile, not a box.

This is more effective than a dedicated decluttering session because you are already handling and evaluating every item as part of the packing process. You catch the hidden clutter that survives traditional sort-and-purge methods: the holiday decorations you replaced two years ago, the duplicate kitchen tools buried in a back cabinet, the hobby supplies for a hobby you quit.

## 4. The Photo and Measure Method

Photograph every piece of furniture and measure its footprint. Then pull up the floor plan of your new home and figure out where each piece will go. Anything without a clear destination gets sold or donated before moving day.

![Closeup Of Carton Box With Stripes On Wo 2](/images/blog/2029/10/7-decluttering-hacks-that-make-moving-easier/closeup-of-carton-box-with-stripes-on-wo-2.webp)


This prevents two expensive mistakes:
- Paying to move furniture that will not fit through the new doorway or down the new hallway
- Arriving at the new place with a couch, a table, or a dresser that has no room and ends up cluttering a garage

Condos and apartments in Brickell, Aventura, and Miami Beach often have smaller layouts than the homes people are leaving. Measuring first saves the cost of moving something you will just have to get rid of later anyway.

## 5. The Sell-By Deadline

Pick a date three weeks before your move and list everything you want to sell on Facebook Marketplace, OfferUp, or Craigslist by that date. Price to sell fast -- 50 percent of retail or less. Anything that has not sold one week before the move gets donated. Schedule the donation pickup for that date and do not reschedule it.

The deadline matters because without one, unsold items become "maybe I should keep it" items that end up on the truck. A hard cutoff eliminates that decision loop entirely.

In Miami-Dade, items sell fast on Marketplace if you price them right and offer porch pickup. Furniture, working appliances, and outdoor equipment move especially quickly in neighborhoods with high turnover like Doral, Kendall, and Homestead.

## 6. The Bathroom and Medicine Cabinet Sweep

This is the easiest room to declutter and the one most people skip. Pull everything out of your bathroom cabinets and check expiration dates. Medications, sunscreen, cosmetics, and skincare products all expire, and South Florida humidity accelerates degradation.

Most households throw away a full garbage bag of expired or near-empty products. Travel-size bottles from hotel stays, half-used shampoo you switched away from, prescription medications from two years ago -- none of it should go in a moving box.

For remaining items, consolidate. Pour two half-bottles of the same product into one. Combine cotton balls, bandages, and first-aid supplies into a single container. You will likely reduce three bathroom boxes down to one.

## 7. The Garage and Storage Unit Reckoning

If you have a garage, storage closet, or rented storage unit, that space probably holds the highest concentration of items you do not need. Storage areas become default dumping grounds for things you could not decide about during your last move or last cleaning spree.

Pull everything out and sort it into three groups:
- **Active use**: Items you use at least once a season (tools, sports equipment, seasonal decorations)
- **Archive**: Items with genuine future value (tax records, family heirlooms)
- **Dead weight**: Everything else

Be honest about the "archive" category. If you have boxes that have not been opened since your last move, the contents are almost certainly dead weight dressed up as archives. Open them, verify, and let go of what you do not need.

If you are paying for a separate storage unit, do the math on what those monthly fees add up to over a year. Often the items inside are worth less than the annual rent on the unit.

## How Much Can Decluttering Actually Save?

Moving companies calculate costs based on volume and time. Every box you eliminate means less truck space and fewer labor minutes. A typical Miami-Dade household that declutters seriously before a move reduces their load by 15 to 25 percent, which translates to roughly $200 to $500 in savings on local moves and significantly more on long-distance relocations.

Beyond the direct cost savings, fewer items means:
- Faster packing and unpacking
- Less chance of damage during transport
- A cleaner start at your new home
- Less post-move clutter to deal with

## FAQ

**How far in advance should I start decluttering before a move?**
Six weeks is ideal. That gives you time to sell items, schedule donations, and work through your home without rushing. If you are on a tighter timeline, focus on the trash bag sprint and the bathroom sweep first, since those deliver the fastest results.

**What should I do with items that are too big to donate but still usable?**
Large furniture and appliances can be listed on Facebook Marketplace or OfferUp for free pickup. In Miami-Dade, items listed for free usually disappear within 24 hours. You can also contact Habitat for Humanity ReStore or schedule a bulk pickup through your city's waste management department.

**Is it worth hiring a junk removal service before a move?**
If you have a large volume of items to discard, yes. A single junk removal trip can clear out a garage or storage unit in a few hours. The cost typically runs $150 to $400 depending on volume, which is often less than what you would spend moving those same items to a new location.
