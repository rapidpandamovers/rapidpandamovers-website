# Hanging Art in Your New Home After a Move

The boxes are inside, the furniture is placed, and now you're staring at bare walls with a stack of wrapped paintings leaning against the baseboard. Hanging art in a new space requires more thought than just finding a stud and driving a nail. Different walls, different light, and different room proportions all change how your collection should be displayed. Here's how to get it right in your new Miami home.

## Wait Before You Unwrap

Give your wrapped artwork at least 24 hours to acclimate to your new home's environment before removing the packing. If the AC hasn't been running in the new place, wait until the interior climate stabilizes. Rapid temperature and humidity shifts cause condensation on glass and canvas surfaces. In Miami, where outdoor humidity often exceeds 80%, this adjustment period matters more than in drier climates. Unwrapping too soon in a warm, humid room puts moisture right against the art.

## Plan Your Layout First

Before you put a single nail in the wall, map out where each piece will hang. Walk through the house and think about:

![Discover Classic Art In An Elegantly Des 2](/images/blog/2028/10/hanging-art-in-your-new-home-after-a-move/discover-classic-art-in-an-elegantly-des-2.webp)


**Lighting**: Avoid hanging valuable artwork in direct sunlight. South- and west-facing walls in Miami get intense afternoon sun that fades pigments over time. Oil paintings, watercolors, and photographs are all vulnerable. If a wall gets direct sun, consider UV-filtering glass or window treatments.

**Traffic flow**: Don't hang large pieces in narrow hallways where they'll get bumped by passing shoulders or moving furniture. High-traffic areas in Miami homes often include the path between the kitchen and the lanai.

**Viewing distance**: A large painting needs space in front of it for the viewer to stand back. Hanging a six-foot canvas in a small study doesn't give the piece room to breathe.

**Wall composition**: Think about groupings and relationships between pieces. A single large work anchoring a wall makes a different statement than a salon-style cluster of smaller pieces.

## Choosing the Right Hardware

The hardware depends on the wall material and the weight of the piece.

**Drywall**: Most Miami homes built after the 1970s have drywall interiors. For pieces under 20 pounds, a single picture hook rated for the weight works fine. For heavier pieces, find a stud with a stud finder and use a wood screw, or use toggle bolts rated for the weight in hollow drywall.

**Concrete block**: Common in older Miami homes and many condo buildings. You'll need a masonry bit, plastic anchors, and screws rated for block. Pre-drill the hole, tap in the anchor, and drive the screw. Don't skip the anchor. Screws alone pull straight out of concrete block.

**Plaster**: Some older homes in neighborhoods like Coral Gables and Miami Beach have plaster walls over lath. These crack easily if you drive a nail too aggressively. Use a small drill bit to create a pilot hole, then insert a picture hook.

For very heavy pieces, especially large framed mirrors or oversized canvases, use a French cleat system. This is a two-piece interlocking bracket that distributes the weight across a wider area of the wall and makes leveling easy.

## Getting the Height Right

The standard museum height places the center of the artwork at 57 inches from the floor, which approximates average eye level. This works well for most spaces. In rooms with high ceilings, common in many newer Miami homes and lofts, you can raise the center point slightly to 60 inches to avoid the piece looking low on a tall wall.

![Indoor Staircase In A Museum With Sculpt 4](/images/blog/2028/10/hanging-art-in-your-new-home-after-a-move/indoor-staircase-in-a-museum-with-sculpt-4.webp)


Above furniture like a sofa or console table, hang the piece so the bottom edge sits 6 to 8 inches above the furniture top. This creates a visual connection between the piece and the furniture below it.

## Leveling and Finishing Touches

Use a level, not your eye. Even a half-degree tilt is noticeable once you step back. For pieces hung on wire, the wire stretches slightly over time, so check the level again a week after hanging.

Once everything is hung, step back and look at the room as a whole. Adjust spacing between grouped pieces until the arrangement feels balanced. Take a photo from the doorway. Sometimes what looks right up close reads differently from across the room.
