Hialeah is the sixth-largest city in Florida and the second-largest in Miami-Dade County, with a population of roughly 230,000 people. It's a working-class city with deep Cuban roots, affordable housing compared to Miami proper, and a community feel that's hard to find in flashier parts of the county. If you're considering a move here, this handbook covers what you actually need to know.

## Understanding Hialeah's Layout

Hialeah sits in the northwest corner of Miami-Dade County, bordered by Miami Springs to the east, Miami Lakes to the north, Doral to the southwest, and Medley to the west. The city covers about 22 square miles and is organized on a relatively straightforward grid system.

![A Scenic Aerial View Of Lake Mary Fl Sho 3](/images/blog/2029/08/your-hialeah-moving-handbook/a-scenic-aerial-view-of-lake-mary-fl-sho-3.webp)


The Palmetto Expressway (SR 826) runs along the western edge, connecting to I-75 and the Dolphin Expressway (SR 836). The Gratigny Parkway (SR 924) cuts through the northern section. These highways make Hialeah well-connected to the rest of the county, but rush hour traffic along Okeechobee Road and West 49th Street can be heavy.

Hialeah's Metrorail stations (Hialeah, Okeechobee, and Tri-Rail/Metrorail Transfer) provide direct connections to Downtown Miami, Brickell, and the Health District without dealing with traffic.

## Neighborhoods Worth Knowing

### Palm Springs

The Palm Springs area is one of Hialeah's most established residential neighborhoods. Homes here are primarily single-family, three to four bedrooms, built between the 1950s and 1980s. The community is heavily Cuban-American, with family-owned businesses, bakeries, and restaurants lining the main commercial corridors.

Palm Springs North, a related community north of the main area, offers newer construction and a quieter residential feel. Both areas are popular with families looking for space and value.

### West Hialeah

West Hialeah runs along the Palmetto Expressway corridor and includes a mix of single-family homes, duplexes, and some newer townhouse developments. This area offers the most convenient highway access in the city, making it a good fit for commuters who work in Doral, the Airport area, or Kendall.

Amelia Earhart Park sits on the western edge of the city, spreading across 515 acres with five lakes, a fishing pier, dog park, soccer fields, a watersports complex for wakeboarding and paddleboarding, and a petting zoo. It's the city's largest green space and a major draw for families.

### The Leah Arts District

Hialeah's emerging cultural hub is the Leah Arts District, a mural-covered area filled with art galleries, food trucks, local boutiques, thrift shops, and antique stores. The district hosts regular block parties with live music and community events. If you're coming from a more urban area and want some creative energy in your neighborhood, this is where to look.

### East Hialeah

The eastern portion of the city, closer to Miami Springs and the airport, features older residential blocks and a dense commercial strip along East 4th Avenue and Palm Avenue. This area is more urban in character, with smaller lots and a walkable grid of restaurants, markets, and service businesses.

Properties here tend to be the most affordable in the city, and the proximity to the airport makes it practical for frequent travelers, though you'll hear more plane noise than in other parts of Hialeah.

## The Housing Market

Hialeah's housing costs run well below the Miami-Dade average, which is one of the biggest reasons people move here. Single-family homes typically range from $350,000 to $550,000 depending on size, condition, and location. Newer townhomes and updated properties push higher, while older fixers can be found for under $400,000.

![Aerial View Showcasing The Grid Like Pat 1](/images/blog/2029/08/your-hialeah-moving-handbook/aerial-view-showcasing-the-grid-like-pat-1.webp)


Rentals are also more affordable. A three-bedroom house or duplex rents for $2,000 to $2,800 per month, compared to $3,000 to $4,000 for similar space in Coral Gables or Coconut Grove.

Many properties in Hialeah are duplexes or have efficiency apartments in the back, a common arrangement that helps homeowners offset their mortgage. If you're buying, check the zoning on any property that advertises rental income potential.

## What You Need to Know Before Moving Day

### Parking and Street Access

Street parking in Hialeah's residential areas is tight. Many homes were built before the era of two-car garages, so driveways are short and overflow parking fills the street quickly. On moving day, your moving truck needs a clear path to the front of the property.

If you're moving to a house, let your neighbors know in advance that a truck will need curb space. Most people in Hialeah are helpful about this, but a heads-up prevents conflicts.

For apartment complexes, check whether the building has a loading area. Some of the larger developments along West 68th Street and the Palm Springs Mile area have designated spots. Smaller buildings may not.

### HOA and Community Rules

Many of the newer townhome and gated communities in Hialeah have HOAs with rules about moving hours, common area use, and even where you can park a moving truck. Get the HOA contact information from your landlord or seller and confirm the move-in process before your moving date.

Older single-family neighborhoods generally don't have HOAs, giving you more flexibility with timing and access.

### Utility Setup

Water and sewer services are through the City of Hialeah Public Utilities Department. Set up your account before move-in day to avoid arriving at a house with no water. Electric is through Florida Power & Light (FPL), and internet/cable options include AT&T, Xfinity, and several smaller providers.

## Getting Around from Hialeah

Commute times from Hialeah to major employment centers:

- **Downtown Miami:** 20 to 35 minutes by car, or about 25 minutes via Metrorail from the Hialeah station
- **Brickell:** 25 to 40 minutes by car
- **Doral:** 10 to 20 minutes via the Palmetto or NW 74th Street
- **Miami International Airport:** 10 to 15 minutes
- **Miami Beach:** 30 to 50 minutes depending on traffic

Metrorail is a real option for Downtown and Brickell commuters. Parking at the Hialeah station is free, and the ride to Government Center takes about 25 minutes. That often beats sitting in traffic on I-95.

## Local Landmarks and Essentials

**Hialeah Park Racing and Casino:** A nearly century-old landmark covering 200 acres, the park is also an Audubon Bird Sanctuary where pink flamingos roam the grounds. The casino offers poker and slot machines.

![Scenic View Of A Suburban Street Lined W 4](/images/blog/2029/08/your-hialeah-moving-handbook/scenic-view-of-a-suburban-street-lined-w-4.webp)


**Westland Mall:** The city's main shopping center at 49th Street and 87th Avenue. Anchored by major retailers, it handles most everyday shopping needs.

**Palm Springs Mile:** A commercial corridor along West 49th Street packed with restaurants, markets, and stores. This is where locals shop and eat.

**Amelia Earhart Park:** The 515-acre park on the western edge of the city, offering everything from mountain biking trails to paddleboarding lakes to a petting zoo for kids.

### Is Hialeah a good place to live?

For families looking for affordable housing, a strong community feel, and good access to the rest of Miami-Dade, Hialeah works well. The city has lower housing costs than most of the county, a vibrant food and cultural scene rooted in Cuban heritage, and solid highway and transit connections. The tradeoffs include older housing stock in some areas and heavier traffic on main corridors.

### What are the best neighborhoods in Hialeah for families?

Palm Springs and Palm Springs North are the most popular family neighborhoods, offering single-family homes with yards, proximity to parks and schools, and an established community. West Hialeah is also good for families who want easy access to Amelia Earhart Park.

### How is the commute from Hialeah?

It depends on your destination. Downtown Miami is 20 to 35 minutes by car or 25 minutes by Metrorail. Doral is 10 to 20 minutes. Miami Beach is 30 to 50 minutes depending on traffic.
