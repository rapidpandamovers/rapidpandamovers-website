# 9 Appliance Features That Complicate Moving Day

Appliances keep getting bigger, heavier, and more feature-packed. Great for cooking and laundry, not great for moving day. Many of the premium features that sold you on the appliance in the showroom become obstacles when it's time to get that unit out of your kitchen and into a truck. We've moved appliances with every one of these features across Miami-Dade, and each one adds time, complexity, or risk to the job.

## 1. In-Door Water and Ice Dispensers

That convenient water and ice dispenser on your refrigerator door means there's a water supply line running to the fridge, a water filter inside the unit, and a reservoir that holds water even after you unplug it. During a move, you need to shut off the water supply valve, disconnect the line, drain the reservoir (run the dispenser with the fridge unplugged until it stops flowing), and remove the water filter to prevent leaking. Skip any of these steps and you'll have water dripping through the truck during your drive from Kendall to your new place in Weston.

## 2. French Doors with External Handles

French-door refrigerators are wider than single-door models by 4-6 inches when you include the protruding handles. In older Miami homes throughout Little Havana and Coconut Grove where doorways run 28-30 inches wide, those handles make the difference between fitting and not fitting. Removing the doors at the hinges recovers that width, but it adds 15-20 minutes of work with tools. Some premium brands use concealed hinges that aren't obvious to find without the owner's manual.

## 3. Smart Touchscreens

Samsung Family Hub fridges, LG InstaView models, and similar smart refrigerators have large LCD touchscreens built into the door. These screens are exposed, expensive to replace ($300-$800), and sensitive to impact and pressure. During a move, the screen needs to be covered with a microfiber cloth, then a layer of rigid cardboard taped in place. Bubble wrap is a bad choice for touchscreens since the bubbles can activate touch functions and the texture can leave marks on the coating. In the heat of a Miami moving truck, the screen adhesive can also soften, so keeping the truck cool or minimizing time inside matters.

## 4. Bottom-Freezer Drawers

Bottom-freezer drawers on refrigerators are heavy (the drawer alone can weigh 20-30 pounds loaded with its glide mechanism), and they slide open the moment the fridge is tilted back onto a dolly. An open freezer drawer shifts the center of gravity forward, catches on doorframes, and can bend the glide rails if it swings too far. Secure the drawer with ratchet straps or remove it entirely and pack it separately. The glide rails on some models need to be locked in place with a clip that's easy to miss.

## 5. Steam Functions

Steam ovens, steam washers, and steam dryers all have internal water reservoirs or direct water connections that retain water. A steam oven may have a removable water tank that needs to be emptied and dried. A steam washer connects to a dedicated water line separate from the standard hot and cold connections. During a move, any water left in these systems leaks out during transport, and in some cases, water drips onto electronic components. Drain all steam reservoirs, run a dry cycle where possible, and disconnect any additional water lines.

## 6. Built-In Trim Kits

Built-in microwaves, coffee makers, and warming drawers use trim kits that create a flush appearance with the surrounding cabinetry. The trim kit is a separate metal frame that mounts to the cabinet and hides the gaps around the appliance. When you pull the appliance out for a move, you need to remove the trim kit first without bending it. These kits are model-specific and hard to replace if damaged. Label each piece and pack it with padding. At the new home, the trim kit reinstalls around the appliance in its new cabinet opening, but only if the opening dimensions match.

## 7. Gas Connections with Flexible Connectors

Gas ranges and gas dryers use flexible corrugated metal connectors between the gas shutoff valve and the appliance. Here's the complication: these connectors should be replaced every time they're disconnected. Reusing an old connector risks a gas leak at the fitting, since the metal and the seals degrade with each connection cycle. That means you need a new connector (the right length and diameter) available at the new home for reconnection. A licensed professional should handle both the disconnection and reinstallation, which adds scheduling and cost to the move. In Miami condos where gas connections exist, the building may require proof that a licensed plumber did the work.

## 8. Counter-Depth and Panel-Ready Designs

Counter-depth refrigerators and panel-ready dishwashers are designed to sit flush with cabinetry. They have custom panels on the front that match your kitchen doors and are attached with screws and brackets specific to your cabinet style. Moving these appliances means deciding whether to detach the custom panels (and risk scratching or damaging them) or move the appliance with the panels attached (which adds weight and width). Panel-ready appliances are common in the luxury kitchens of Coral Gables and Coconut Grove, and the custom panels can cost $500-$1,500 to replace if damaged.

## 9. Pedestals and Risers

Front-load washers and dryers often sit on matching pedestals, which are elevated platforms with built-in storage drawers. The pedestal bolts to the bottom of the washer or dryer. Moving the unit means deciding whether to detach the pedestal or move them as a pair. Leaving the pedestal attached adds height (usually 15 inches) and weight (40-60 pounds), which can create clearance issues through doorways and in elevators. Detaching the pedestal requires tipping the unit to access the bolts underneath, which is awkward with a 200-pound washer. We typically detach them, move each piece separately, and reassemble at the new location.
