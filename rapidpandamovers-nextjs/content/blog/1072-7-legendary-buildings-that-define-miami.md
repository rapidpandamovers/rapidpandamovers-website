Miami's August architecture tour starts here. From Art Deco gems to Mediterranean Revival mansions, these are the most fascinating properties that have shaped our city through decades of transformation.

## 1. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens

James Deering, co-founder of the Deering Harvester Company, built this 34-room Italian Renaissance villa between 1914 and 1922. Located at 3251 South Miami Avenue in Coconut Grove, Vizcaya required 1,000 workers and cost $15 million (equivalent to roughly $250 million today). The estate includes formal gardens, a stone barge in Biscayne Bay, and one of the finest collections of European decorative arts in the Americas. Deering shipped entire rooms from European palaces to furnish the home. Today, as a museum, Vizcaya continues to receive and relocate priceless artifacts for exhibitions.

## 2. The Freedom Tower

Rising at 600 Biscayne Boulevard, the Freedom Tower was built in 1925 as the headquarters of The Miami News. Its 255-foot tower, inspired by the Giralda bell tower in Seville, Spain, dominated Miami's skyline for decades. From 1962 to 1974, the building served as the Cuban Assistance Center, processing over 650,000 Cuban refugees seeking asylum in the United States. For many families, their first moments in America began inside these walls. The building is now a National Historic Landmark and houses Miami Dade College's Museum of Art and Design.

## 3. The Biltmore Hotel

George Merrick opened the Biltmore Hotel in 1926 as the centerpiece of his planned city of Coral Gables. The 315-foot tower, modeled after the Giralda in Seville, made it the tallest building in Florida at the time. The hotel's history reads like a novel: it hosted Al Capone's gambling operations, served as a military hospital during World War II, and sat abandoned for years before restoration in 1987. Today, at 1200 Anastasia Avenue, the Biltmore operates as a luxury hotel with the largest hotel pool in the continental United States.

## 4. Art Deco Historic District

Between 5th and 23rd Streets along Ocean Drive, Collins Avenue, and Washington Avenue, South Beach contains the world's largest collection of Art Deco architecture. More than 800 buildings from the 1930s and 40s feature the pastel colors, geometric patterns, and neon signs that define Miami Beach's iconic look. The Colony Hotel (736 Ocean Drive), the Breakwater (940 Ocean Drive), and the Carlyle (1250 Ocean Drive) exemplify the style. These buildings have housed generations of Miami Beach residents, from retirees in the 1970s to celebrities and tourists today.

## 5. Coral Gables City Hall

Designed by Phineas Paist and built in 1928, Coral Gables City Hall at 405 Biltmore Way represents the Mediterranean Revival style that defines the city. The three-story building features a semi-circular colonnade, Spanish barrel tile roof, and a clock tower that chimes every fifteen minutes. George Merrick, the city's founder, chose this style to create a unified aesthetic throughout Coral Gables. The building has served as the city's government center for nearly a century.

## 6. Fontainebleau Miami Beach

When the Fontainebleau opened at 4441 Collins Avenue in 1954, architect Morris Lapidus had created something entirely new: a resort designed for middle-class Americans seeking glamour. The sweeping "Stairway to Nowhere," the "cheese holes" of the facade, and the theatrical lobbies influenced hotel design worldwide. The Fontainebleau hosted Frank Sinatra, Elvis Presley, and the Rat Pack. James Bond visited in "Goldfinger." After a $1 billion renovation completed in 2008, it remains one of Miami Beach's most recognized buildings.

## 7. The Alfred I. duPont Building

At 169 East Flagler Street, the duPont Building opened in 1939 as the Florida National Bank headquarters. This 17-story Art Deco tower features stunning brass elevator doors, terrazzo floors, and a ceiling mural depicting Florida's history. The building's ornate lobby, designed by architects Marsh and Saxelbye, remains one of downtown Miami's most beautiful interior spaces. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places, the duPont Building continues to serve as an office building and event venue.
