Miami's hurricane season runs from June 1 through November 30, which happens to overlap with peak moving season for families with school-aged children. If you're relocating during these months, proper planning can mean the difference between a smooth move and a stressful scramble. Here's everything you need to know about moving safely during hurricane season in South Florida.

## Monitor Weather Forecasts Early and Often

Unlike other regions where weather surprises can strike with little warning, tropical systems in Miami typically provide several days of advance notice. Start monitoring the National Hurricane Center forecasts at least two weeks before your scheduled move date. Pay attention to tropical waves coming off the African coast—these can develop into named storms as they cross the Atlantic.

The best practice is to have a weather monitoring routine:
- Check the NHC five-day forecast daily starting two weeks out
- Sign up for Miami-Dade Emergency Management alerts
- Download a reliable weather app with push notifications
- Watch for tropical storm watches (conditions possible within 48 hours) and warnings (conditions expected within 36 hours)

## What to Do If a Storm Approaches Moving Day

When a tropical system threatens your moving date, communication with your moving company becomes critical. Most reputable Miami movers have established protocols for storm-related schedule changes.

**48-72 hours before potential impact:** Contact your moving company to discuss contingency plans. Many companies will proactively reach out to customers when storms enter the Gulf or approach Florida's east coast.

**Under tropical storm watch:** Consider rescheduling if your move is within 48 hours. Even if the storm doesn't make direct landfall, outer bands can bring dangerous conditions.

**Under hurricane warning:** Your move will almost certainly need rescheduling. Focus on securing your current and new properties instead.

Remember that moving trucks are particularly vulnerable to high winds. A loaded 26-foot truck can become dangerous in sustained winds over 40 mph—well below hurricane strength.

## Insurance Considerations During Storm Season

Review your moving insurance options carefully during hurricane season. Standard released value protection (included with most moves) covers items at a minimal rate. Full value protection provides better coverage but may have exclusions.

Key questions to ask:
- Does the policy cover weather-related delays?
- What happens if items are damaged by wind or water during the move?
- Is there coverage for items stored temporarily due to storm rescheduling?
- How do claims work if both properties sustain storm damage?

Additionally, verify your homeowner's or renter's insurance at both locations. Understand deductibles and coverage limits, especially for wind and flood damage, which often require separate policies in Florida.

## Rescheduling Policies and Best Practices

The best moving companies understand that hurricane-related cancellations are beyond anyone's control. Look for these policies when booking:

- **No-penalty rescheduling** when tropical storm or hurricane watches/warnings are issued for Miami-Dade County
- **Deposit protection** that allows transferring your deposit to a new date
- **Priority rebooking** for customers displaced by weather
- **Flexible scheduling windows** during active storm periods

When you do reschedule, try to book at least a week out from the storm's expected passage. Even after a hurricane moves through, debris, power outages, and road closures can make moving impossible for several days.

## Emergency Supplies for Hurricane-Season Moves

When moving during hurricane season, keep emergency supplies accessible—not packed in the truck. Prepare a "storm kit" that stays with you:

- Flashlights and batteries
- Phone chargers and portable power banks
- Important documents in waterproof containers
- Three days of non-perishable food and water
- First aid supplies
- Cash (ATMs may be offline after storms)
- Prescription medications
- Pet supplies if applicable

This kit should be the last thing loaded and first thing unloaded, or better yet, transported in your personal vehicle.
