# Relocating a Home Gym: Complete Planning Guide

You spent months building your home gym in your Miami garage or spare bedroom. Now you're moving, and all that equipment needs to come with you. The good news: a well-planned gym move keeps everything intact and gets you back to training fast. The bad news: winging it almost guarantees damaged equipment and a very sore back.

## Take Inventory and Photograph Everything

Before disassembling anything, walk through your gym and list every piece of equipment. Photograph each item from multiple angles, and take close-ups of how multi-part machines connect. Label photos with room dimensions and equipment placement if you want to recreate the same layout. This inventory also serves as your insurance documentation if something gets damaged.

## Create a Disassembly Plan

Not everything needs to come apart. A standalone dumbbell rack can travel assembled if it's not too wide for doorways. A Bowflex home gym or cable machine absolutely needs disassembly. Work through your inventory and decide what disassembles and what doesn't. Order replacement hardware kits from manufacturers for any equipment older than five years, since original bolts may strip during removal.

## Handling Cardio Equipment

Treadmills fold and lock for transport, but the folding mechanism varies by brand. NordicTrack, Sole, and ProForm each have different locking methods. Check the manual. Elliptical machines need their arms locked in a fixed position or removed entirely. Stationary bikes are usually simple: remove the pedals, lower the seat, and wrap the console. Rowers need the seat secured to the rail, or it slides off during transport.

## Disassembling Strength Equipment

Squat racks, power cages, and Smith machines are the big projects. These typically bolt together with 10-20+ individual bolts per connection point. We use a systematic approach: work from top to bottom, label each piece with blue painter's tape and a number, and bag all hardware for each section separately. A labeled zip-lock bag taped to its corresponding frame section prevents the nightmare of a pile of mystery bolts at reassembly.

## Weight Plates and Dumbbells

Iron and rubber-coated plates are straightforward but heavy in aggregate. A complete set of Olympic plates runs 300-400 pounds. Use small reinforced boxes or milk crates, keeping each container under 60 pounds. Stack plates flat with cardboard dividers between them to prevent scratching. Hex dumbbells can ride in milk crates without wrapping. Adjustable dumbbells like Bowflex SelectTech or PowerBlock need their adjustment mechanisms locked and get wrapped like fragile equipment.

## Flooring and Mirrors

Rubber gym flooring in Miami garages often sits directly on concrete. Horse stall mats weigh about 100 pounds each. Roll them up, strap them with ratchet ties, and stand them upright in the truck. Interlocking rubber tiles stack flat with cardboard between layers. Gym mirrors mount with clips or adhesive. We remove clip-mounted mirrors, wrap them in mirror boxes, and transport upright. Adhesive-mounted mirrors sometimes crack during removal, so budget for potential replacement.

## Setting Up the New Space

Measure the destination room before moving day. Draw out equipment placement on a floor plan so the heavy stuff goes in first and in the right spot. Rubber flooring goes down before anything else. Power cages and racks assemble next since they need the most floor space and wall clearance. Cardio equipment fills in around the edges. Check that the floor can support the concentrated weight load, especially on second-floor rooms.
