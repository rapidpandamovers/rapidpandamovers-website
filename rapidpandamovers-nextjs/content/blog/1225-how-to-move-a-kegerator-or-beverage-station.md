# How to Move a Kegerator or Beverage Station

Miami's entertaining culture means plenty of homes come equipped with kegerators, wine dispensers, beverage fridges, and full-blown bar stations. When it's time to move, these units combine the challenges of heavy appliances with the complications of pressurized gas systems and temperature-sensitive contents. Here's how we handle them.

## Kegerator Prep: Start 24 Hours Early

A kegerator needs at least 24 hours of preparation before moving day. Turn off the CO2 tank and disconnect the gas line from the keg coupler. Disconnect the beer lines and drain any remaining beer through the tap. Remove the keg, which weighs about 160 pounds when full (a half-barrel, the standard size). If you're moving locally, you can keep a partially full keg cold in a cooler during transport. For longer moves, consider finishing the keg beforehand or returning it to the distributor.

## Handling the CO2 Tank

CO2 tanks are pressurized cylinders that require specific handling. Close the tank valve completely, disconnect the regulator, and cap the tank. CO2 tanks should travel upright and secured so they can't roll or fall. Never leave a CO2 tank in a hot truck, especially in Miami summer. Heat increases pressure inside the tank, and while they have safety valves, it's better to transport CO2 in the cab or your personal vehicle.

## Moving the Unit Itself

A standard kegerator weighs 75-150 pounds empty, depending on the model. Full-size units are about the same dimensions as a small refrigerator. Like any refrigerated appliance, keep it upright during transport. If it must tilt more than 45 degrees to clear a doorway, let it stand upright for the same number of hours it was tilted before plugging it in. This gives the compressor oil time to settle back down. We wrap the exterior in moving blankets and secure the door shut with a strap or bungee cord.

## Built-In Beverage Stations

Custom-built bar areas with under-counter beverage coolers, ice makers, and glass-front fridges need each component disconnected individually. Ice makers require water line shut-off and disconnection. We drain the water lines and stuff towels inside to absorb any remaining moisture. Built-in units that slide out of cabinetry need careful extraction to avoid scratching countertops and side panels. We note the exact dimensions and configuration so reinstallation at the new home matches.

## Wine and Beverage Fridges

Thermoelectric wine coolers (the kind without a compressor) are lighter and more transport-friendly, but the cooling elements are fragile. Compressor-based units follow the same rules as regular refrigerators: stay upright, defrost before moving, and wait before plugging in. We remove all bottles and shelves. Glass shelves get wrapped individually in bubble cushion and packed in a box. Wine bottles travel in insulated wine shippers, not loose in the fridge.

## Reconnecting at the New Home

At the destination, position the kegerator in its final spot before reconnecting anything. It needs to be level for proper dispensing (an unlevel kegerator pours foamy beer). Plug it in and let it cool for 4-6 hours before loading a keg. Reconnect the CO2 line, set the regulator to the proper PSI for your beer style (typically 10-14 PSI for most ales and lagers), and connect the keg coupler. Pull a pint and check that the carbonation and temperature are right.
