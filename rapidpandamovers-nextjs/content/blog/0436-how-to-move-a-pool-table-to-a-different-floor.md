# How to Move a Pool Table to a Different Floor

Moving a pool table across a room is hard enough. Moving one between floors adds gravity, narrow stairwells, and tight corners to an already heavy, fragile operation. Whether you're relocating a table from a first-floor living room to a second-floor game room, or moving one out of a third-floor condo, the height change creates risks that flat-ground moves don't have. Here's how we handle floor-to-floor pool table moves in Miami.

## Why Floor Changes Make Pool Table Moves Harder

A standard 8-foot slate pool table weighs around 800 pounds fully assembled. Even broken down, the individual slate pieces weigh 150 to 250 pounds each. Carrying that kind of weight up or down stairs requires:

![A Man Setting Up Billiard Balls On A Poo 2](/images/blog/2027/02/how-to-move-a-pool-table-to-a-different-floor/a-man-setting-up-billiard-balls-on-a-poo-2.webp)


- Enough people to safely bear the load at an angle (minimum 3-4 experienced movers per slate piece)
- Stairways wide enough to fit the slate pieces plus the people carrying them
- Secure footing on every step
- A plan for turns, landings, and doorways at the top and bottom

In Miami, many homes have tile or marble stairs that become slippery, especially during summer when humidity causes condensation on hard surfaces. High-rise condos add freight elevator logistics, loading dock schedules, and long hallway distances between the elevator and your unit.

## The Pre-Move Measurement

Before we show up on moving day, our [**Pool Table Moving**](/pool-table-moving) team measures every part of the route between the old location and the new one. We check:

- **Stairway width and ceiling height**: Slate pieces need at least 36 inches of clearance to pass through. Most residential stairways in South Florida are 36 to 42 inches wide, which is tight but workable.
- **Landing dimensions**: The turn at a stairway landing is often the trickiest part. We need enough room to rotate the slate pieces around the corner.
- **Doorway clearances**: Both the room you're leaving and the room you're entering need to accommodate the largest component.
- **Elevator dimensions**: For condo moves, the freight elevator has to fit the slate pieces. We get these specs from building management before scheduling.
- **Floor load capacity**: Older homes with wooden upper floors should be checked to confirm they can support an 800-pound table in the intended location.

## Going Up: Carrying Slate Upstairs

Moving slate pieces upstairs is the more demanding direction. The team at the bottom bears most of the weight, and gravity works against you the whole way. Our approach:

- Each slate piece gets wrapped in heavy moving blankets and strapped to a specialized carrying frame
- One crew member leads from the top, guiding placement and calling out obstacles
- Two to three crew members below support the weight and control the angle
- We never rush. Each step is deliberate, with full stops at landings
- Non-slip mats are placed on tile or marble stairs

For homes with spiral staircases or extremely narrow stairways, we sometimes use the balcony or window route instead. This involves rigging equipment to hoist the slate pieces to the upper floor from outside. It sounds dramatic, but it's actually safer than trying to force heavy stone through a stairway that's too tight.

## Going Down: Gravity Is Not Your Friend

Carrying slate downstairs is riskier in some ways because a slip means the weight accelerates with gravity. The team above carries the brunt of the load, using controlled lowering rather than walking. We protect stair edges and walls with corner guards and blankets. One spotter stays ahead of the piece at all times, watching for obstacles and confirming each step.

## Elevator Moves in Miami High-Rises

Many of our floor-to-floor moves happen in Miami's condo towers. The freight elevator is your best friend here, but it requires coordination:

- **Reserve the freight elevator** well in advance. Popular buildings in Brickell and Downtown book up weeks ahead.
- **Provide a COI** (certificate of insurance) to building management, which we handle.
- **Pad the elevator interior** with moving blankets to protect both the slate and the elevator walls.
- **Time the move** to avoid resident traffic, usually early morning or mid-afternoon.
