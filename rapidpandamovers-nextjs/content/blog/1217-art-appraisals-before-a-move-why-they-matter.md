# Art Appraisals Before a Move: Why They Matter

You know that painting is worth something. But do you know exactly how much? When it comes time to move your art collection, "I think it's worth around ten thousand" isn't good enough for an insurance claim. A professional appraisal completed before the move sets the documented value that determines your coverage, your premiums, and your settlement if something goes wrong.

## What an Art Appraisal Actually Is

An art appraisal is a written opinion of value prepared by a qualified professional. It includes a description of the piece, its provenance (ownership history), its condition, and a market value based on comparable sales and current demand. The appraiser signs the document and stands behind the valuation professionally.

This is different from a gallery's asking price or what you paid at Art Basel five years ago. Market values change. An artist who was emerging when you bought the piece might now be established, and the value could have doubled. Or the market may have shifted, and the piece is worth less than you assumed. Either way, you need an accurate number.

## Why You Need One Before a Move

Insurance coverage for artwork in transit is based on declared value. If you don't have an appraisal, you're guessing at the value, and guesses cause problems on both ends.

**Undervaluing**: If you declare a painting at $5,000 and it's actually worth $20,000, your insurance payout after a total loss is capped at $5,000. You eat the $15,000 difference.

**Overvaluing**: If you declare a painting at $20,000 and it's worth $5,000, you've been overpaying for insurance coverage. Worse, the insurer may challenge the claim and delay settlement while they verify the actual value.

A current appraisal eliminates both problems. The number is documented, justified, and defensible.

## When to Get the Appraisal

Schedule the appraisal at least two to four weeks before your move. This gives the appraiser time to research comparables, write the report, and deliver it before your packing date. Rushing an appraisal leads to less thorough research and potentially less accurate valuations.

If your collection hasn't been appraised in more than three years, it's time for an update regardless of whether you're moving. Art markets move, and values can shift significantly over that period.

## Finding a Qualified Appraiser in Miami

Look for appraisers who hold credentials from recognized professional organizations like the American Society of Appraisers (ASA), the Appraisers Association of America (AAA), or the International Society of Appraisers (ISA). These organizations require education, testing, and adherence to professional standards.

Miami has a strong community of certified art appraisers who specialize in various categories, from Latin American art to contemporary works to decorative arts. Ask your gallery, your insurance agent, or your estate attorney for referrals. Avoid appraisers who also want to buy or sell the work, as that creates a conflict of interest.

## What the Appraisal Should Include

A proper appraisal document for moving purposes should contain:

- Full description of the artwork (artist, title, medium, dimensions, date)
- Provenance and exhibition history if available
- Current condition assessment
- Market analysis with comparable sales data
- Fair market value or replacement value (ask your insurer which they require)
- The appraiser's credentials, signature, and date
- Photographs of the piece

Keep the original appraisal in a safe place away from the artwork. Store a digital copy in the cloud so it's accessible from anywhere.

## How Appraisals Support the Moving Process

Beyond insurance, appraisals help your moving team prioritize. When we see the declared values for each piece, we know which items need custom crating, climate-controlled transport, and the highest level of handling care. A collection with one $50,000 painting and twenty $500 prints gets packed differently than one where every piece is equally valuable.
