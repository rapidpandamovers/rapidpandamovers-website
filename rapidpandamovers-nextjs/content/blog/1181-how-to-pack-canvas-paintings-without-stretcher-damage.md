# How to Pack Canvas Paintings Without Stretcher Damage

The stretcher bar is the wooden frame inside a canvas painting that keeps the fabric taut. Damage a stretcher bar during a move and the canvas goes slack, wrinkles form, and the painting needs professional restretching that can cost hundreds of dollars. We see stretcher damage regularly on moves we unpack for new clients, usually from incorrect packing methods that put pressure in the wrong places. Here's how to protect the stretcher and the canvas during your move.

## How Stretcher Bars Get Damaged

Stretcher bars are typically made from pine or poplar and joined at the corners with either mortise-and-tenon joints or simple lap joints held by wooden keys (small wedges). These joints are designed to be adjustable, not permanent, which makes them vulnerable to several kinds of damage during a move.

![Grayscale Image Of A Classic Sculpture I 3](/images/blog/2032/10/how-to-pack-canvas-paintings-without-stretcher-damage/grayscale-image-of-a-classic-sculpture-i-3.webp)


**Corner separation**: When a painting is lifted by one corner or leaned at an angle, the weight of the canvas and frame pulls the opposite corner outward. Over time or under sudden stress, the corner joint opens up and the stretcher goes out of square.

**Warping**: Wood absorbs moisture and expands. In Miami, where humidity swings between indoor air conditioning and outdoor air are dramatic, stretcher bars can warp, bow, or twist. A warped stretcher pulls the canvas unevenly, creating visible distortion in the painted surface.

**Pressure dents**: Stacking paintings flat with other items on top puts direct pressure on the stretcher bars and the canvas between them. This can crack bars, dislodge corner keys, and leave permanent dents in the canvas.

## Step 1: Secure the Corner Keys

Before wrapping, check the back of the painting for small wooden wedges in the corner joints. These keys keep the stretcher bars tight and the canvas taut. During transport, vibration can work them loose and they fall inside the frame, rattling around and potentially puncturing the canvas from behind.

Use a small piece of painter's tape over each key to hold it in place. Don't push the keys deeper or adjust them, just secure them where they are.

## Step 2: Support the Canvas From Behind

For medium to large canvases, place a rigid backing board behind the painting before wrapping. A piece of acid-free corrugated cardboard or foam board cut to the same dimensions as the stretcher bars works well. This board serves two purposes: it prevents anything from pressing into the canvas from behind, and it adds rigidity to the whole assembly so the stretcher bars flex less during handling.

Attach the backing board with painter's tape along the edges. Don't tape directly to the canvas or the painted surface.

## Step 3: Wrap the Face Properly

Lay a sheet of glassine paper over the front of the painting. This protects the paint surface without sticking to it. Wrap the entire piece, glassine and backing board included, in a layer of bubble wrap with the bubbles facing outward. The bubble wrap should be snug but not tight. Compressing the wrapping against the canvas surface creates the same kind of pressure damage you're trying to prevent.

Secure the bubble wrap with painter's tape on the back side only.

## Step 4: Use a Properly Sized Box or Crate

Place the wrapped painting into a mirror box or picture box. The box should be close to the painting's dimensions with just enough room for a layer of packing paper on each side. Too much extra space allows the painting to shift and bounce, which stresses the stretcher corners with every bump in the road.

For valuable paintings or pieces larger than four feet in any dimension, a custom wooden crate with internal foam support provides much better protection than cardboard.

## Step 5: Transport Upright

Canvas paintings always travel upright, never flat. Flat transport puts the full weight of the frame and stretcher bars on the center of the canvas, which causes sagging. In a truck, stand the boxed painting on its longest edge with padding underneath and between adjacent pieces. Our trucks use padded vertical racks that hold paintings in place without leaning pressure.

If you're transporting paintings in your personal vehicle, stand them upright in the back seat or cargo area. Wedge soft items around the box to prevent it from falling over during turns and stops.
