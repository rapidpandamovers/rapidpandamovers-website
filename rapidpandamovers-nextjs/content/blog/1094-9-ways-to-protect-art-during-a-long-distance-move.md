# 9 Ways to Protect Art During a Long-Distance Move

Moving artwork across town is one thing. Moving it from Miami to New York, Chicago, or Los Angeles multiplies every risk. The piece spends more time on the road, passes through different climate zones, and faces more handling at loading docks and transfer points. Here are nine proven methods our team uses to keep art safe on long-distance moves.

## 1. Get a Professional Appraisal Before You Pack

For long-distance moves, your insurance requirements go up. An up-to-date appraisal from a certified appraiser gives you the documentation needed for transit insurance claims. Appraisals older than two to three years may not reflect current market values, and undervalued pieces receive undervalued settlements. Schedule the appraisal at least two weeks before the move so the paperwork is in hand before packing begins.

## 2. Use Custom Crates, Not Just Boxes

For cross-country transport, cardboard mirror boxes aren't enough. The extended travel time and multiple handling points mean your art needs rigid protection. Custom wooden crates built around each piece absorb impacts that would crush a cardboard box. The crate interior gets lined with closed-cell foam and a vapor barrier to protect against both shock and humidity changes as the truck moves between climate zones.

## 3. Add Vibration Dampening Inside Every Crate

Road vibration over hundreds of miles fatigues materials in ways that a short local move doesn't. Foam padding inside a crate can compress over time, loosening the fit and allowing the piece to shift. We use dense, slow-recovery foam that maintains its cushioning over long distances. For glass-fronted pieces, vibration-dampening mounts prevent the sustained rattling that leads to stress fractures.

## 4. Seal Against Humidity Changes

A truck driving from Miami to the Northeast passes through dramatic humidity shifts. Miami's 80% humidity drops to 30% or less in winter in northern states. That swing causes wood to contract, canvas to tighten, and adhesives to become brittle. Sealing artwork inside polyethylene wrapping with conditioned silica gel creates a stable microclimate that travels with the piece regardless of what's happening outside.

## 5. Insist on Climate-Controlled Transport

Long-distance art transport requires a truck with active climate control, not just insulation. Over a two-day drive, an insulated truck will gradually equalize with outdoor temperatures. Active HVAC maintains a stable 68-72 degrees and 45-55% humidity for the entire journey. This is non-negotiable for valuable artwork.

## 6. Load Art Last, Unload Art First

Art should be the final thing loaded into the truck and the first thing removed at the destination. This minimizes the time art sits in the vehicle during the chaotic loading and unloading phases when other items are being shuffled around. It also ensures art gets placed in the most protected position in the truck, typically against the front wall where it's shielded from shifting cargo behind it.

## 7. Track the Shipment

For high-value collections, GPS tracking on the transport vehicle lets you monitor the shipment in real time. You'll know exactly where your art is, how long the truck has been stopped, and whether it's on schedule. Some carriers also offer temperature monitoring that alerts you if conditions inside the truck drift outside acceptable ranges.

## 8. Coordinate Receiving at the Destination

Don't let crated artwork sit on a loading dock in a different city's weather. Arrange for someone to receive the delivery, open the building, and get pieces inside a climate-controlled space immediately. If you're not at the destination yet, designate a representative or hire a local art handler to supervise the delivery and perform a condition check on arrival.

## 9. Allow Extended Acclimation Time

After a long-distance move, artwork needs longer to acclimate than after a local move. The climate difference between Miami and your destination city may be substantial. Leave wrapped art in the new space for 48 to 72 hours before unwrapping. This gradual adjustment prevents the condensation and dimensional changes that come with sudden environmental shifts.
