# Construction Debris Removal After Home Renovations

You finally finished that kitchen remodel or bathroom renovation, but now your driveway looks like a construction site. Piles of old drywall, ripped-out tile, busted cabinets, and strips of carpet are stacked next to your house. In Miami-Dade County, you can't just leave construction debris at the curb and expect regular trash service to take it. Here's how to handle the aftermath of a renovation project.

## What Counts as Construction Debris

Construction and demolition (C&D) waste includes a wide range of materials generated during renovation, remodeling, or demolition work:

![A Chaotic Pile Of Metal Scrap And Waste  3](/images/blog/2028/08/construction-debris-removal-after-home-renovations/a-chaotic-pile-of-metal-scrap-and-waste--3.webp)


- Drywall and plaster
- Wood framing, trim, and lumber
- Tile, stone, and concrete
- Old cabinets and countertops
- Roofing materials and shingles
- Carpet, padding, and vinyl flooring
- Metal fixtures, pipes, and wiring
- Glass and windows
- Insulation

Miami-Dade's regular bulk trash pickup does not cover most construction debris. The county's waste management guidelines specifically exclude C&D materials from curbside collection. If your contractor didn't include debris removal in their bid, you're responsible for getting it off your property.

## Your Disposal Options

**Contractor removal:** The best scenario is having debris removal written into your renovation contract upfront. Many Miami contractors include a dumpster in their project cost or factor in haul-away fees. If your project is still being planned, make sure this line item is in the estimate.

**Dumpster rental:** For large renovation projects, renting a roll-off dumpster is common. Sizes range from 10 to 40 cubic yards. In Miami-Dade, you may need a permit from the county if the dumpster will sit on a public street or right-of-way. Rental typically runs $300 to $600 for a week, depending on size and weight limits.

The downside is that you still have to load it yourself, and overweight charges can add up fast with heavy materials like tile and concrete.

**Professional junk removal:** For smaller to medium renovation projects, or when the dumpster is gone and debris remains, a [**Junk Removal**](/junk-removal) crew is the fastest solution. We show up with the truck, load everything, and haul it to the appropriate disposal or recycling facility. No permits needed, no waiting for a dumpster delivery window.

**C&D recycling facilities:** Miami-Dade has several facilities that accept construction debris for recycling. Concrete, metal, wood, and asphalt can be processed and reused. Facilities like those in Medley and northwest Miami-Dade handle large volumes of C&D waste and charge by the ton. If you're hauling debris yourself, this is more cost-effective than general landfill disposal.

## Materials That Need Special Handling

Not everything from a renovation can go into a standard dumpster or removal load:

**Asbestos:** Homes built before the early 1980s in Miami may contain asbestos in floor tiles, insulation, popcorn ceilings, or pipe wrap. Florida law requires licensed asbestos abatement for any material that's friable (crumbly). Get it tested before you tear it out.

**Lead paint:** Pre-1978 homes may have lead-based paint. Debris from lead paint removal must be handled according to EPA guidelines and cannot be mixed with regular C&D waste.

**Treated lumber:** Pressure-treated wood, common in Miami for outdoor structures, contains chemicals that prevent it from being recycled with regular lumber. It goes to a lined landfill section rather than a wood recycling operation.

## Keeping Your Property Clean During Renovations

Prevent debris from becoming an overwhelming pile by scheduling regular removal during the project rather than waiting until the end. If you're managing the renovation yourself, have a junk removal crew come every week or two to clear accumulated materials. This keeps your yard, driveway, and neighbors happy, which matters a lot in Miami's close-knit neighborhoods and HOA-governed communities.
