Miami is one of the most culturally diverse cities in America, with residents from Latin America, the Caribbean, Europe, and beyond. Each culture brings unique traditions about moving into a new home. Whether you're relocating to Brickell, settling into Coral Gables, or making Coconut Grove your new neighborhood, these traditions can add meaning to your transition.

## 1. Don't Move on a Rainy Day

Many cultures consider rain on moving day to be bad luck, believing water washes away good fortune. Others view it as a cleansing fresh start. In Miami, afternoon thunderstorms roll through almost daily from May through October, so schedule your morning move early and let the rain handle the cleansing later.

## 2. Carry Salt Into Your New Home First

Salt has protected against evil spirits in folklore worldwide. Many people sprinkle salt in corners or carry a container across the threshold first. This tradition is common in Miami's Cuban, Puerto Rican, and Caribbean communities, where you might see neighbors setting out small dishes of salt in each room before the furniture arrives.

## 3. Never Move on a Friday

In Western traditions, Friday moves are considered unlucky, possibly stemming from superstitions about Friday the 13th. From a practical standpoint, Friday moves in Miami also mean dealing with heavy weekend traffic heading to the Keys or the beaches, so there may be something to this one.

## 4. Bring a New Broom

Old brooms are said to sweep away good luck and bring old problems into your new space. A new broom represents a fresh start. Stop by a Sedano's or Publix in Hialeah or Kendall on your way to the new place and grab a fresh one for good measure.

## 5. Don't Look Back at Your Old Home

Some believe looking back as you leave brings bad luck or means you will return to your old troubles. This tradition resonates with many Miami families who relocated from other countries. Looking forward, not backward, symbolizes commitment to your new life in South Florida.

## 6. Boil Milk and Rice First

In Indian tradition, boiling milk until it overflows symbolizes abundance and prosperity. Cooking rice first brings wealth to your household. Miami has a growing Indian community, particularly in areas like Weston and Pembroke Pines, where these traditions remain strong. Your new neighbors might appreciate learning about this meaningful custom.

## 7. Enter with Bread and Salt

Slavic and Jewish traditions involve entering with bread (so you never know hunger) and salt (so life always has flavor). In neighborhoods like Aventura, Sunny Isles Beach, and Bal Harbour, which have significant Jewish populations, you might see families observing this beautiful tradition when they move in.

## 8. Light a Candle First

Many cultures believe lighting a candle upon entering brings light and warmth to your new home, symbolizing hope and positive energy. In Miami's Cuban community, you might also see families lighting candles to their patron saints or placing a glass of water behind the door for good luck in the new space.
