# Moving Musical Instruments Beyond Pianos

Everyone knows pianos need special handling during a move. But what about the drum kit in the garage, the upright bass in the living room, or the vintage Fender amp collection? Musical instruments range from featherlight to back-breakingly heavy, and nearly all of them are more fragile than they look. Miami's music scene means we move instruments constantly, from home studios in Wynwood to rehearsal spaces in Little Haiti.

## Drum Kits

A full drum kit disassembles into a dozen or more pieces: shells, hardware stands, cymbals, pedals, and the throne. Shells are the priority. We remove all heads (tension rods out, heads off, rods bagged by drum) and nest smaller toms inside larger ones with towels between them. Cymbal edges chip easily, so each cymbal gets a felt divider and rides in a padded cymbal bag or a dedicated box. Hardware stands collapse and bundle together in a hardware bag. The kick drum pedal clamps to nothing during transport and goes in its own case.

## Guitars and String Instruments

Guitars should travel in hard cases, period. A gig bag won't protect a neck from a box shifting in the truck. We loosen string tension by a half step to reduce neck stress during temperature changes. Acoustic guitars are especially sensitive to Miami's humidity swings, and a rapid change from 80% outdoor humidity to a 50% air-conditioned truck can cause the top to crack. We keep guitar cases away from truck walls where heat transfers through the metal.

## Upright Bass and Cello

These instruments are large, awkward, and extremely fragile at the neck joint. An upright bass stands over 6 feet tall and has a thin wooden neck that supports enormous string tension. We loosen the strings, lay the instrument on its back in a padded hard case (or build a custom padded cradle if no case exists), and strap it flat in the truck with nothing stacked above it. Cellos get similar treatment. The bridge should be loosened or removed to prevent it from driving through the top plate during a sudden stop.

## Amplifiers and Speaker Cabinets

Tube amplifiers contain glass vacuum tubes that shatter with impact. We remove tubes, wrap each in bubble cushion, and pack them in a partitioned box. The amp head travels without its tubes, wrapped in moving blankets. Solid-state amps are more durable but still get wrapped. Speaker cabinets are heavy (a Marshall 4x12 weighs 90 pounds) and the speakers are sensitive to puncture. We face speaker cones toward the truck wall and pad the front with foam.

## Keyboards and Synthesizers

A weighted 88-key keyboard runs 50-70 pounds and has a sensitive action mechanism. We transport these in their hard cases or wrap them in blankets on a keyboard stand laid flat. Vintage synthesizers (Moog, Roland, Korg) can be worth thousands to tens of thousands and have delicate knobs, sliders, and circuit boards. These get custom padded, boxed, and treated like fragile electronics.

## Home Recording Equipment

Microphones, audio interfaces, studio monitors, and mixing boards all need individual attention. Condenser microphones should travel in their original shock-mount cases. Studio monitor speakers get wrapped with the cones protected. Cables should be coiled properly (over-under method) and bagged, not tangled into a pile. Rack-mounted equipment stays in the rack if it's a portable case, or gets unracked and individually wrapped.
