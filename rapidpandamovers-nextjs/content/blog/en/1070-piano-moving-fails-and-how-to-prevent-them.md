# Piano Moving Fails and How to Prevent Them

We've been called in to fix situations that started with someone saying "we can handle the piano ourselves." The results are almost always the same: a damaged instrument, scratched floors, injured backs, and a repair bill that dwarfs what professional movers would have charged. Here are the most common piano moving mistakes we see in Miami, and how to make sure they don't happen to you.

## Fail: Rolling a Grand Piano on Its Legs

Grand piano legs are designed to hold the instrument stationary on a flat, level surface. They are not designed for movement, especially over transitions between rooms, thresholds, and the uneven surfaces of driveways and sidewalks. Rolling a grand on its legs puts lateral stress on the leg bolts, and once a bolt loosens, the leg folds and the piano drops.

![A Close Up Of A Grand Piano Keyboard In  4](/images/blog/2028/11/piano-moving-fails-and-how-to-prevent-them/a-close-up-of-a-grand-piano-keyboard-in--4.webp)


**How to prevent it:** Grand pianos must have their legs removed and be placed on a piano board for transport. There are no shortcuts to this step. If someone suggests rolling your grand piano out the door, stop them.

## Fail: Using a Furniture Dolly for an Upright

A standard furniture dolly from a hardware store is rated for maybe 200 to 300 pounds and has small, hard casters that catch on transitions. An upright piano weighs 300 to 800 pounds. The dolly bottoms out, the casters lock on a tile lip, and the piano tips over.

**How to prevent it:** Use a heavy-duty piano dolly rated for the weight, with large rubber casters that roll smoothly over flooring transitions. Professional [**Piano Moving**](/piano-moving) crews carry dollies specifically designed for piano weights and sizes.

## Fail: Not Measuring Doorways

A standard upright piano is about 58 inches wide. A standard interior door opening is 32 to 36 inches. The piano doesn't fit straight through, so it needs to be angled. If the hallway on the other side of the door is narrow, there's no room to complete the angle, and the piano gets stuck.

**How to prevent it:** Measure every doorway, hallway, and turn between the piano and the exit point before moving day. If clearance is tight, remove doors from their hinges to gain 2 to 3 inches. For spaces that are genuinely too tight, identify alternate routes (wider doors, patio access) in advance.

## Fail: Moving in the Afternoon Rain

Miami's afternoon thunderstorms show up fast, especially between June and October. A piano that's halfway between the front door and the truck when a downpour starts is in serious trouble. Water damages wood, ruins felt, corrodes strings, and destroys finishes. Five minutes of rain can cause thousands in damage.

**How to prevent it:** Schedule piano moves for early morning. Check the forecast the night before and the morning of. If rain is likely, have tarps ready and a plan to get the piano under cover quickly. Professional movers monitor radar and adjust timing to avoid storms.

## Fail: Tipping an Upright Piano on Its Back

Some people think the easiest way to get an upright through a tight space is to lay it on its back and slide it. This is a terrible idea. The back panel of an upright piano is thin and not designed to bear the instrument's weight. Laying it down can crack the back, damage internal components, and shift the action mechanism.

**How to prevent it:** Upright pianos should stay upright. They can be tilted slightly and briefly during maneuvering, but they should never be laid flat. If the piano needs to travel through a space where it doesn't fit upright, find a different path.

## Fail: Using the Wrong Crew Size

Moving a grand piano with two people is asking for trouble. The weight requires at least three people for a ground-floor move and four for stairs. Two-person crews end up in situations where they can't safely control the weight during transitions, which leads to drops, strain injuries, and wall damage.

![Musician S Hands Playing Wooden Piano Ke 2](/images/blog/2028/11/piano-moving-fails-and-how-to-prevent-them/musician-s-hands-playing-wooden-piano-ke-2.webp)


**How to prevent it:** Match the crew size to the job. Two experienced movers can handle most upright pianos on a single floor. Grand pianos need three to four. Any move involving stairs adds at least one more person.

## Fail: Skipping the Wrapping

"We'll just be careful" is not a protection plan. Unwrapped pianos get scratched against doorframes, dented by stair railings, and scuffed by the truck's interior. The high-gloss finish on a grand piano shows every mark, and what takes seconds to inflict can take hours and hundreds of dollars to repair.

**How to prevent it:** Wrap the piano completely in quilted moving blankets before it moves an inch. Secure the blankets with straps or stretch wrap. For high-gloss finishes, add a cotton sheet layer beneath the blankets.

## Fail: Ignoring Building Requirements

High-rise buildings in Brickell, Downtown Miami, and the Beaches have strict moving policies: freight elevator reservations, certificates of insurance, specific moving hours, floor protection requirements. Showing up without the right paperwork means getting turned away at the loading dock.

**How to prevent it:** Contact building management at both the origin and destination at least two weeks before the move. Get the requirements in writing and share them with your movers. Professional piano movers in Miami deal with building management regularly and can handle the documentation.

## Fail: No Insurance

Moving a piano without insurance means you're personally liable for any damage to the instrument, your property, and anyone who gets hurt. A piano falling on someone's foot is a medical emergency. A piano crashing through a stair railing is a homeowner's insurance nightmare.

**How to prevent it:** Hire movers with cargo insurance, general liability, and workers' compensation. Ask for proof of insurance before booking. Full value protection for the piano should be discussed and documented before moving day.
