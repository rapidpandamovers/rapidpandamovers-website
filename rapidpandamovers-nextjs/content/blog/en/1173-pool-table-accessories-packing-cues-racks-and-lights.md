# Pool Table Accessories: Packing Cues, Racks, and Lights

When people think about moving their pool table, they focus on the table itself. That makes sense. The slate, frame, and felt are the heavy, expensive, fragile parts. But the accessories that surround your table also need careful handling. Cues, racks, billiard lights, ball sets, and other game room items can be damaged or lost if they're tossed in a box as an afterthought. Here's how we pack and protect everything that goes with your table.

## Cue Sticks

Cues are the most damage-prone accessories during a move. A quality cue can cost anywhere from $50 for a house cue to several thousand for a custom-made playing cue. The main risks are warping, tip damage, and joint problems.

![A Man Aligning Billiard Balls In A Rack  1](/images/blog/2032/08/pool-table-accessories-packing-cues-racks-and-lights/a-man-aligning-billiard-balls-in-a-rack--1.webp)


### How to Pack Cues Properly

- **Hard cases first**: If you have hard cue cases, use them. A good 2x4 or 3x6 case protects multiple cues with individual compartments and padded interiors.
- **No cases? Wrap individually**: Roll each cue in bubble wrap, paying extra attention to the tip and the joint (where the butt and shaft connect). Use rubber bands or tape to keep the wrap in place, but never put tape directly on the cue's finish.
- **Bundle carefully**: Wrapped cues can be bundled together with a moving strap, but don't overtighten. Cues should not press against each other hard enough to flex.
- **Transport flat or vertical**: Never lean cues at an angle in a hot truck. Miami heat (especially in a truck bed that can exceed 120 degrees) softens the wood and accelerates warping. Cues travel best flat in the cab or standing upright in a secure holder.

### Humidity Warning

Wood cues are sensitive to humidity changes. In Miami, going from an air-conditioned house to a hot, humid truck bed and back to AC creates expansion and contraction cycles that can cause warping. Minimize the time cues spend in uncontrolled environments.

## Cue Racks

Wall-mounted cue racks need to be removed carefully from your old wall and reinstalled at the new location:

- **Remove the cues first**, then detach the rack from the wall. Most racks are mounted with screws into studs or drywall anchors.
- **Keep the hardware**: Bag the screws and anchors and tape them to the rack. You'll need them at the new location.
- **Wrap the rack**: A cue rack is typically made of finished wood. Wrap it in a moving blanket to prevent scratches and dings during transport.
- **New wall, new anchors**: At the new home, we can help position the rack near the table. If the wall material or stud spacing is different, you may need different mounting hardware.

## Billiard Lights

A billiard table light fixture is usually a hanging light bar or chandelier-style fixture centered over the table. Moving it requires basic electrical work:

![Competitive Billiards Game In A Modern P 2](/images/blog/2032/08/pool-table-accessories-packing-cues-racks-and-lights/competitive-billiards-game-in-a-modern-p-2.webp)


- **Turn off the breaker** before disconnecting the light. Don't just flip the switch.
- **Lower the fixture** and disconnect the wiring. Cap the exposed wire ends with wire nuts and tape.
- **Disassemble if possible**: Many billiard lights have removable shades or glass panels. Take these off, wrap each one individually, and pack them in a box with padding.
- **Wrap the fixture body**: Moving blankets or bubble wrap protect the metal or wood body from scratches.
- **Reinstallation**: At the new home, the light should be centered over the table, hanging 32 to 36 inches above the playing surface. If there's no existing electrical box in the ceiling at the right position, you'll need an electrician.

Our [**Pool Table Moving**](/pool-table-moving) team handles the table and its accessories, but we recommend hiring a licensed electrician for light fixture installation at the new location.

## Ball Sets

Pool balls are durable, but they can chip if they bang against each other during transport:

- **Don't leave them in the table pockets**: Balls will shift and collide during transport.
- **Use a ball tray or case**: If you have one, great. If not, wrap each ball individually in paper or soft cloth and place them in a box with dividers.
- **A small box works**: A ball set fits in a box about the size of a shoebox. Fill any empty space with packing paper so the balls don't roll around.

## Other Accessories

Don't forget these commonly overlooked items:

![Vibrant Billiard Balls Neatly Arranged O 3](/images/blog/2032/08/pool-table-accessories-packing-cues-racks-and-lights/vibrant-billiard-balls-neatly-arranged-o-3.webp)


- **Bridge stick and mechanical bridge**: Pack with the cues.
- **Chalk**: Toss your chalk cubes in a small bag. They're cheap but annoying to replace.
- **Table brush**: Pack it in a clean bag so it doesn't pick up dirt during the move.
- **Table cover**: Fold the cover and pack it in a labeled bag or box. If it's dirty, this is a good time to wash it.
- **Scoreboard or abacus**: Wall-mounted scoreboards come off the wall and travel with the rack.
