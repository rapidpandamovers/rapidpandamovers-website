# What Not to Put in a Storage Unit

Most storage facilities have a list of prohibited items, but many customers don't read the fine print until after they've already packed everything. Some restrictions are obvious; others catch people off guard. Knowing what stays out of storage prevents safety issues, policy violations, and damage to your other belongings.

## Hazardous Materials and Chemicals

This category covers more household items than people realize:

![A Well Organized Warehouse Aisle With St 2](/images/blog/2026/09/what-not-to-put-in-a-storage-unit/a-well-organized-warehouse-aisle-with-st-2.webp)


- **Gasoline, propane, and lighter fluid**: Even small amounts create fire and explosion risks in an enclosed space.
- **Paint, paint thinner, and stains**: Fumes build up in sealed units and create toxic environments. Miami's heat amplifies the problem by accelerating off-gassing.
- **Cleaning products**: Bleach, ammonia-based cleaners, and pool chemicals (common in South Florida households) are restricted at most facilities.
- **Pesticides and herbicides**: Another Florida staple that doesn't belong in storage.
- **Fireworks**: Illegal to store at any facility, period.

Before packing your garage, pull out anything with a flammable, corrosive, or toxic warning label. These items need proper disposal through Miami-Dade County's hazardous waste collection programs.

## Perishable Food and Plants

Any food that can spoil, rot, or attract pests is banned from storage units. This includes canned goods (they can expand and burst in heat), pet food, cooking oils, and opened pantry items. Even sealed dry goods attract mice and roaches in South Florida's warm climate.

Live plants won't survive in a storage unit regardless of climate control. The lack of light and ventilation kills them within days.

## Weapons and Ammunition

Most storage facilities prohibit firearms and ammunition unless the facility specifically caters to firearms storage with proper licensing. Ammunition is particularly dangerous in hot storage units because heat can cause rounds to discharge. If you own firearms and need storage during a move, look into licensed gun storage facilities or check whether your homeowner's policy covers them at a relative's home.

## Valuables Without Proper Protection

While not technically prohibited, storing certain high-value items without appropriate protection is a mistake:

- **Cash and financial documents**: If stolen, these are gone with no recovery option. Use a bank safe deposit box instead.
- **Irreplaceable family photos and heirlooms**: If the facility floods during a hurricane or a pipe bursts, these can't be replaced. Digitize photos and keep originals in your home or a bank vault.
- **Jewelry**: Too easy to lose in a box and too tempting for theft.

## Unregistered or Illegal Items

This should go without saying, but storage facilities can and do conduct inspections. Anything you can't legally possess doesn't become legal by putting it behind a padlock. Stolen property, controlled substances, and counterfeit goods are reported to law enforcement.

## Items That Suffer Without Climate Control

In Miami specifically, the following items shouldn't go in a standard (non-climate-controlled) unit:

![Two Workers Discussing Inventory In A Wa 1](/images/blog/2026/09/what-not-to-put-in-a-storage-unit/two-workers-discussing-inventory-in-a-wa-1.webp)


- **Electronics**: Condensation from temperature swings corrodes circuit boards.
- **Leather goods**: Mildew develops on leather surfaces within weeks in humid conditions.
- **Wood instruments and furniture**: Warping, cracking, and finish damage from heat and moisture cycling.
- **Photographs and artwork**: Humidity destroys both rapidly.

If you're storing these items, always opt for climate-controlled space.
