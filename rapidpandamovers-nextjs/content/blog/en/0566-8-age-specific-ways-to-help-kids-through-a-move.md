A move that unsettles a teenager barely registers with a toddler. What comforts a 5-year-old might embarrass a 12-year-old. Generic advice falls flat because kids at different developmental stages process change differently. Here are eight approaches tailored to specific age groups.

## For Toddlers (Ages 1-3)

### 1. Stick to Sleep and Feeding Schedules

![A Joyful Child With Socks On Feet Playin 4](/images/blog/2027/12/8-age-specific-ways-to-help-kids-through-a-move/a-joyful-child-with-socks-on-feet-playin-4.webp)


Toddlers lack the language to express anxiety, but they feel disruption intensely. The one thing you can control is timing. Move nap time around loading the truck, not the other way around. Pack their crib or bed last and set it up first at the new place. A toddler who sleeps in their familiar bed the first night adjusts faster than one whose routine gets scrambled.

If moving to Miami from out of state, account for time zone changes. A child used to sleeping at 7pm EST will struggle if you expect them to adjust immediately. Shift bedtime gradually in the days before the move.

### 2. Keep Comfort Objects Accessible

That ratty stuffed animal or worn blanket is your toddler's anchor. Keep it in your bag, not a box. During our moves across Miami-Dade, parents who kept comfort objects visible during the truck loading reported calmer kids throughout the day. Toddlers don't understand boxes, but they understand their bunny is right there.

## For Elementary Kids (Ages 4-10)

### 3. Give Them a Job

![Kids Enjoying Creative Time Drawing On A 2](/images/blog/2027/12/8-age-specific-ways-to-help-kids-through-a-move/kids-enjoying-creative-time-drawing-on-a-2.webp)


Elementary-age kids thrive when they feel useful. Assign real tasks, not busy work. A 6-year-old can sort their books into "keep" and "donate" piles. An 8-year-old can label boxes with room names. A 10-year-old can inventory their belongings on a clipboard.

One Coral Gables family we moved had their 9-year-old create a floor plan for their new room in Kendall. She spent hours deciding where furniture would go, turning anxiety into anticipation.

### 4. Create a Countdown Calendar

Abstract concepts like "we're moving in three weeks" mean nothing to younger elementary kids. A visual countdown calendar with stickers or X marks makes the timeline concrete. Include exciting milestones: "Two weeks: last day at school party. One week: pack your special box. Moving day: meet the movers!"

For Miami moves, add local events to build excitement: "First Saturday in new house: Visit Zoo Miami."

### 5. Facilitate Real Goodbyes

Elementary kids form strong friendships. Rushed goodbyes create unfinished emotional business that surfaces as behavioral problems later. Host a small goodbye gathering. Exchange contact information with other parents. In Miami, where many families relocate frequently, kids who maintain friendships across neighborhoods adjust better than those who make clean breaks.

## For Tweens and Teens (Ages 11-17)

### 6. Acknowledge Their Social Loss

Teenagers lose more than a house when they move. They lose their social network, their identity within a friend group, their physical spaces of independence. Minimize this loss with statements like "I know this is hard" rather than "You'll make new friends."

For Miami-area teens, identify overlap opportunities. If they played soccer at their old school, research programs at their new one before the move. Sports, music programs, and clubs provide ready-made social entry points in communities like Doral, Aventura, and Weston.

### 7. Give Them Control Over Their Space

Let teenagers design their new room without parental input. They might want walls painted black or furniture arranged strangely. Let it happen. Their room becomes a sanctuary when they've made every decision about it.

Budget permitting, let them pick one new item for the new house, whether a desk chair, a rug, or new curtains. Ownership of the space starts with choosing something that wasn't in the old house.

### 8. Don't Force Family Time

Teens process stress by withdrawing. Respect that. Forced family dinners and mandatory "check out the new neighborhood together" activities backfire when teenagers feel dragged along. Instead, invite without pressure. "We're checking out a Cuban place in Little Havana tonight, if you want to come" works better than mandatory outings.

Once they've had time to settle in, their need for space decreases. Families who pushed too hard early often reported longer adjustment periods.

## When to Seek Extra Help

Watch for signs that your child needs support beyond what you can provide:
- Persistent sleep problems lasting more than two weeks
- Regression (bedwetting, baby talk) that doesn't resolve
- School refusal or major academic decline
- Withdrawal that deepens rather than lightens
