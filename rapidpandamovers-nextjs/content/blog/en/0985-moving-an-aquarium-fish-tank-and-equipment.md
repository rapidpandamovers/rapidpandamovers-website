# Moving an Aquarium: Fish, Tank, and Equipment

Aquarium owners in Miami face a unique challenge on moving day. You can't just drain the tank, toss the fish in a bag, and hope for the best. A healthy reef tank or large freshwater setup represents months or years of careful balance, and the move needs to preserve that ecosystem while getting a heavy, fragile glass box safely across town.

## Planning Two Weeks Out

Start reducing feeding a week before the move. Less waste in the system means better water quality during the stressful transport period. Two weeks out, stop adding new fish or corals. Arrange for battery-powered air pumps, insulated coolers, and enough clean 5-gallon buckets to transport your existing tank water. For a 100-gallon reef tank, you'll want to keep at least 50% of the original water since it contains the established bacteria your tank depends on.

## Packing the Fish

Fish travel in clear plastic bags filled one-third with water and two-thirds with air (or pure oxygen from an aquarium supplier for long transports). Double-bag each fish. Aggressive species ride alone. Place bagged fish inside an insulated cooler with a battery-powered aerator. In Miami's heat, add ice packs wrapped in towels to the cooler, not touching the bags directly. Water temperature swings kill faster than most other stressors. Aim to keep the cooler between 72 and 78 degrees.

## Live Rock and Corals

Live rock needs to stay wet. We wrap each piece in damp newspaper, place it in a bucket with a lid, and add enough tank water to keep the bottom covered. Corals get individually bagged in tank water. For a large reef setup, the live rock alone can fill several 5-gallon buckets. These ride in the cab of the truck or in your personal vehicle to maintain temperature control.

## Draining and Moving the Tank

Once livestock and live rock are out, we drain the remaining water. The tank itself is a glass box held together by silicone seals, and those seals are only designed to handle pressure from the inside. A 75-gallon glass tank weighs about 140 pounds empty. We move tanks upright, never on their side or face, because uneven pressure on the seals can cause slow leaks that show up days later. Padding goes underneath and around all sides. For acrylic tanks, we're also careful about surface scratches from moving blankets.

## Equipment and Plumbing

Filters, heaters, protein skimmers, UV sterilizers, and dosing pumps each get disconnected and packed separately. We keep filter media wet in sealed bags of tank water to preserve the beneficial bacteria colony. Canister filters travel upright. Heaters need to cool completely before packing to avoid cracking. Sumps get drained, and plumbing connections get photographed before disassembly so reassembly goes smoothly.

## Reassembly Timeline

At the new home, the tank goes into position first. You won't want to move a full tank twice. We set the stand, level it, place the tank, and begin filling with the saved water. Equipment reconnects, heaters fire up, and the system runs for at least two hours before we add livestock back. Fish get temperature-acclimated by floating their bags for 15-20 minutes. The full biological cycle takes 2-4 weeks to re-establish, so expect some cloudiness.
