# 5 Oversized Items That Won't Fit Through Your Door

Standard interior doorways in Miami homes measure 30-32 inches wide. Standard exterior doors are 36 inches. That sounds like plenty of space until you're staring at an item that's 40 inches across and realizing it's not going through without a plan. Here are the five items that stop homeowners in their tracks on moving day and what we do about each one.

## 1. Sectional Sofas

A three-piece sectional might seem manageable, but the corner piece is the problem. L-shaped corner sections are often 40-44 inches deep and can't be turned, angled, or squeezed through a 32-inch doorway. Removing legs helps with height but not width. For many luxury sectionals common in Miami living rooms, the solution is sliding glass door removal. Patio sliders give us 60-72 inches of clearance. If that's not an option, we sometimes remove interior door frames to gain 2-3 inches, which is enough for a tight angle-and-pivot maneuver.

## 2. King-Size Box Springs

A standard king box spring measures 76 by 80 inches. It will not clear a standard doorway, a hallway turn, or most stairwells. Split king box springs solve this for future purchases, but if you own a one-piece king box spring, it's a problem. In some cases, we can flex it just enough to bend around a tight corner, but rigid platform-style box springs won't flex at all. For high-rise condos in Downtown or Brickell, the freight elevator is the only path, and even freight elevators sometimes fall short on depth.

## 3. Pool Tables

A 7-foot pool table is roughly 44 inches wide and 88 inches long. The slate alone weighs 450+ pounds on a standard model. Pool tables absolutely must be disassembled for moving. The rails come off, the felt gets removed, the slate lifts off in sections, and the frame disassembles. Trying to move a pool table assembled is both physically impossible through standard doors and a guaranteed way to destroy the level and damage the felt. We've seen people try. It never ends well.

## 4. Large Armoires and Entertainment Centers

Floor-to-ceiling armoires and custom entertainment centers from older Miami homes are built like tanks. A solid wood armoire from the early 2000s can measure 48 inches wide, 24 inches deep, and 84 inches tall. That combination of width and height makes doorway clearance impossible without tilting and angling. We remove doors, drawers, and shelving to reduce weight and width where possible. For pieces that simply can't fit, we assess whether the item can be partially disassembled or if a window or slider removal creates enough access.

## 5. Commercial-Style Refrigerators

Sub-Zero, Viking, and Thermador built-in refrigerators in Miami's luxury kitchens range from 36 to 48 inches wide. While they slide out of their built-in cabinetry, getting them through a 36-inch exterior door requires removing the refrigerator doors, which on modern models involves disconnecting water lines and electronic wiring harnesses. Some units also need the hinges removed, which takes the width down just enough to clear. We carry manufacturer-specific tools for the most common brands.

## What to Do When Nothing Works

In rare cases, the item truly cannot exit through any existing opening. At that point, options include window removal (common in ground-floor rooms), temporary wall opening (for a single-stud section), or hoisting through a balcony with a crane. We've used all three methods in Miami. The balcony hoist is actually the most common for oversized furniture in high-rise condos where hallways and elevators are the bottleneck.
