# Transporting Taxidermy and Mounted Collections

Taxidermy mounts and trophy collections hold both financial and sentimental value that can't be replaced. A shoulder-mounted deer head might run $500-1,500 to create, but a record-class marlin mount from a charter off Key West? That's a once-in-a-lifetime piece. Miami and South Florida homes are full of mounted fish, game animals, and display collections, and getting them to a new home requires understanding what makes these items so fragile.

## Why Taxidermy Is Difficult to Move

Taxidermy mounts are built on a rigid foam or fiberglass form covered with preserved skin, fur, or scales. The form itself is sturdy, but the exterior finish is delicate. Glass eyes can pop loose from impact. Ears, fins, and antler tips break easily. Fur compresses and mats if packed too tightly. Fish mounts have painted finishes that chip, crack, and flake with any contact. And everything is mounted on a wooden backboard or plaque that adds awkward shape and weight.

## Wrapping Shoulder Mounts

Deer, elk, and boar shoulder mounts need to come off the wall first. We remove the mounting hardware and set it aside in a labeled bag. The mount itself gets wrapped starting with the most fragile areas: ears, nose, and antler tips. We use acid-free tissue paper over the face and ears, then build custom padding around the antlers using foam pool noodles (cheap, effective, and the right shape). The entire mount then gets wrapped in a clean cotton sheet and placed in an oversized box with crumpled paper filling the voids.

## Fish Mounts and Painted Finishes

Mounted marlins, sailfish, tarpon, and other game fish are common in South Florida homes. These mounts are often 4-8 feet long with thin, protruding fins that snap if bumped. The painted finish is the most vulnerable element. We wrap the entire fish in acid-free tissue, then use soft foam sheets over the body. Fins get individual cardboard splints taped to themselves (never to the mount surface). For large mounts, we build custom plywood crates lined with foam. The mount hangs inside the crate on padded brackets rather than resting on any surface.

## Full-Body Mounts and Display Cases

Full-body mounts like standing bears, alligators (common in Florida collections), or birds in glass display cases require the most care. Glass cases should have the glass panels removed and packed in mirror boxes when possible. Full-body mounts travel standing in their natural position when feasible, secured in a crate that prevents any rocking or tipping. We've moved life-size alligator mounts from dens in the Everglades area, and the key is preventing any flex in the body form during transit.

## Climate Control During Transport

Taxidermy is sensitive to heat and humidity. In Miami's climate, high humidity causes mold growth on preserved hides and fur, while extreme heat softens adhesives used in the mounting process. We transport taxidermy in climate-controlled sections of the truck when available, or schedule moves during cooler morning hours. For long-distance transport, desiccant packs inside the wrapping help manage moisture.

## Hanging at the New Home

At the destination, we install mounting hardware appropriate for the wall construction. Drywall needs toggle bolts or finds a stud. Concrete block walls common in South Florida homes need masonry anchors. A 50-pound shoulder mount needs a mounting system rated for at least 100 pounds. We verify the mount sits level and secure before calling the job done.
