Miami's architecture tells the story of our city's rich history and cultural diversity. From Art Deco gems on South Beach to Mediterranean Revival mansions in Coral Gables, these are the most fascinating properties that have shaped our community through decades of growth and change. As local movers, we've helped families relocate near many of these landmarks, and each neighborhood offers its own architectural character.

## 1. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens

James Deering's 1916 Italian Renaissance villa at 3251 South Miami Avenue in Coconut Grove took 1,000 workers to build. The 34-room estate features European antiques, formal gardens, and a stone barge in Biscayne Bay. Today, the museum continues receiving loaned artworks for special exhibitions.

## 2. The Freedom Tower

At 600 Biscayne Boulevard, this 255-foot Mediterranean Revival tower opened in 1925 as The Miami News headquarters. From 1962 to 1974, it processed over 650,000 Cuban refugees, earning its nickname "Miami's Ellis Island." Now a National Historic Landmark housing Miami Dade College's Museum of Art and Design.

## 3. The Biltmore Hotel

George Merrick's 1926 Coral Gables centerpiece at 1200 Anastasia Avenue features a 315-foot tower modeled after Seville's Giralda. The hotel has served as a military hospital, VA facility, and university building before its 1987 restoration. Its pool remains the largest hotel pool in the continental United States.

## 4. Art Deco Historic District

Between 5th and 23rd Streets on Ocean Drive, Collins Avenue, and Washington Avenue, South Beach contains over 800 Art Deco buildings from the 1930s and 40s. The Colony Hotel at 736 Ocean Drive, the Breakwater at 940 Ocean Drive, and the Carlyle at 1250 Ocean Drive exemplify the pastel-and-neon style that defines Miami Beach.

## 5. Coral Gables City Hall

Phineas Paist designed this 1928 Mediterranean Revival building at 405 Biltmore Way with a semi-circular colonnade, Spanish barrel tile roof, and clock tower that chimes every fifteen minutes. It remains the city's government center after nearly a century of continuous use.

## 6. The Fontainebleau

Morris Lapidus opened this Miami Beach landmark at 4441 Collins Avenue in 1954. The "Stairway to Nowhere" and distinctive "cheese hole" facade influenced hotel design worldwide. Frank Sinatra, Elvis, and the Rat Pack performed here, and James Bond visited in "Goldfinger." A $1 billion renovation completed in 2008.

## 7. The Deering Estate

Charles Deering built this 1922 estate at 16701 SW 72nd Avenue in Palmetto Bay (formerly Cutler). The Mediterranean Revival complex sits on 444 acres with natural pine rockland, mangroves, and archaeological sites dating back 10,000 years. Now a county park offering nature trails and kayak launches.

![Historic Lincoln Theatre In Miami Beach  2](/images/blog/2030/08/9-legendary-buildings-that-define-miami/historic-lincoln-theatre-in-miami-beach--2.webp)


## 8. The Alfred I. duPont Building

Marsh and Saxelbye designed this 17-story Art Deco tower at 169 East Flagler Street, which opened in 1939 as the Florida National Bank headquarters. The ornate lobby features stunning brass elevator doors, terrazzo floors, and a ceiling mural depicting Florida's history. Listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

## 9. The Miami Marine Stadium

Hilario Candela designed this 1963 folded-plate concrete structure at 3501 Rickenbacker Causeway in Key Biscayne. The first purpose-built stadium for powerboat racing, it seats 6,566 spectators in a 326-foot-wide grandstand cantilevered over the water. Closed since Hurricane Andrew in 1992, restoration efforts continue.
