# 6 Types of Pianos and Their Moving Challenges

Pianos come in more varieties than most people realize, and each type presents different challenges when it's time to move. Weight, shape, internal construction, and fragility vary significantly across piano types. Knowing what you have helps you understand what's involved in getting it safely from one Miami home to another. Here are six common types and what makes each one unique to move.

## 1. Spinet Piano

The spinet is the smallest type of upright piano, standing just 36 to 40 inches tall and weighing 200 to 300 pounds. Spinets were popular from the 1930s through the 1970s as affordable home instruments, and many still show up in South Florida homes.

![Detailed View Of Piano Internal Mechanis 5](/images/blog/2028/06/6-types-of-pianos-and-their-moving-challenges/detailed-view-of-piano-internal-mechanis-5.webp)


**Moving challenges:** Spinets are the easiest pianos to move. Their compact size fits through standard doorways without angling, and their weight is manageable for a two-person crew. The main risk is the drop action mechanism, which hangs below the keys rather than sitting behind them like in taller uprights. This action is more delicate and can be knocked out of alignment by rough handling. The other concern is that many spinets are older instruments with weakened glue joints and fragile cabinetry, so they need gentle treatment despite their small size.

## 2. Console and Studio Upright

Console pianos stand 40 to 44 inches tall, and studio uprights range from 44 to 52 inches. Both weigh between 350 and 500 pounds. These are the most common acoustic pianos in Miami homes and apartments.

**Moving challenges:** The weight is the primary factor. A 450-pound studio upright on a staircase requires a strong, coordinated crew with stair-climbing equipment. The taller profile makes them slightly top-heavy during tilting and maneuvering through doorways. Console and studio uprights stay in their upright position throughout the move, which simplifies the process compared to grand pianos. In Miami's older neighborhoods like Little Havana and Wynwood, walkup apartments with narrow stairs make these moves physically demanding.

## 3. Full-Size Upright (Professional Upright)

Full-size uprights stand 48 to 60 inches tall and weigh 500 to 800 pounds. These are serious instruments with longer strings and larger soundboards than their shorter cousins, producing a richer, louder sound.

![Enchanting Scene Of A Grand Piano Amidst 4](/images/blog/2028/06/6-types-of-pianos-and-their-moving-challenges/enchanting-scene-of-a-grand-piano-amidst-4.webp)


**Moving challenges:** Weight is a major factor. An 800-pound upright going down stairs is one of the most physically demanding piano moves. These instruments are also tall enough that ceiling height becomes a concern in basements with low ceilings and in freight elevators with restricted overhead clearance. The back panel on full-size uprights is large and can flex if not properly supported during transport. ## 4. Baby Grand Piano

Baby grands range from 4'11" to 5'7" in length and weigh 500 to 600 pounds. They're the most popular grand piano size for Miami homes, fitting well in living rooms and music rooms without overwhelming the space.

**Moving challenges:** Every baby grand move requires leg removal, pedal lyre detachment, and placement on a piano board. The piano is tipped onto its side for transport, which means the crew needs to support the full weight during the transition. The curved body and the lid (which can be scratched during the tipping process) require careful padding. In Miami condos with tight elevators and narrow hallways, getting a baby grand on a board through the building requires precise measurement and maneuvering.

## 5. Grand Piano (Standard and Concert)

Standard grands run from 5'7" to 7' in length. Concert grands are 7' to 9'+ and can weigh over 1,200 pounds. These instruments are found in performance venues, recording studios, and larger homes throughout Coral Gables, Pinecrest, and Key Biscayne.

![Man Playing A Grand Piano On Stage Creat 1](/images/blog/2028/06/6-types-of-pianos-and-their-moving-challenges/man-playing-a-grand-piano-on-stage-creat-1.webp)


**Moving challenges:** Everything about a baby grand move applies here, amplified by size and weight. A concert grand on a piano board is a massive load that requires a four-person crew and a truck with a wide ramp. The longer body means more surface area to protect, more weight to manage during the tipping process, and bigger challenges fitting through doorways and elevators. Some concert grands are simply too large for standard freight elevators and require crane lifts to upper floors.

## 6. Digital Piano (Cabinet Style)

Cabinet-style digital pianos like the Yamaha Clavinova or Kawai CA series weigh 150 to 250 pounds. They look similar to upright acoustic pianos but use electronic sound generation instead of strings and hammers.

**Moving challenges:** Weight is manageable but not trivial. The real concerns are the electronics (circuit boards, speakers, and wiring sensitive to moisture and impact), the display screens that crack under pressure, and the pedal assembly connected by wiring through the legs. In Miami, humidity is the biggest threat, as condensation on circuit boards during the walk from building to truck can cause failures weeks after the move. Cabinet-style digitals need to be disassembled (legs removed, cables disconnected) and wrapped with extra care around screens and control panels.
