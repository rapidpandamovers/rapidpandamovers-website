Miami's September architecture tour starts here. From Art Deco gems to Mediterranean Revival mansions, these are the most fascinating properties that have shaped our city through decades of transformation.

## 1. Vizcaya Museum and Gardens

This 1916 Italian Renaissance-style estate sits on Biscayne Bay in Coconut Grove. Built for industrialist James Deering, the mansion required hundreds of workers and artisans from Europe to construct. The 34-room main house contains original furnishings from the 15th through 19th centuries, with many pieces shipped directly from European castles. The 10-acre formal gardens feature sculptures, fountains, and an ornate stone barge in the bay.

## 2. The Freedom Tower

Known as "Miami's Ellis Island," this 1925 building on Biscayne Boulevard in downtown Miami originally served as the headquarters for The Miami News. Its Mediterranean Revival tower, designed by Schultze and Weaver, reaches 255 feet. Between 1962 and 1974, the federal government used it to process Cuban refugees fleeing Castro's regime. Today it houses the Miami Dade College Museum of Art and Design, and remains a powerful symbol of the Cuban exile experience that shaped modern Miami.

## 3. The Biltmore Hotel

This 1926 Coral Gables landmark rises from the heart of George Merrick's planned community. The 315-foot tower, modeled after the Giralda bell tower in Seville, Spain, dominated the South Florida skyline for decades. During World War II, the Army converted it into a military hospital, and it later served as a VA hospital and then the University of Miami's School of Medicine. After sitting vacant for years, a $55 million restoration returned it to hotel use in 1987. The hotel's massive pool was once the largest in the world, where Johnny Weissmuller broke swimming records and taught children to swim.

## 4. Art Deco Historic District

South Beach's Art Deco Historic District stretches along Ocean Drive, Collins Avenue, and Washington Avenue. The 960+ buildings constructed between 1923 and 1943 represent the largest collection of Art Deco architecture in the world. Designed by architects like Henry Hohauser and L. Murray Dixon, these pastel-colored hotels and apartment buildings feature porthole windows, racing stripes, and geometric patterns inspired by cruise ships and the machine age. The Miami Design Preservation League saved these buildings from demolition in the 1970s when Barbara Capitman led the fight to preserve them.

## 5. Coral Gables City Hall

Built in 1928, this Mediterranean Revival building at the corner of Biltmore Way and Coral Way served as the center of George Merrick's "City Beautiful." The three-story structure features a grand staircase, ornate columns, and a bell tower that chimes the hours. Architect Phineas Paist designed the building to match Merrick's vision of a Spanish-inspired planned community. Inside, historic murals by Denman Fink depict scenes from Coral Gables' founding. The building still houses city offices and the Commission Chamber where local government meets.

## 6. The Barnacle Historic State Park

Florida's oldest house in its original location sits on five acres along Biscayne Bay in Coconut Grove. Commodore Ralph Munroe built this home in 1891 using timber salvaged from shipwrecks. The house's unique design includes a central skylight for ventilation in the days before air conditioning, and Munroe actually raised the entire structure and added a new first floor when his family grew. The surrounding tropical hardwood hammock looks much as it did when Coconut Grove was just a pioneer settlement. Monthly moonlight concerts draw crowds to the waterfront grounds.

## 7. Plymouth Congregational Church

This 1917 coral rock church on Devon Road in Coconut Grove was built by hand by one man, Felix Rebom, over 12 years. The massive wooden door at the entrance came from a 400-year-old Spanish monastery. George Merrick, who later developed Coral Gables, married his wife Eunice here, and his father served as the church's pastor. The surrounding grounds include lush gardens, a historic schoolhouse, and some of the oldest trees in Miami-Dade County. The church remains an active congregation and hosts community events throughout the year.
