# 5 Art Moving Supplies You Didn't Know You Needed

Most people know they need bubble wrap and boxes to move artwork. But after years of relocating collections across Miami, from waterfront mansions on Star Island to studios in the Wynwood Arts District, our team relies on a handful of specialty supplies that most people have never heard of. These items make a real difference in how well your art survives the trip.

## 1. Glassine Paper

Glassine is a smooth, glossy, semi-transparent paper that's acid-free and moisture-resistant. Museums use it to interleave prints, separate stacked photographs, and wrap paintings before storage. Unlike plastic wrap, glassine lets artwork breathe while still protecting the surface from fingerprints, dust, and moisture transfer. It's especially useful for oil paintings, where any material that sticks to the surface can pull pigment when removed. A roll costs around $15 to $30 and covers a surprising number of pieces. If you're moving art in Miami, glassine should be your first layer of protection, sitting between the paint surface and everything else.

## 2. Silica Gel Packets

You've seen these small packets inside shoe boxes and electronics packaging. For art moves in South Florida, they serve a much more serious purpose. Tucked inside sealed wrapping or crates, silica gel absorbs excess moisture and helps maintain stable humidity around the artwork. During a Miami move, your art transitions from 50% indoor humidity to 80%+ outdoor humidity and back again. Those rapid shifts cause condensation, warping, and mold. Industrial-grade silica gel packets rated for specific square footage are available online and at specialty packing suppliers. A few dollars' worth can prevent hundreds in restoration costs.

## 3. Foam Corner Protectors

These L-shaped foam pieces slide over the corners of framed artwork, and they solve a problem that bubble wrap alone cannot. Frame corners are the most impact-prone point during transport. A corner that bumps against a doorframe during carry-out or shifts against another piece in the truck can crack, chip, or dent. Foam corner protectors absorb that shock without adding bulk. They come in various sizes for different frame depths. For ornate gilded frames common in many Coral Gables and Coconut Grove homes, these protectors are practically mandatory.

## 4. Tyvek Wrap

Tyvek is a synthetic material that's tear-resistant, water-resistant, and breathable. It's the same material used as house wrap in construction. For art moving, Tyvek works as an outer wrapping layer that shields artwork from dust, dirt, and light moisture without trapping humidity inside. It's tougher than tissue paper and more breathable than plastic, making it ideal for wrapping sculptures, large frames, and items that will spend time in storage between moves. Tyvek sheets and rolls are available from art supply and industrial packing retailers.

## 5. Acid-Free Corrugated Cardboard

Regular cardboard contains lignin and other acids that can transfer to artwork over time, causing brown staining called "foxing" on paper and discoloration on fabrics and mats. Acid-free corrugated board looks and feels like normal cardboard but won't cause chemical damage. Use it to create rigid support boards for unframed works on paper, build dividers between stacked pieces, or construct custom sleeves for oddly shaped items. It costs more per sheet than standard corrugated, but for artwork worth protecting, the upgrade pays for itself immediately.
