# 5 Kitchen Appliances That Need Special Moving Prep

Not every kitchen appliance can be unplugged and loaded onto a truck. Some need hours or days of preparation, specialized disconnection, or specific transport positions. We've moved thousands of kitchens across Miami-Dade County, and these five appliances cause the most problems when people skip the prep work.

## 1. Refrigerator

The refrigerator tops the list because it needs the longest lead time. You can't just unplug it and roll it out. The defrosting process alone takes 24-48 hours, and in Miami's humidity, it produces more meltwater than people expect. You need to empty all food, remove shelves and drawers (wrapping each one separately), disconnect the water line to the ice maker, and tape the power cord to the back. The doors need to be strapped or taped shut since the magnetic seal won't hold during transport when the truck brakes or turns. If you skip defrosting, you'll have water leaking through the truck and onto your other belongings during the drive from Doral to your new place in Aventura.

The transport position matters too. Refrigerators must stay upright. Laying one on its side sends compressor oil into the cooling lines, and plugging it in before the oil settles back can kill the compressor. That's a $500-$1,000 repair.

## 2. Gas Range

Any appliance connected to a gas line requires professional disconnection. In Miami-Dade County, building codes are strict about gas work, especially in multi-unit buildings. A gas range needs the supply valve turned off, the flexible gas connector disconnected, and the open gas pipe capped. Doing this incorrectly risks a gas leak, which is a safety hazard that can endanger your family and your neighbors.

Beyond the gas line, you need to remove all oven racks, burner grates, drip pans, and control knobs. These pieces break, scratch, and get lost when left attached. Pack them in a labeled box. Clean the oven interior before loading it into the truck. Old grease in a hot moving truck (Miami summer truck temps hit 140+ degrees) bakes into a hardened mess that's nearly impossible to clean later.

## 3. Dishwasher

People forget that dishwashers hold a surprising amount of water even after the last cycle. The pump, the hoses, and the internal tub all retain water. Run an empty hot cycle the night before moving, then disconnect the water supply and drain hose, letting both drain into a bucket. Leave the door cracked overnight to air dry.

If the dishwasher is built into the cabinetry (standard in most Miami kitchens), you also need to remove the mounting screws from the top that attach it to the countertop. Pull it out carefully since the water supply line and drain hose run through a hole in the cabinet wall and can snag or kink. Missing the mounting screws means you'll be tugging on a unit that won't budge, and forcing it damages the countertop.

## 4. Wine Refrigerator

Wine fridges have smaller, more sensitive compressors than standard refrigerators. They also typically have glass doors and wooden shelves that are fragile during transport. Remove all bottles and shelves before moving. The bottles need to be packed separately in cell boxes and transported in a climate-controlled environment. A car with AC works. A 130-degree moving truck in August does not.

The wine fridge itself must stay upright during the entire move. Even a slight tilt beyond 45 degrees can displace the compressor oil. And because these compressors are smaller, they're less tolerant of running without full lubrication. Let the unit sit upright for 4-24 hours at the new home before plugging in, depending on how upright it stayed during transport.

Many homes in Coral Gables, Pinecrest, and Key Biscayne have built-in wine storage units that are mounted into cabinetry. These need the same removal process as other built-in appliances: remove trim panels, disconnect power, remove mounting hardware, and slide the unit out carefully.

## 5. Built-In Cooktop

Built-in cooktops, whether gas, electric, or induction, sit in a cutout in your countertop. They're held in place by mounting clips underneath the counter surface. Removing one means accessing the clips from below (inside the cabinet), releasing them, and lifting the cooktop straight up out of the cutout.

The risks here are specific: scratch the countertop edge during removal and you're paying for countertop repair or replacement. For granite and quartz countertops, common in renovated Miami kitchens, that cost runs $300-$1,000+ depending on the size of the damage. Gas cooktops also have a gas supply line running from below that needs professional disconnection.

Electric and induction cooktops are hardwired through a junction box underneath. Turn off the breaker, disconnect the wiring, and cap the wires before lifting the cooktop. Label which wire goes where so reconnection at the new home goes smoothly.
