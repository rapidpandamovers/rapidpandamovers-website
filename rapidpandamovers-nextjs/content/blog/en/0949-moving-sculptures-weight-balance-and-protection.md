# Moving Sculptures: Weight, Balance, and Protection

Sculptures are the hardest artwork to move. Unlike flat paintings that slide neatly into boxes, sculptures have irregular shapes, unpredictable centers of gravity, and surfaces that can chip, crack, or scratch from a single bump. When you add weight into the equation, a marble bust or a large bronze figure can weigh hundreds of pounds, and the challenge goes up another level. Our team has moved sculptures ranging from tabletop ceramics in South Beach apartments to monumental outdoor pieces on Fisher Island estates, and every one requires a unique approach.

## Understanding Weight Distribution

A painting's weight distributes evenly across its surface. A sculpture concentrates weight unevenly, often in ways that aren't obvious. A bronze figure with outstretched arms puts most of its weight on the legs and base, but the extended arms create a long lever that amplifies any tipping force. A stone piece with a narrow pedestal base is top-heavy by design.

![Grayscale Image Of A Classic Sculpture I 5](/images/blog/2028/05/moving-sculptures-weight-balance-and-protection/grayscale-image-of-a-classic-sculpture-i-5.webp)


Before we wrap anything, we study how the sculpture sits. Where's the center of gravity? Which direction does it want to fall? Where are the stress points? This tells us how to orient the piece for packing and transport and how many crew members we need for the carry.

## Disassembly When Possible

Many sculptures have removable components. A figure might sit on a separate base. An abstract piece might have arms or extensions that detach with a pin or bolt. Removing these parts before transport reduces the chance of breakage and makes the piece lighter and more compact.

We label and photograph every component and its connection point before disassembly. Each removed piece gets wrapped individually and packed with a corresponding label so reassembly goes smoothly at the destination.

## Wrapping and Padding Techniques

Start with acid-free tissue over any finished surface. This prevents chemical reactions between the sculpture's material and the packing materials. Follow with soft foam sheeting wrapped around the entire piece, paying extra attention to protruding elements like fingers, noses, handles, or sharp edges.

For heavy stone and metal pieces, foam alone isn't enough. We wrap the piece in foam, then build a rigid support structure around it using cardboard, plywood, or custom crate walls depending on the weight.

Delicate elements get individual treatment. A ceramic figure's outstretched hand, for example, gets wrapped in tissue, then cushioned with a foam pocket specifically shaped around it.

## Custom Crating for Heavy Pieces

Any sculpture over about 50 pounds needs a custom crate. We build these on-site from plywood, with internal foam cradles carved to match the sculpture's contours. The piece sits in the cradle with zero room to shift, and the crate provides rigid protection against impact.

![Indoor Staircase In A Museum With Sculpt 2](/images/blog/2028/05/moving-sculptures-weight-balance-and-protection/indoor-staircase-in-a-museum-with-sculpt-2.webp)


For very heavy pieces, we reinforce the crate floor with extra plywood layers and add handles or lifting points so the crew can move the loaded crate safely. Some crates get mounted on skids so they can be moved with a pallet jack.

## Safe Carrying and Loading

Heavy sculptures require equipment. Furniture dollies, appliance hand trucks, lifting straps, and sometimes rigging gear for pieces that weigh several hundred pounds. Our crews use proper lifting techniques and always have enough people on hand so no one is overloaded.

In Miami's high-rise buildings, we plan the route from the apartment to the truck in advance. Elevator weight limits, hallway widths, and loading dock access all factor into the plan. Some outdoor sculptures on waterfront terraces require crane access for removal, which we coordinate with local rigging companies.
