# Common Pool Table Moving Mistakes to Avoid

We've been moving pool tables across Miami for years, and we've seen the aftermath of just about every mistake you can make. Some homeowners call us to fix a table that was poorly moved by another company. Others call after a failed DIY attempt. The same mistakes come up again and again. Here's what to watch out for so your table arrives at your new home in the same condition it left the old one.

## Trying to Move the Table Without Disassembly

This is the most damaging mistake, and it happens more than you'd think. Someone figures they can tip the table on its side, load it onto a trailer, and drive it across town. The result is almost always cracked slate, split frame joints, and bent rail bolts. A pool table is engineered to bear weight straight down through its legs. Tipping it sideways puts stress on joints that were never designed for lateral force. The slate shifts, the frame flexes, and things break.

There's no shortcut here. Full disassembly is required for any move that involves a truck. Our [**Pool Table Moving**](/pool-table-moving) team disassembles every table before transport, no exceptions.

## Skipping the Pre-Move Measurement

Your pool table came into your current room somehow, so it'll fit out the same way, right? Not necessarily. If the table was assembled in the room by a delivery crew who brought the pieces in through the door, taking it apart and carrying it out should work. But we've seen tables that were assembled before the walls went up (basement renovations, new construction), or that came in through a window or patio door that's since been replaced. Measuring the exit path before moving day avoids finding out the hard way that a 30-inch-wide slate piece won't fit through a 28-inch doorway.

## Using General Movers Instead of Pool Table Specialists

Your household movers might be great with boxes, sofas, and appliances. Pool tables are a different skill set. General movers often don't have slate jacks, don't know the correct rail bolt removal sequence, and may not have a machinist's level for proper reassembly. We regularly get calls to re-level tables that were "set up" by non-specialist crews who used a phone app level (not accurate enough) or skipped leveling entirely.

## Folding the Felt

Pool table cloth should always be rolled, never folded. A fold creates a crease that affects ball roll and is extremely difficult to smooth out. In Miami's humid conditions, a fold line can also become a moisture trap that leads to mildew. When our crew saves the felt, it goes around a cardboard tube, wrapped in clean cotton, and stored where nothing will crush it.

## Stacking Slate Pieces Flat

Laying slate pieces flat in a truck bed and stacking them seems logical since they're flat surfaces, after all. But slate is brittle, and road vibrations can cause the pieces to shift and grind against each other. Over a long enough drive, this can chip edges or create hairline fractures at stress points. Slate travels upright, individually wrapped, and strapped to the truck wall.

## Forgetting Hardware

A pool table has dozens of bolts, screws, nuts, and washers. Lose even a few rail bolts and you're scrambling to find replacements that match the thread pitch and length. We label every piece of hardware by position and tape the bags directly to the component they belong to. It takes an extra 10 minutes during disassembly and saves hours of frustration during reassembly.

## Ignoring Humidity During Transport

Even a 20-minute drive in Miami can expose your table components to moisture. Loading during a summer rain shower, or leaving the truck door open in high humidity, allows moisture to reach the felt and wood. We wrap components in moisture barriers and limit exposure time, especially from June through November.

## Rushing the Leveling

After a long disassembly and drive, there's a temptation to bolt everything together and call it done. Leveling is the final step, and it's where most of the value is. Spending 15 minutes with a precision level and micro-adjustable shims means every ball rolls true from the first break. Skipping this step means you'll notice every slight tilt every time you play.

## Not Planning for the New Floor

The floor in your new home is not the same as the floor in your old one. Concrete slab, tile, hardwood, carpet, floating laminate, each surface behaves differently under an 800-pound table. We level the table to the new floor specifically, accounting for any slopes, soft spots, or irregularities. If the table is going on carpet, we warn owners to expect a re-leveling in 2-3 weeks after the carpet compresses.
