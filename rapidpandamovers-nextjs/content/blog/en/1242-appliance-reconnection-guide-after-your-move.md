# Appliance Reconnection Guide After Your Move

You made it to the new place. The truck is unloaded, the appliances are sitting in their spots, and now you need everything working again. Reconnecting appliances after a move is where people rush and make costly mistakes. A cross-threaded water line fitting leaks behind the fridge for days before you notice. A dryer vent that's kinked behind the unit becomes a fire hazard. A gas line that isn't properly sealed sends natural gas into your kitchen. Take the time to reconnect each appliance correctly, and your first night in the new home goes smoothly.

## Before You Start

### Check the Hookups

![Hands Washing A Fresh Red Apple Under Ru 4](/images/blog/2029/10/appliance-reconnection-guide-after-your-move/hands-washing-a-fresh-red-apple-under-ru-4.webp)


Walk through the new home and confirm that each appliance's location has the right connections. Check for:

- **Electrical outlets**: Does the fridge spot have a standard 120V outlet? Does the oven location have a 240V outlet? Is it a 3-prong or 4-prong connection?
- **Water supply valves**: Are there working shutoff valves behind the fridge (for the ice maker) and near the washer location?
- **Gas connections**: If you have gas appliances, are there gas shutoff valves at each appliance location?
- **Dryer venting**: Is there a vent port in the wall, and is the duct clear?

In older Miami homes, especially in neighborhoods like Shenandoah, Silver Bluff, and parts of Hialeah, wiring and plumbing may not match modern standards. A house built in the 1950s might have a 3-prong oven outlet when your range needs a 4-prong connection. This is a simple fix (replace the cord or the outlet), but you need to know before you start pushing appliances into place.

### Gather Your Tools

- Adjustable wrench
- Pliers
- Screwdriver set
- Teflon tape (for water connections)
- Pipe joint compound (for gas connections)
- Bucket and towels
- Level
- Voltage tester
- Your photos of the original connections

## Reconnecting Each Appliance

### Refrigerator

![Stylish Commercial Washing Machines In A 3](/images/blog/2029/10/appliance-reconnection-guide-after-your-move/stylish-commercial-washing-machines-in-a-3.webp)


Position the fridge near the outlet and water supply but leave enough room to work behind it. If the fridge was tilted during the move, let it sit upright for 2-4 hours before plugging in. This allows the compressor oil to settle back into the compressor.

Connect the water supply line to the shutoff valve using Teflon tape on the threads. Hand-tighten, then give it a quarter turn with a wrench. Don't overtighten. Turn on the water slowly and watch the connection for any drips. Plug in the fridge and set the temperature to the manufacturer's recommended setting (typically 37 degrees for the fridge, 0 degrees for the freezer). It takes 24 hours to reach full operating temperature, so don't load food until then.

### Washer

Remove the transit bolts. This step is easy to forget after a long moving day, but running a washer with transit bolts installed will damage the suspension system. Store the bolts somewhere you can find them for future moves.

Connect the hot and cold water hoses to the correct wall valves (they're color-coded). Use Teflon tape and tighten by hand plus a quarter turn. Turn on both valves slowly while watching for leaks. Connect the drain hose to the standpipe or laundry sink. Make sure it's inserted at least 6 inches into the standpipe but not sealed airtight, as it needs an air gap to drain properly. Level the washer using the adjustable feet and a bubble level. An unlevel washer vibrates, walks across the floor, and wears out bearings faster. Run an empty cycle to check for leaks and proper drainage.

### Dryer

Attach the vent hose to the back of the dryer using a proper clamp (not duct tape, which deteriorates and lets lint escape). Connect the other end to the wall vent. Keep the vent run as short and straight as possible. Every bend in the hose reduces airflow.

For electric dryers, plug into the 240V outlet. If the plug doesn't match the outlet, you'll need either a new dryer cord or a new outlet. Both are straightforward jobs for an electrician.

For gas dryers, have a licensed professional reconnect the gas supply line. They'll test for leaks using a gas leak detector solution. This is not a DIY job. Gas leaks in enclosed laundry areas are dangerous.

### Dishwasher

Slide the dishwasher into its cabinet space but don't push it all the way in yet. Connect the water supply line under the sink to the hot water valve. Connect the drain hose to the garbage disposal or the sink drain (check your photo from the old home for the correct setup). Run the water supply briefly to check for leaks. Push the dishwasher into position and secure it to the underside of the countertop with the mounting screws. Run a test cycle.

### Oven and Range

For electric ranges, simply plug into the 240V outlet and test all burners and the oven. Reinstall racks, grates, drip pans, and knobs.

For gas ranges, have a professional reconnect the gas supply line. Once connected, test each burner by turning it on and checking for a clean, blue flame. An orange or yellow flame indicates a problem. Test the oven at 350 degrees and verify it reaches temperature.

## Testing Everything

After reconnecting all appliances, run through this checklist:

- [ ] Fridge is cooling and water/ice dispenser works
- [ ] Washer completes a full cycle without leaks or excessive vibration
- [ ] Dryer heats up and air flows through the vent to the exterior
- [ ] Dishwasher fills, washes, and drains without leaking
- [ ] All oven burners ignite and oven reaches temperature
- [ ] No gas smell near any gas appliance
