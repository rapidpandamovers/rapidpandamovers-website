# How Movers Handle Extremely Heavy Items

There's a line where regular moving turns into a rigging job. That line sits somewhere around 400 pounds. Above that weight, standard hand trucks and two-person lifts give way to specialized equipment, engineering calculations, and crews trained to move objects that could seriously injure someone or destroy property if handled wrong. In Miami homes, we cross that line more often than you'd think.

## What Counts as Extremely Heavy

Gun safes top the list. A Liberty Colonial series safe runs 800-1,200 pounds. Commercial safes can exceed 2,000. After safes, we see marble and granite slabs (300-600 pounds), cast iron wood stoves (400-700 pounds), commercial kitchen equipment (commercial ranges hit 500-800 pounds), grand pianos (700-1,200 pounds), and hot tubs (600-900 pounds empty). These aren't items you can wrestle through a doorway with muscle alone.

## Pre-Move Engineering

Before we touch anything, we survey the entire path from the item's current location to the truck and from the truck to the destination. We measure doorways, hallway widths, stair angles, floor construction, and load-bearing capacity. In older Miami homes, especially in areas like the Roads and Little Havana, the floors may be terrazzo over concrete, which handles weight well. In newer construction with engineered wood subflooring, we calculate whether the floor can support a 1,500-pound safe without structural risk.

## The Equipment

Heavy-item moving uses a different toolkit entirely. Our standard gear for these jobs includes: heavy-duty piano boards rated for 1,500 pounds, appliance dollies with stair-climbing treads, 4-point ratchet strap systems, hydraulic lift gates on the truck, furniture sliders rated for 1,000+ pounds, plywood sheets for floor protection, and Johnson bars (heavy pry bars) for initial positioning. For items over 1,500 pounds or situations involving stairs and tight turns, we bring in a gantry crane or coordinate with a crane service.

## The Crew

Heavy items need more people, but adding bodies doesn't help if they're not trained. We assign experienced leads who understand weight distribution, center of gravity, and tipping points. A four-person crew working in sync with proper equipment moves a heavy safe more safely than six people trying to muscle it. Everyone wears steel-toed boots and back support belts. One person calls every move out loud so the team operates as a unit.

## Handling Stairs

Stairs multiply the difficulty by a factor of three. Going up, gravity fights you on every step. Going down, gravity wants to pull the item away from the crew. We use stair-climbing dollies with motorized treads for items up to 1,200 pounds. Beyond that, we rig a controlled lowering system using straps, pulleys, and anchor points. In some Brickell high-rises, the freight elevator is the only option, and we verify weight capacity with building management before moving day.

## Protecting the Home

A 1,000-pound safe on a furniture dolly puts enormous pressure on small wheel contact points. That pressure destroys tile, dents hardwood, and cracks pavers. We lay down plywood runways over every surface the heavy item crosses. At transitions between flooring types, we add extra padding. Doorframes get padded with corner guards. Walls along tight hallways get blanket protection. The goal is zero damage to the home, because a cracked marble threshold costs more to fix than the moving fee.
