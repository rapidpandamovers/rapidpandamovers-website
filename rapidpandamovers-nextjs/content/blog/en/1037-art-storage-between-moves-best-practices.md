# Art Storage Between Moves: Best Practices

Sometimes the timing doesn't line up. You've sold your Coconut Grove home, but the new place in Coral Gables won't be ready for another three months. Your art collection needs somewhere safe to wait. Storing artwork between moves is more involved than stacking boxes in a garage. Done wrong, you can cause more damage in storage than during the actual move.

## Why Regular Storage Units Fall Short

A standard self-storage unit is basically a metal box with a roll-up door. Temperatures inside can swing from 60 degrees on a winter night to well over 100 degrees on a summer afternoon. Humidity in South Florida means condensation builds up on any cool surface as the unit heats up during the day. That moisture cycle, repeated day after day, warps wood, grows mold, foxes paper, and degrades adhesives.

Pests are another concern. Standard units in Miami see roaches, silverfish, and occasionally rodents. Silverfish feed on paper and glue, which means your matted prints and canvas linings are literally on the menu.

## Climate-Controlled Storage Is Non-Negotiable

For artwork, climate-controlled storage is the only option that makes sense. These units maintain temperatures between 65 and 78 degrees and keep humidity below 55%. That stability prevents the expansion-contraction cycles that crack paint and warp frames.

When choosing a climate-controlled facility, verify that the system runs 24/7, not just during business hours. Ask about backup power. A facility that loses climate control during a Miami power outage from a summer storm defeats the purpose.

## How to Prepare Art for Storage

Even in a climate-controlled unit, artwork needs proper preparation. Wrap each piece in acid-free tissue or glassine, then add a layer of breathable wrapping like Tyvek. Avoid sealing art in plastic bags or shrink wrap, which traps moisture inside and creates a microclimate that encourages mold growth.

Paintings should stand upright on padded blocks, not lean directly against the wall or floor. Use wooden pallets or foam risers to keep everything off the concrete. Store paintings face to face and back to back with cardboard dividers between them. This protects the painted surfaces while making efficient use of space.

Sculptures should stay in their custom crates if possible. If they were uncrated, re-wrap them with foam padding and place them on stable surfaces where they can't be bumped or tipped.

## Inventory and Access

Create a detailed inventory of everything going into storage, with photos and condition notes taken at the time of packing. Label each piece or crate clearly so you can find specific items without unwrapping everything.

Limit access to the unit. Every time the door opens, outside air floods in. In Miami, that means a blast of hot, humid air that takes the climate control system time to counteract. Plan your visits and retrieve what you need in a single trip rather than making multiple quick stops.

## Insurance While in Storage

Check whether your fine art insurance policy covers items in storage. Many policies require that the storage facility meet certain conditions, such as climate control, fire suppression, and security systems. Some policies have specific exclusions for self-storage facilities. If your current coverage doesn't extend to storage, ask your insurer about a rider or look into the facility's own insurance options.

## How Long Is Too Long?

There's no hard expiration date, but artwork in storage should be checked periodically. We recommend inspecting your collection every three to six months. Look for signs of moisture, pests, shifting, or wrapping degradation. Replace silica gel packets if they've been in place for more than a few months.
