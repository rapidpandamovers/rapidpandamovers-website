You just got the keys to your new Miami home. Before you start unpacking boxes and hanging pictures, there's something more urgent on your list: security. You have no idea how many copies of those keys exist. Previous owners, their family members, contractors, cleaning crews, real estate agents, and staging companies may all have had access at some point.

Here are the 9 security features to set up before your first night in your new place.

## 1. Change All Locks Immediately

This is non-negotiable. Previous owners, contractors, and real estate agents may have copies of keys floating around. In Miami neighborhoods like Coral Gables and Coconut Grove, homes often sit on the market for months with multiple showings, and that means dozens of people may have had access.

![A Person Holding A Cardboard Box Labeled 5](/images/blog/2027/04/9-essential-security-features-for-new-homes/a-person-holding-a-cardboard-box-labeled-5.webp)


Call a locksmith before moving day or buy new lock sets from Home Depot on Coral Way or the Lowes in Kendall. Smart locks with changeable codes work well for Miami's rental-heavy market since you can easily update access when guests or cleaning services change.

## 2. Check All Windows and Doors

Walk through every room and test each window latch and door lock. Miami homes, especially older ones in areas like Little Havana and Wynwood, often have original hardware that may be worn or broken. Jalousie windows, common in vintage Miami homes, are particularly vulnerable and may need upgraded security pins.

Check sliding glass doors too. These are everywhere in South Florida and can be lifted off their tracks if not properly secured. Add a security bar or install secondary locks at the top of the track.

## 3. Install or Update Security System

Whether you choose professional monitoring through ADT or a DIY system like Ring or SimpliSafe, get something active immediately. Miami-Dade Police report that homes without visible security systems are significantly more likely to be targeted.

For Miami condos in Brickell or Aventura high-rises, you may already have building security, but adding a doorbell camera and interior sensors provides extra protection. Many condo associations have rules about exterior modifications, so check with your HOA before installing anything visible.

## 4. Add Exterior Lighting

Motion-sensor lights around entry points deter intruders and help you move around safely at night. Miami's early sunset during winter months (around 5:30 PM) means you'll be coming home in the dark more often than you might expect.

![Smiling Woman Holding A Box Marked Fragi 1](/images/blog/2027/04/9-essential-security-features-for-new-homes/smiling-woman-holding-a-box-marked-fragi-1.webp)


Focus on the front door, garage, side yards, and any back patio areas. Solar-powered options work great in sunny South Florida and don't require an electrician. Place lights high enough that they can't be easily tampered with or unscrewed.

## 5. Test Smoke and CO Detectors

Replace batteries in all detectors on move-in day, even if they seem to work. If units are more than 10 years old, replace them entirely. Florida building codes require smoke detectors in every bedroom and on each floor of your home.

Carbon monoxide detectors are especially important if you have a gas stove, water heater, or attached garage. Miami homes with older gas appliances should have CO detectors on every level.

## 6. Secure the Garage

The garage is often the most vulnerable entry point. Program a new garage door opener and disable any previous remotes by resetting the system. Most openers have a "learn" button that clears old codes when held for 10-15 seconds.

If your Miami home has an interior door from the garage to the house, treat it like an exterior door. Install a solid-core door with a deadbolt, not just the basic passage lock many builders use. Many break-ins happen through the garage because homeowners leave this interior door unlocked.

## 7. Install Deadbolts

Upgrade to high-quality deadbolts on all exterior doors. Look for Grade 1 or Grade 2 ANSI-rated locks from brands like Schlage or Kwikset. These can withstand significant force compared to standard builder-grade hardware.

For Miami hurricane season, consider reinforced strike plates with 3-inch screws that go into the door frame studs, not just the trim. This protects against both break-ins and the impact of wind-blown debris.

## 8. Trim Landscaping Near Entry Points

South Florida's tropical climate means vegetation grows fast. Cut back bushes, palm fronds, and hedges near windows and doors. Dense vegetation provides hiding spots for intruders and blocks sight lines from the street.

![Two Movers Load A Van With Furniture Dur 3](/images/blog/2027/04/9-essential-security-features-for-new-homes/two-movers-load-a-van-with-furniture-dur-3.webp)


Many Miami neighborhoods like Pinecrest and Palmetto Bay have lush landscaping, but keep plants at least 3 feet from windows and below sill height. Thorny plants like bougainvillea near windows can actually add security while still looking beautiful.

## 9. Meet Your Neighbors

Building relationships with neighbors creates an informal watch network. They can alert you to suspicious activity, accept packages when you're at work, and keep an eye on things when you travel.

In Miami communities like Doral or Miami Lakes, many neighborhoods have active HOAs and community watch programs. Introduce yourself within the first week. You'll learn about any recent issues in the area and get the inside track on local services, from reliable locksmiths to recommended landscapers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does it cost to rekey or replace locks?

Rekeying existing locks runs about $15-25 per lock through a locksmith. Replacing with new smart locks costs $150-300 per door depending on features. Budget $200-500 total for a typical Miami home.

### Should I get monitored security or a DIY system?

Monitored systems (ADT, Brinks) cost $30-60/month but provide 24/7 response and may qualify you for homeowner's insurance discounts. DIY systems (Ring, SimpliSafe) cost less monthly but rely on you to check alerts. Many Miami homeowners use a combination of both.

### What about hurricane shutters and security?

Hurricane shutters add security year-round, not just during storms. Accordion shutters or roll-downs can be quickly deployed if you'll be away for an extended period, making your home look less vacant.
