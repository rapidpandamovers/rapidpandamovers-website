# Setting Up a Game Room in Your New Home

Moving to a new home is the perfect opportunity to build the game room you've always wanted. Whether you're bringing a pool table from your old place or buying one for the first time, setting up a proper game room takes planning. The room layout, climate control, lighting, and flooring all affect how your table plays and how much you enjoy the space. Here's our guide to getting it right, based on years of setting up game rooms across Miami.

## Choosing the Right Room

The best game room candidates in a typical South Florida home are:

![Close Up Of Billiard Balls And Cue Stick 1](/images/blog/2028/12/setting-up-a-game-room-in-your-new-home/close-up-of-billiard-balls-and-cue-stick-1.webp)


### Dedicated Bonus Rooms

If your new home has a bonus room, flex room, or loft space, this is often the best option. These rooms are typically 15x20 feet or larger, which comfortably fits an 8-foot table with full cue clearance on all sides. They're already climate-controlled and away from the main living areas, so noise from a lively game night won't bother anyone sleeping upstairs.

### Garage Conversions

Garage game rooms are a South Florida favorite. A two-car garage is roughly 20x20 feet, giving you ample space for a regulation table, a dart board, a bar area, and seating. The main investment is climate control. A non-insulated, non-air-conditioned garage in Miami will destroy your table's felt and warp the wood frame within months. Budget for a mini-split AC unit and proper insulation before you move the table in.

### Covered Patios (With Caveats)

Some Miami homeowners ask about putting a pool table on a covered patio or lanai. We generally advise against this unless the space is fully enclosed and climate-controlled. Open-air exposure to Miami's humidity, rain splash, and insects will ruin the table quickly. A screened patio with AC, on the other hand, can work if the ventilation is good.

### Rooms to Avoid

Skip any room where the table would block a primary walkway, where the ceiling is below 8 feet, or where the floor isn't structurally sound enough for the weight. Also avoid rooms with large west-facing windows that get direct afternoon sun, which heats the table unevenly and fades the felt.

## Room Layout Planning

Before our [**Pool Table Moving**](/pool-table-moving) crew arrives with your table, plan the layout:

### Table Position

Center the table in the room with equal clearance on all four sides. If the room isn't large enough for equal clearance, prioritize the long sides (where most shots are played) over the short ends. Leave at least 58 inches from each table edge to the nearest wall or obstacle for standard cue length.

### Spectator Area

Set up seating along one wall so spectators can watch without blocking the players. Bar-height tables and stools work well. Keep the seating area outside the cue-clearance zone.

### Cue Storage

Wall-mount a cue rack near the table but outside the playing area. Cues leaning against walls get knocked over, stepped on, and warped. A mounted rack keeps them organized and protected.

### Entertainment

Many game rooms include a TV, sound system, or dartboard. Mount these on the wall opposite the main seating area. Keep electronics away from direct proximity to the table to avoid accidental collisions.

## Climate Control Specifics

For a Miami game room, climate control is about more than comfort. It directly affects your table's condition:

![Competitive Billiards Game In A Modern P 2](/images/blog/2028/12/setting-up-a-game-room-in-your-new-home/competitive-billiards-game-in-a-modern-p-2.webp)


- **Temperature**: Keep the room between 68 and 76 degrees Fahrenheit consistently. Temperature swings cause wood expansion and contraction that can throw off your table's level.
- **Humidity**: Target 40-60% relative humidity. In Miami's climate, this means running AC and potentially adding a dehumidifier, especially during the summer months.
- **Air circulation**: Ceiling fans are fine for comfort but position them so they don't hang directly over the table where they could affect ball roll on delicate shots (this is mostly a concern for very high-speed fans).

## Lighting

Proper billiard lighting makes a huge difference in gameplay:

- **Billiard-specific fixtures**: A dedicated pool table light fixture hangs directly over the center of the table, 32-36 inches above the playing surface. This eliminates shadows cast by overhead can lights.
- **Even coverage**: The light should illuminate the entire playing surface without hot spots or dark corners. Multi-shade fixtures (like a three-shade bar light) provide better coverage than single-point lights.
- **Hire an electrician**: If there's no ceiling box directly above your planned table position, have an electrician install one before the table goes in. Running a new electrical box takes an hour; working around a table that's already assembled takes much longer.

## Flooring

Game room flooring needs to support the table's weight and provide a stable surface:

![Vibrant Billiard Balls Neatly Arranged O 3](/images/blog/2028/12/setting-up-a-game-room-in-your-new-home/vibrant-billiard-balls-neatly-arranged-o-3.webp)


- **Tile or concrete**: Both are excellent choices and common in Miami homes. Stable, flat, and strong.
- **Hardwood**: Works well. Use felt pads under the legs to prevent denting.
- **Luxury vinyl or laminate**: Check that it's installed over a solid subfloor. Floating floors can flex under an 800-pound table.
- **Carpet**: Playable, but expect to re-level the table after a few weeks as the carpet compresses under the legs.
