# 5 Things Your Hot Tub Mover Wishes You Knew

After hundreds of [**Hot Tub Moving**](/hot-tub-moving) jobs across the Miami area, our crews have a pretty clear picture of what makes a move go smoothly and what creates problems. Most homeowners have never moved a hot tub before, so there's no reason they'd know these things. But if you're planning a spa relocation, this insider perspective will save you time, money, and frustration.

## 1. We Need Accurate Access Information, Not Estimates

When we ask "how wide is your gate?" and you say "I think it's about four feet," that estimate can be off by a foot in either direction. A 36-inch gate versus a 48-inch gate is the difference between the tub fitting through and needing to remove a fence panel. The same applies to side yard width, overhead clearance under pergolas, and the distance from the tub to the truck.

![A Person Enjoys A Serene Soak In A Styli 4](/images/blog/2031/05/5-things-your-hot-tub-mover-wishes-you-knew/a-person-enjoys-a-serene-soak-in-a-styli-4.webp)


Take actual measurements with a tape measure. Better yet, take photos from multiple angles and send them during the quoting process. Five minutes of measuring saves an hour of problem-solving on moving day. Miami properties have all kinds of unexpected tight spots, from narrow walkways between the house and the pool cage to mature bougainvillea that's grown over the fence line.

## 2. A "Drained" Tub Is Not Always a Drained Tub

We show up to moves where the homeowner says the tub is empty, and there are still 15 gallons of water sloshing around in the jets, pump housing, and footwell. That's 125 extra pounds of shifting weight that makes the tub unpredictable during lifting.

Draining means more than opening the drain valve. You need to vacuum out the footwell with a wet/dry vac and blow compressed air through every jet line and air control. The tub should be completely dry inside. In South Florida's humidity, this means draining at least 24 hours ahead and leaving the cover off to let it dry.

## 3. Clear the Path Before We Arrive

Our crew shouldn't have to move your patio furniture, kids' toys, pool floats, and potted plants before we can start on the hot tub. Every obstacle we move adds time to the job, and time costs money for everyone.

![A Woman Enjoys A Relaxing Time In An Out 5](/images/blog/2031/05/5-things-your-hot-tub-mover-wishes-you-knew/a-woman-enjoys-a-relaxing-time-in-an-out-5.webp)


Walk the entire route from the tub to where the truck will park and clear everything out of the way. That includes:
- Patio furniture and grills
- Potted plants and planter boxes
- Garden hoses and pool equipment
- Bikes, toys, and storage bins
- Low-hanging branches that could snag the tub or the crew

If there's a tricky spot in the path (a tight corner, a step down, a gate that only opens one direction), mention it during the estimate.

## 4. The Electrical Needs to Be Disconnected Before We Get There

We're movers, not electricians. Your hot tub's 240V electrical circuit needs to be disconnected by a licensed electrician before our crew arrives. Live wiring near water and heavy lifting is a safety hazard we won't work around, and no reputable mover should.

Schedule the electrician for the day before the move. The disconnect takes about 30 minutes for a standard 240V spa circuit. If you wait until moving day, we're standing around on the clock while the electrician works, and that costs you money.

At the new home, have the electrician lined up to reconnect the same day if possible. That way, you can fill and start heating the tub right after placement.

## 5. Not Every Tub Is Worth Moving

This one is hard to hear, but it's honest. If your hot tub is 10+ years old, has a cracked shell, failing pumps, or outdated controls, the cost of moving it ($300 to $600 for a local move, more for long distance) may not be worth it. Add the electrician fees ($300 to $800 for disconnect and reconnect), potential pad work at the new home, and any repairs needed after the move, and you could be spending $1,000 or more on a tub that's approaching the end of its life.

![Two Women Enjoy A Relaxed Moment In An O 3](/images/blog/2031/05/5-things-your-hot-tub-mover-wishes-you-knew/two-women-enjoy-a-relaxed-moment-in-an-o-3.webp)


We'd rather give you an honest assessment than move a tub that's going to cost you more in repairs than it's worth. Sometimes selling the old tub and buying new at the destination makes more financial sense.

## The Bottom Line

The best hot tub moves are the ones where the homeowner and the moving crew are equally prepared. Send us good photos, take real measurements, drain the tub properly, clear the path, and get the electrician handled before we arrive. Do those five things and the move will go faster, smoother, and cheaper for everyone.
