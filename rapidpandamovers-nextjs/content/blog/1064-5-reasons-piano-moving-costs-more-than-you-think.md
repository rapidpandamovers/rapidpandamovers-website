# 5 Reasons Piano Moving Costs More Than You Think

When people hear the price of a professional piano move, the first reaction is often sticker shock. A few hundred dollars to move one item? But a piano isn't a piece of furniture, and the price reflects real costs that most people don't see. Here are five reasons the number is higher than you expected, and why each one is worth paying for.

## 1. The Equipment Is Specialized and Expensive

A [**Piano Moving**](/piano-moving) company doesn't use the same tools as a regular moving crew. Piano boards alone cost several hundred dollars each and wear out with use. Heavy-duty piano dollies rated for 800+ pounds are not the $40 furniture dollies from the hardware store. Stair-climbing rigs, reinforced ramps, padded ratchet straps, industrial stretch wrap, professional-grade quilted blankets, floor runners, and leg removal tools all add up.

![A Close Up View Of Vintage Piano Keys Sh 2](/images/blog/2031/03/5-reasons-piano-moving-costs-more-than-you-think/a-close-up-view-of-vintage-piano-keys-sh-2.webp)


A fully equipped piano moving truck carries thousands of dollars in specialized gear. That investment gets built into every job. The alternative is a crew showing up with a regular furniture dolly and some packing blankets, and we've all heard how those stories end.

## 2. The Crew Training Takes Time and Money

Piano moving is a skilled trade. A new crew member doesn't show up on day one and start moving grand pianos. They train for weeks alongside experienced movers, learning how to remove legs safely, how to support the piano body during tipping, how to control an 800-pound load on a staircase, and how to communicate during the coordinated movements that piano handling requires.

That training period is an investment the company makes before the employee generates revenue. Experienced piano movers also command higher wages than general laborers because their skills are harder to develop and the physical demands are extreme. A three-person piano crew costs more per hour than a general moving crew, and the job requires it.

## 3. The Insurance Premiums Are Higher

Insuring a moving company that handles items worth $5,000 to $100,000 each costs significantly more than insuring one that moves couches and dressers. Cargo insurance premiums are based on the value of the goods being transported, and pianos push those numbers up.

General liability insurance is also higher because the risk of property damage during piano moves is real. A piano dolly can crack a marble floor. A piano board can scuff a wall. The insurance covers these incidents, but the premiums reflect the elevated risk.

Workers' compensation rates for piano movers are higher as well. The physical demands of lifting, carrying, and managing hundreds of pounds on stairs lead to a higher injury rate than standard moving work. The comp premiums reflect that.

All three of these insurance costs are baked into the price you pay, and they're what protect you if something goes wrong.

## 4. The Job Takes Longer Than You'd Think

A baby grand piano move looks simple from the outside: take it apart, put it on the truck, drive it over, put it back together. In practice, a standard local baby grand move takes about two hours for a three-person crew.

![Cardboard Box Filled With Clothes Hanger 1](/images/blog/2031/03/5-reasons-piano-moving-costs-more-than-you-think/cardboard-box-filled-with-clothes-hanger-1.webp)


That two hours includes: wrapping the piano in multi-layer protection, removing three legs and the pedal lyre (each piece individually wrapped), placing the piano on a piano board, rolling it to the truck, securing it for transport, driving to the destination, reversing the entire process, placing the piano, and setting up floor protection at both locations.

Factor in travel time between the crew's base and the pickup location, the drive to the destination, and the return trip, and the total labor commitment for a single local piano move can be four to five hours per crew member. Multiply that by three crew members and a competitive hourly rate, and the labor cost alone is significant.

Stairs, tight doorways, elevator coordination for high-rises, and long carry distances all add time. In Miami, building access procedures at Brickell and Downtown condos can add 30 to 60 minutes of logistics before the piano even moves.

## 5. The Risk the Company Absorbs Is Real

Every piano move carries the possibility of a catastrophic outcome. A dropped piano is a total loss. A cracked soundboard can cost more to repair than the piano is worth. A crew member's herniated disc is a workers' comp claim and a human cost.

The price of a professional piano move includes a margin for the company to absorb these risks: to pay claims, to cover downtime for injured workers, to replace damaged equipment, and to stay in business through the inevitable bad days. A company pricing too low either isn't carrying proper insurance, isn't paying its crew fairly, or isn't investing in the equipment needed to do the job safely. All three of those shortcuts eventually cost someone.

## The Real Question Isn't "Why Does It Cost So Much?"

The real question is "what happens if it costs too little?" Piano repairs start at several hundred dollars for minor issues and climb into thousands fast. A scratched high-gloss finish costs $2,000+ to refinish. A cracked soundboard can be $5,000 to $10,000 to replace. An injury lawsuit from an uninsured helper can be devastating.

The professional price pays for prevention. Prevention is always cheaper than repair.
