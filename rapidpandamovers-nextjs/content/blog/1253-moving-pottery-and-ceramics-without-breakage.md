# Moving Pottery and Ceramics Without Breakage

Pottery and ceramics look solid sitting on a shelf, but they're among the most breakage-prone items in any move. Fired clay is hard but brittle. It doesn't flex or absorb impact the way wood or metal does. A fall of just a few inches onto a hard surface can crack a piece that survived decades of daily handling. We've packed and moved everything from hand-thrown studio pottery in Coconut Grove to museum-quality porcelain collections in Coral Gables, and the approach is always the same: individual attention to every piece.

## Why Ceramics Break During Moves

Most ceramic breakage happens for predictable reasons. Pieces shift inside boxes that are too large or too loosely packed. Handles, spouts, and lids catch on wrapping material during packing and snap off. Heavy pieces placed on top of lighter ones crush what's underneath. Boxes labeled "fragile" get stacked under heavier boxes by a rushed crew.

Understanding these failure points lets you prevent them. The packing technique for ceramics is all about immobilizing each piece and protecting its weakest points.

## Gather the Right Materials

For ceramics, you need:

- **Acid-free tissue paper**: For the first wrapping layer against glazed surfaces
- **Bubble wrap**: For the outer cushioning layer, small-bubble wrap for delicate pieces
- **Packing paper** (unprinted newsprint): For filling gaps and cushioning inside boxes
- **Double-walled boxes**: Single-wall cardboard isn't strong enough for heavy ceramics
- **Painter's tape**: Won't leave residue on glazed surfaces
- **Foam pouches or sheets**: For extra cushioning on high-value pieces

## Packing Flat Pieces (Plates, Platters, Bowls)

Wrap each plate individually in acid-free tissue, then in a layer of bubble wrap. Stack wrapped plates vertically, on their edges, not flat. Flat stacking puts the weight of the entire stack on the bottom piece, which is the one most likely to crack. Standing them on edge distributes the load along the strongest axis of the plate.

Use a dish-pack box or cell-divider box if you have one. These boxes have internal cardboard dividers that keep each piece in its own slot. Fill remaining space with crumpled packing paper until nothing moves when you gently shake the box.

For platters and large bowls, wrap them individually and pack them in their own box or separated from other items by thick padding.

## Packing Vases, Pitchers, and Handled Pieces

Handles and spouts are the most vulnerable parts of any ceramic piece. Wrap the handle or spout separately with tissue and bubble wrap, building up a cushion around the protrusion so it's protected from impact in every direction.

Stuff the interior of hollow pieces like vases and pitchers with crumpled packing paper. This prevents the walls from collapsing inward under pressure and adds structural support during transport.

Wrap the entire piece in tissue, then bubble wrap, and place it in a box with at least three inches of cushioning on all sides. The piece should not be able to touch any wall of the box even when pressed.

## Packing Sculptures and Figurines

Ceramic sculptures with thin elements, fingers, tails, raised arms, need those elements wrapped individually before the whole piece gets packaged. If a component is detachable, remove it and pack it separately. Label both pieces so they stay together.

![Grayscale Image Of A Classic Sculpture I 1](/images/blog/2034/02/moving-pottery-and-ceramics-without-breakage/grayscale-image-of-a-classic-sculpture-i-1.webp)


Build a nest of crumpled packing paper in the bottom of the box, press the wrapped sculpture into the nest, and fill around it until the piece is completely immobilized. Close the box and shake it gently. If anything moves, add more paper.

## Lids and Loose Components

Never pack a lid attached to its base. The rattling during transport will chip both pieces where they meet. Remove lids, wrap them separately, and pack them next to their bases in the same box with padding between them. Label the box so you know which lid goes with which piece.

## Loading the Truck

Ceramic boxes go on top of the load, never on the bottom. Mark every box "FRAGILE" and "THIS SIDE UP" on multiple sides. Place ceramic boxes in an area of the truck where they won't have heavier items stacked on top and where they're shielded from shifting during braking and turns.
